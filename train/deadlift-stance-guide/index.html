Conventional vs Sumo Deadlift

Visual comparison to help you find your strongest pulling stance.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

Both conventional and sumo deadlifts are legal in powerlifting and both build serious strength. The best stance for you depends on your body proportions, mobility, and what feels strongest. Here's how they compare.

The Two Stances: Top View

Top-down view: Conventional has narrow feet with hands outside. Sumo has wide feet with hands inside.

Side-by-Side: Starting Position

Side view comparison: Conventional has more forward torso lean and longer range of motion. Sumo is more upright with shorter ROM.

Key Differences

Conventional

  • Feet hip-width apart
  • Hands outside knees
  • More forward torso lean
  • Longer range of motion
  • More back/hip dominant
  • Higher hip start position

Sumo

  • Feet wide (near plates)
  • Hands inside knees
  • More upright torso
  • Shorter range of motion
  • More quad/hip dominant
  • Lower hip start position

Muscle Emphasis

Conventional emphasizes back and hamstrings more. Sumo emphasizes glutes, adductors, and quads more.

Which Stance Is Right for You?

Conventional May Be Better If:

  • You have longer arms relative to torso
  • You have limited hip mobility/flexibility
  • You're stronger in hip extension (posterior chain dominant)
  • You have shorter legs relative to torso
  • Sumo feels awkward or weak off the floor

Sumo May Be Better If:

  • You have shorter arms relative to torso
  • You have good hip mobility and can get wide
  • You're quad/glute dominant
  • You have longer legs relative to torso
  • You struggle with back position in conventional
  • Conventional feels like a grind off the floor
Try Both

The only way to know for sure is to train both stances for several weeks and see which feels stronger and more natural. Many lifters are surprised—the stance that "looks right" for their build isn't always strongest.

Common Mistakes by Stance

Conventional Mistakes

  • Hips rising too fast: Turns into a stiff-leg deadlift
  • Bar drifting forward: Keep it close to your legs
  • Rounding lower back: Brace harder, or the weight is too heavy

Sumo Mistakes

  • Hips too far from bar: Get your hips close to the bar at setup
  • Knees caving in: Push knees out over toes
  • Not patient off the floor: Sumo is slower off the floor—don't jerk

Can You Train Both?

Yes. Many lifters use one stance for competition and the other as an accessory. For example:

  • Main lift: Competition stance (whichever is stronger)
  • Variation: Other stance as a secondary lift for balanced development

This builds well-rounded strength and can help address weaknesses in your main stance.

The Bottom Line

Neither stance is "cheating" or universally better. Conventional and sumo are both legal, both build strength, and both have produced world records. Choose the one that's strongest for your body, and train it with intent.
