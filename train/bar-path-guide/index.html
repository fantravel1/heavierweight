Bar Path Analysis

Optimal bar paths for the squat, bench press, and deadlift.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

The bar path—the trajectory the barbell takes during a lift—directly affects efficiency, safety, and how much weight you can move. Understanding optimal bar paths helps you identify and fix technique issues.

Why Bar Path Matters

Physics dictates that the most efficient path for moving a weight is one that keeps it over your base of support (your feet). Deviations from this create moment arms—horizontal distances between the bar and your balance point—that your muscles must work extra hard to overcome.

A good bar path minimizes wasted energy and keeps you in mechanically advantageous positions throughout the lift.

Squat Bar Path

The squat bar path should be vertical, staying over midfoot throughout. Forward drift creates unwanted moment arm.

Key Points

  • Bar should travel in a straight vertical line over midfoot
  • High bar: slightly more forward bar position
  • Low bar: bar sits lower, torso leans more forward
  • Either way, bar stays over midfoot when viewed from the side

Common Deviations

  • Forward drift: Often from weak quads, poor ankle mobility, or looking down
  • Rising hips first: Causes bar to shift forward as chest drops
  • Backward drift: Rare, usually from falling back in the hole

Bench Press Bar Path

The bench press follows a J-curve: lockout over shoulders, touch lower on chest, press up and back.

Key Points

  • Lockout position is over the shoulders (not over face or chest)
  • Bar travels diagonally down to touch lower chest/sternum
  • Press up AND back toward the lockout position
  • This J-curve keeps the bar over your base of support

Why Not Straight?

A straight up-and-down path would either touch too high (over shoulders = shoulder stress) or lock out too far forward (over chest = unstable). The J-curve solves both problems.

Deadlift Bar Path

The deadlift bar path should be vertical, staying as close to the body as possible.

Key Points

  • Bar starts over midfoot
  • Travels straight up in a vertical line
  • Stays in contact with (or very close to) the legs throughout
  • Minimal horizontal movement

Common Deviations

  • Bar drifting forward: Usually from hips rising too fast or not engaging lats
  • Bar swinging around knees: Knees not clearing out of the way
  • Hitching: Bar stopping and restarting (illegal in competition)
Keep It Close

The bar should scrape your shins and thighs. Wear long socks or pants. If the bar isn't leaving marks, it's probably too far away from your body, creating a longer moment arm and making the lift harder.

How to Analyze Your Own Bar Path

  1. Video from the side: Camera at hip height, perpendicular to your lift
  2. Use an app: Apps like WL Analysis can track and draw bar path
  3. Draw a vertical line: Compare your bar path to a straight reference
  4. Look for deviations: Where does the bar move away from ideal?
  5. Identify the cause: Technique issue, mobility limitation, or strength weakness?
The Bottom Line

Efficient bar paths minimize wasted energy. The squat and deadlift should be vertical over midfoot. The bench follows a J-curve from shoulders to lower chest and back. Video yourself, analyze the path, and address the causes of any deviations.
