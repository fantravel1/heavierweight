Finding a Strength Coach

How to find the right coach for your powerlifting or weightlifting goals.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

A good coach accelerates your progress, helps you avoid injuries, and provides accountability and expertise you can't get on your own. A bad coach wastes your money and time—or worse. Here's how to find a good one.

Do You Need a Coach?

Not everyone needs a coach, but most people benefit from one. Consider coaching if:

  • You're new to lifting and want to learn properly from the start
  • You've plateaued and can't figure out why
  • You want to compete and need a structured approach
  • You don't trust yourself to program effectively
  • You need accountability to stay consistent
  • You have specific weaknesses or technique issues
Self-Coaching Works Too

Many successful lifters have never had a coach. If you're educated, self-aware, and disciplined, self-coaching is viable. But most people progress faster with expert guidance, especially early in their lifting career.

Types of Coaching

In-Person Coaching

  • Work directly with a coach at a gym
  • Real-time feedback on technique
  • Hands-on cueing and spotting
  • Usually most expensive option
  • Limited by geography

Online/Remote Coaching

  • Coach writes programming, reviews videos
  • Communication via email, app, or video calls
  • More flexible and usually cheaper
  • Works if you can self-execute in the gym
  • Access to coaches anywhere in the world

Hybrid

  • Some in-person sessions, some remote
  • Check-ins every few weeks with video review between
  • Good balance of hands-on and cost-effective

What to Look For

Green Flags

  • Experience coaching your sport (powerlifting, weightlifting)
  • Track record of athlete results
  • Clear communication style
  • Asks about your goals, history, and constraints
  • Individualizes programming
  • Willing to explain their reasoning
  • Continued education and learning
  • Has athletes similar to you (age, experience level, goals)
  • Reasonable and transparent pricing

Red Flags

  • Promises unrealistic results
  • Cookie-cutter programming for everyone
  • Can't explain why you're doing something
  • Dismissive of your questions or concerns
  • No athlete testimonials or results
  • Pushes supplements they sell
  • Makes you feel bad for questioning them
  • Pressure to commit immediately or long-term
  • Poor communication or slow responses

Questions to Ask

About Their Experience

  • How long have you been coaching?
  • What's your background in this sport?
  • What certifications or education do you have?
  • Can you share examples of athlete progress?
  • Do you currently coach athletes at my level?

About Their Approach

  • How do you individualize programming?
  • What does a typical training week look like?
  • How do you handle injuries or setbacks?
  • What's your communication style and frequency?
  • How do you provide feedback on technique?

About Logistics

  • What's the cost and payment structure?
  • Is there a minimum commitment?
  • What's included in coaching?
  • How do I submit videos and communicate?
  • What happens if I need to cancel?

Where to Find Coaches

  • Your gym: Ask strong lifters who they work with
  • Competition: Watch who's coaching athletes you admire
  • Social media: Many coaches have Instagram presence
  • Federation websites: Some list certified coaches
  • Referrals: Ask in lifting communities for recommendations

Evaluating Coaching Quality

The Trial Period

Many coaches offer a trial month or initial consultation. Use this to assess:

  • Do they respond promptly?
  • Is the programming appropriate for you?
  • Do you understand why you're doing what you're doing?
  • Is feedback helpful and actionable?
  • Do you feel comfortable asking questions?

Ongoing Assessment

Even after hiring a coach, continue to evaluate:

  • Are you progressing toward your goals?
  • Do you feel supported?
  • Is the relationship working?
  • Are they adjusting based on your feedback?
It's Okay to Move On

Not every coach-athlete relationship works. If after a reasonable trial period (3+ months) you're not progressing or the relationship isn't working, it's okay to find someone else. This isn't failure—it's finding the right fit.

What Good Coaching Looks Like

  • Programming that makes sense for your goals and constraints
  • Clear explanations of what you're doing and why
  • Responsive communication within reasonable timeframes
  • Adjustments based on your feedback and progress
  • Technique feedback that's specific and actionable
  • Support during setbacks and challenges
  • Celebration of your successes

Cost Expectations

Coaching prices vary widely:

  • Online coaching: $100-300+/month typically
  • In-person coaching: $50-150+/session
  • Group coaching: Often cheaper than individual

Cheaper isn't always worse; expensive isn't always better. Value matters more than price. A $150/month coach who delivers results is better than a $300/month coach who doesn't.

Making the Most of Coaching

Your results depend on you as much as your coach:

  • Do the programming as written
  • Submit videos and feedback regularly
  • Communicate honestly about how things feel
  • Ask questions when you don't understand
  • Be patient—progress takes time
  • Trust the process (while staying engaged)
The Bottom Line

A good coach is a long-term investment in your lifting. Take time to find the right fit. Ask questions, evaluate their track record, and make sure the relationship works for you. When you find the right coach, it can transform your progress.
