How to Use a Lifting Belt

Position, tightness, and bracing technique for squat, bench, and deadlift.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

A belt doesn't just hold your guts in‚Äîit provides something to brace against, increasing intra-abdominal pressure and spinal stability. But only if you use it correctly. Here's how to position and brace with a belt for maximum effect.

How Belts Actually Work

Contrary to what some believe, a belt doesn't support your spine directly. Instead, it:

  • Provides feedback: Something to push your abs against
  • Increases IAP: More intra-abdominal pressure = more stability
  • Teaches bracing: You learn what a proper brace feels like
  • Allows more load: Research shows 5-15% more weight can be lifted
Top-down view: The belt resists abdominal expansion, increasing internal pressure

How Tight?

The right tightness allows you to take a full breath and brace hard, but doesn't leave gaps between you and the belt.

The right tightness allows full breathing but provides resistance when bracing
The Finger Test

When exhaled and relaxed, you should be able to fit 1-2 fingers between the belt and your body. When you take a full breath and brace, there should be no gap‚Äîyou're pushing out against the belt.

How to Brace with a Belt

  1. Get set in your starting position (standing for squat, hinged for deadlift)
  2. Take a deep breath into your belly, not your chest
  3. Brace your core by pushing out in 360¬∞‚Äîfront, sides, and back
  4. Feel the belt resist your expansion on all sides
  5. Hold this brace throughout the entire rep
  6. Reset at the top with a new breath if needed

Common Belt Mistakes

1. Wearing It Too Tight

If the belt is so tight you can't breathe, you can't brace properly. The belt should provide resistance, not compression. Loosen up until you can take a full belly breath.

2. Not Bracing Into It

The belt doesn't work passively. You must actively push your abs out against it. If you're not bracing, you're just wearing a decorative strip of leather.

3. Wrong Position for the Lift

If the belt digs into your hips at the bottom of a deadlift, it's too low. If it rides up during squats, it's too high. Adjust position for each lift.

4. Using It for Everything

A belt is for heavy compound lifts where spinal stability is critical. You don't need it for curls, lat pulldowns, or leg extensions. Learn to brace without a belt first.

When to Start Using a Belt

  • Not immediately: Learn to brace without a belt first
  • After ~6 months: Once you have solid bracing fundamentals
  • For heavy work: Introduce it on your heaviest sets (85%+ 1RM)
  • Gradually: Start using it occasionally, not for every set
Belt vs No Belt in Training

Many lifters do warm-up sets and lighter work without a belt, then put it on for top sets. This builds core strength while still getting the benefit of a belt when it matters most. In competition, always wear what you're used to.
