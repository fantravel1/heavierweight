Snatch Technique Guide

The most technical lift in sport—floor to overhead in one explosive motion.

14 min read Updated Dec 2024

The snatch is often called the most athletic movement in sport. In one fluid motion, you lift the barbell from the floor to overhead. It requires strength, speed, mobility, and precision—all in under two seconds.

The Snatch: Full Movement

The snatch: Wide grip setup → First pull → Explosive extension → Turnover → Catch in overhead squat → Stand to complete

Snatch vs. Clean

The snatch and clean share similar pulling mechanics, but key differences:

  • Grip: Snatch uses a much wider grip (arms form a wide "V" overhead)
  • Catch position: Snatch catches overhead; clean catches on shoulders
  • Weights: Snatch uses less weight (typically 75-85% of clean)
  • Mobility demands: Snatch requires more shoulder and hip flexibility

The Snatch Grip

The snatch grip is wide—when standing with arms straight down, the bar should rest in your hip crease.
Finding Your Grip Width

Stand tall and hold a PVC pipe or empty bar with straight arms. Adjust grip until the bar sits at your hip crease. Mark this position. In the overhead position, your arms should form a wide "V" with the bar directly over your heels.

Phase Breakdown

1. Setup

Feet hip-width, bar over midfoot. Wide snatch grip with hook grip. Shoulders over or slightly in front of bar. Hips higher than in a clean. Back flat, lats engaged pulling the slack out of the bar.

2. First Pull

Push the floor away. Maintain back angle—chest and hips rise together. Bar stays close to shins. This phase is controlled, not explosive yet. Bar passes the knees.

3. Second Pull / Extension

The power phase. Violently extend hips, knees, and ankles (triple extension). Bar contacts upper thigh/hip crease. This contact point is lower than in the clean. Maximum vertical acceleration happens here.

4. Third Pull / Turnover

After extension, actively pull yourself under the bar. Elbows rotate up and out. The bar continues upward while you drop into an overhead squat. This is the fastest part of the lift.

5. Catch

Receive the bar in a deep overhead squat. Arms locked, bar over heels, torso upright. The catch must be stable before you stand.

6. Recovery

Stand up from the overhead squat while keeping the bar locked overhead. Demonstrate control at the top. Lift complete.

Mobility Requirements

The snatch demands significant mobility:

  • Overhead position: Arms must lock straight with bar over heels
  • Shoulder flexibility: Full overhead range with external rotation
  • Thoracic extension: Upper back must extend to support overhead position
  • Hip mobility: Deep squat with upright torso
  • Ankle mobility: Knees must track forward in the catch
Overhead Squat Test

If you can't hold a stable overhead squat with a PVC pipe, you're not ready to snatch. Work on overhead squat mobility first—the snatch demands even more flexibility under load and speed.

Common Snatch Errors

  • Early arm bend: Arms must stay straight until extension. Bending early kills power.
  • Not finishing extension: Cutting the pull short means less height on the bar.
  • Swinging the bar forward: Bar should travel straight up, close to body. Forward swing = missed lift.
  • Slow turnover: You must aggressively pull under. Speed is everything.
  • Pressing out: Arms must lock instantly. Pressing overhead is a no-lift.
  • Forward catch: Bar drifts forward = chase or miss. Keep it over heels.

Learning Progressions

  1. Overhead squat: Master the catch position first
  2. Snatch grip behind-neck press: Build overhead stability
  3. Snatch pull: Practice the pull without the catch
  4. Power snatch (high catch): Learn timing without deep squat
  5. Hang snatch: Above knee, then below knee
  6. Full snatch: Put it all together
Get a Coach

The snatch is the most technical lift in the gym. A qualified weightlifting coach can accelerate your learning dramatically and prevent you from ingraining bad habits that take months to fix.
