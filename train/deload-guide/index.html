When and How to Deload

Strategic recovery for continued progress.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

You can't train hard forever without a break. Deloads are planned reductions in training stress that allow your body to recover, adapt, and come back stronger. They're not weakness—they're strategy.

What Is a Deload?

A deload is a temporary reduction in training volume, intensity, or both. It's typically 1 week, but can range from 3 days to 2 weeks depending on the situation.

The purpose isn't to stop training—it's to reduce stress enough that accumulated fatigue dissipates while you maintain fitness. Think of it as letting the adaptation you've earned actually happen.

The Fitness-Fatigue Model

Training creates both fitness (good) and fatigue (necessary but limiting). Fatigue masks fitness. When you deload, fatigue drops faster than fitness, allowing you to express the strength you've built.

When to Deload

Scheduled Deloads

Many programs include deloads at regular intervals:

  • Every 4th week (3 weeks hard, 1 week deload)
  • Every 5th or 6th week for more advanced lifters
  • Before competition (peaking)
  • After competition (recovery)

Reactive Deloads

Sometimes you need a deload outside your schedule. Signs it's time:

  • Persistent fatigue: Tiredness that doesn't resolve with a good night's sleep
  • Stalled progress: Weights that should be moving aren't
  • Nagging aches: Joint discomfort that lingers
  • RPE creep: Familiar weights feel harder than they should
  • Decreased motivation: Dreading sessions you normally enjoy
  • Poor sleep: Difficulty falling or staying asleep
  • Irritability: Short temper, mood changes

Don't Ignore the Signs

Pushing through accumulated fatigue leads to worse outcomes: injury, illness, or forced time off that's longer than a planned deload would have been. A week of reduced training is better than a month of recovery from overdoing it.

Types of Deloads

Volume Deload

Keep intensity (weight) similar, reduce volume (sets/reps) by 40-60%.

  • Normal week: Squat 4×5 at 80%
  • Deload week: Squat 2×5 at 80%
  • Best for: Maintaining strength feel while reducing stress

Intensity Deload

Keep volume similar, reduce weight by 10-20%.

  • Normal week: Squat 4×5 at 80%
  • Deload week: Squat 4×5 at 65%
  • Best for: Joint recovery while maintaining movement patterns

Full Deload

Reduce both volume and intensity significantly.

  • Normal week: Squat 4×5 at 80%
  • Deload week: Squat 2×5 at 60%
  • Best for: When you're really beat up, or before competition

Active Recovery Week

Different activities entirely—no barbell work.

  • Light cardio, walking, swimming
  • Mobility work, stretching
  • Maybe some light machine work
  • Best for: Mental break, post-competition, after very hard blocks

How to Structure a Deload Week

Option 1: Reduce Sets (Most Common)

  • Cut working sets in half
  • Keep weight the same or slightly lighter
  • Drop accessories to just 1-2 exercises
  • Sessions take 30-45 minutes instead of 60-90

Option 2: Reduce Sessions

  • Train 2 days instead of 4
  • Keep sessions relatively normal but less frequent
  • More recovery days between sessions

Option 3: Light Week

  • Use 50-70% of normal working weights
  • Focus on technique and positions
  • Nothing should feel hard
  • Just maintaining movement patterns

What to Do During a Deload

In the Gym

  • Do the movements, just less and/or lighter
  • Focus on technique refinement
  • Use the extra time for mobility work
  • Don't add random exercises to "fill time"

Outside the Gym

  • Sleep: Prioritize it even more than usual
  • Nutrition: Keep eating well—recovery requires fuel
  • Stress management: Good time to catch up on life
  • Light activity: Walking, swimming, easy movement
Don't Cut Calories During Deload

A common mistake is eating less because you're training less. But deloads are for recovery—and recovery requires nutrition. Keep protein high and eat normally. You're not going to gain fat from one easier week.

Deloading for Competition

Pre-competition deloads (often called "peaking" or "tapering") are specifically designed to shed fatigue while maintaining strength:

  • 1-2 weeks out: Significantly reduce volume
  • Practice openers: Take your planned opening attempts
  • Keep intensity: Touch heavy weights, just fewer sets
  • Last heavy session: Usually 7-10 days before the meet
  • Final week: Very light, just staying loose

Common Deload Mistakes

  • Skipping them entirely: "I don't need rest" is ego talking
  • Going too hard: A deload that still exhausts you isn't a deload
  • Adding volume elsewhere: Running extra miles or trying new exercises defeats the purpose
  • Feeling guilty: Rest is part of training, not cheating
  • Waiting too long: Deload before you're forced to, not after
  • Deloading too often: Every week can't be a deload week

After the Deload

Post-deload, you should feel:

  • Eager to train again
  • Stronger and fresher
  • Less achy and stiff
  • Better sleep and energy

If you don't feel recovered after a deload, you might need a longer break, or there might be something else going on (nutrition, sleep, life stress).

The Bottom Line

Deloads are strategic, not lazy. Plan them regularly, take them when you need them, and trust the process. You'll come back stronger, more motivated, and less injured over the long term.
