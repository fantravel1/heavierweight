The Bench Press Arch

Understanding, building, and safely using arch technique for powerlifting.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

The bench arch is one of the most misunderstood techniques in powerlifting. Critics call it cheating. Lifters call it essential. Here's what you need to know about why it works, how to do it safely, and what the rules actually say.

What Is the Bench Arch?

The bench arch refers to the thoracic (upper back) extension created when a lifter retracts their shoulder blades and creates a curve in their spine while maintaining contact with the bench. This is not a dangerous lower back hyperextension—it's a controlled upper back position.

The arch maintains three points of contact: head, shoulder blades, and glutes

Why Use an Arch?

1. Reduced Range of Motion

By raising the chest, the bar has less distance to travel. In competition, this means you can lift more weight. For the same strength, shorter ROM = higher lift.

2. Better Shoulder Position

Scapular retraction (pulling shoulder blades together) puts your shoulders in a more stable, protected position. This actually reduces injury risk compared to bench pressing with a flat back and protracted shoulders.

3. Improved Leg Drive

A proper arch creates tension through the entire body. When you drive through your legs, that force transfers up through your torso into the bar.

Is It Cheating?

No. The arch is a legal technique within all major powerlifting federations. The rules require head, shoulders (both), and glutes to maintain contact with the bench, and feet to be on the floor. Any arch that meets these requirements is legal.

Common Arch Mistakes

Correct: Thoracic Extension

The arch comes from your upper back (thoracic spine). Shoulder blades are pinched together and down. Lower back has natural curve only.

Incorrect: Lumbar Hyperextension

Cranking from your lower back is painful and unstable. If your lower back hurts after benching, you're arching from the wrong place.

Correct: Glutes Stay Down

Glutes maintain contact with the bench throughout the lift. This is a competition requirement and ensures proper force transfer.

Incorrect: Butt Comes Up

If your glutes lift off the bench, the lift is a red light in competition. It also means your leg drive is pushing you up, not into the bench.

Building Arch Mobility

If you can't get into a good arch, you likely need to work on thoracic mobility. Here are the key areas:

Thoracic Extension

  • Foam roll upper back (not lower back)
  • Cat-cow stretches focusing on upper back
  • Thoracic extensions over a foam roller
  • Prayer stretch with reach

Hip Flexor Flexibility

  • Couch stretch
  • Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch
  • Pigeon pose

Shoulder Mobility

  • Shoulder dislocations with band/PVC
  • Wall slides
  • Door frame stretches
Forced Arch = Pain

Never force an arch beyond your current mobility. Build it progressively over weeks and months. If it hurts, you're either arching from the wrong place or pushing too hard. Work on mobility and let the arch come naturally.

Competition Rules

Different federations have slightly different rules. Here are the key requirements:

IPF / USAPL Rules

  • Head, shoulders (both), and buttocks must maintain contact with the bench
  • Feet must be flat on the floor (heels down)
  • No excessive movement of feet during the lift
  • Bar must touch the chest and pause motionless

USPA / Other Federations

  • Same contact requirements (head, shoulders, glutes)
  • Some allow heels up (on toes)
  • Check specific federation rules before competing
The Legality Test

If a judge can slide their hand under your lower back (between you and the bench), that's fine—the arch is in your thoracic spine. But your shoulder blades and glutes must be solidly on the bench. Practice with a training partner checking your contact points.

Should Everyone Arch?

Some arch is beneficial for almost everyone who bench presses because it creates a safer shoulder position. However, an extreme competition arch isn't necessary for:

  • General fitness: A moderate arch provides the safety benefits without maximizing ROM reduction
  • Bodybuilding: More ROM can mean more muscle stimulus
  • Those with lower back issues: Focus on thoracic mobility, use a moderate arch

For powerlifting competition, learning to arch effectively is simply part of the sport. It's a skill like any other technique element.

Source: Juggernaut Training Systems - Developing Your Arch

Summary

  • The bench arch is a legal, safe technique when done correctly
  • Focus on thoracic (upper back) extension, not lumbar hyperextension
  • Maintain three points of contact: head, shoulders, glutes
  • Build mobility progressively—don't force it
  • Check federation-specific rules for foot position
  • Use leg drive to push yourself into the bench, not up off it
