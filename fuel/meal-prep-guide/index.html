Meal Prep for Strength Athletes

Eat well without living in the kitchen.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

You know you need to eat well to support your training. But cooking every meal from scratch is unsustainable. Meal prep solves this—a few hours once or twice a week sets you up for nutritional success.

Why Meal Prep Works

  • Removes decision fatigue: When food is ready, you eat it. No choosing, no ordering.
  • Ensures protein: Pre-portioned meals make hitting protein targets automatic.
  • Saves money: Cooking in bulk costs a fraction of eating out.
  • Saves time: Daily cooking adds up. Weekend prep frees weekday hours.
  • Reduces temptation: When good food is available, you eat good food.
Start Simple

You don't need elaborate meals. Prepped protein + prepped carbs + prepped vegetables = complete meals assembled in 2 minutes. Master the basics before trying anything fancy.

The Building Blocks

Think in categories rather than specific recipes. Prep each category in bulk, then mix and match throughout the week.

Protein Sources

Prep 2-3 protein sources each week:

  • Chicken breast: Baked, grilled, or air-fried in bulk
  • Ground beef/turkey: Browned with basic seasoning
  • Eggs: Hard-boiled in batches
  • Greek yogurt: Buy in bulk (no prep needed)
  • Fish: Baked salmon or cod stores well for 3-4 days

Carb Sources

Prep 2-3 carb sources:

  • Rice: White or brown, cooked in a rice cooker
  • Potatoes: Baked or roasted sweet/regular potatoes
  • Oats: Overnight oats prepped in jars
  • Pasta: Cook and store separately from sauce
  • Quinoa: Cooks similarly to rice

Vegetables

Prep several vegetable options:

  • Roasted: Broccoli, Brussels sprouts, asparagus, peppers
  • Raw: Washed and cut vegetables for snacking
  • Frozen: Steam-in-bag vegetables require zero prep
  • Salad greens: Washed and stored with paper towel

The Prep Process

Step 1: Plan (10 minutes)

Before shopping, decide what you'll prep. Count the meals you need. Factor in any meals out. Make a simple list: 2 proteins, 2 carbs, 3 vegetables.

Step 2: Shop (30-45 minutes)

Buy in bulk. Stick to your list. The perimeter of the store has most of what you need: meat, produce, dairy. Most of your cart should be single-ingredient foods.

Step 3: Prep (2-3 hours)

Cook everything at once. Use all oven racks. Start with the longest-cooking items. Work through washing, chopping, and cooking systematically.

Step 4: Store

Let food cool, then portion into containers. Label if needed. Stack in fridge. Most prepped food lasts 4-5 days refrigerated.

Sample Prep Day

Here's what a typical Sunday prep session might look like:

Oven (2 hours)

  • 2 lbs chicken breast (400°F, 25-30 min)
  • 2 lbs sweet potatoes (400°F, 45 min)
  • 2 sheet pans roasted broccoli (400°F, 20 min)

Stovetop (45 min)

  • 2 cups rice in rice cooker
  • 2 lbs ground beef, browned with onions
  • 12 hard-boiled eggs

No-Cook Prep (20 min)

  • 5 containers overnight oats
  • Washed and cut peppers, cucumbers, carrots
  • Portioned Greek yogurt into grab-and-go containers

Meal Assembly Ideas

With prepped components, meals come together fast:

Breakfast Options

  • Overnight oats + protein powder + banana
  • 2 hard-boiled eggs + Greek yogurt + berries
  • Scrambled eggs (cook fresh) + prepped potatoes

Lunch/Dinner Combos

  • Chicken + rice + roasted broccoli
  • Ground beef + potatoes + mixed vegetables
  • Salmon + quinoa + asparagus
  • Chicken + greens + sweet potato + dressing

Quick Additions

Keep these on hand for variety:

  • Different sauces and seasonings
  • Avocados (add fresh)
  • Cheese
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Fresh herbs

Storage Tips

  • Use quality containers: Glass lasts longer and heats better than plastic
  • Cool before storing: Hot food creates moisture = soggy food
  • Store sauces separately: Add when eating to keep food fresh
  • Label everything: Date containers so you know what's oldest
  • Freeze what you won't eat: Prep more, freeze half for next week
The 4-Day Rule

Most prepped food stays good for 4-5 days in the fridge. Plan accordingly: prep on Sunday for Mon-Thu, quick prep or fresh cooking for Fri-Sat. Or prep twice per week (Sunday and Wednesday).

Hitting Your Macros

Meal prep makes macro tracking much easier. When you prep, weigh and log the totals. Divide by number of portions. Now you know exactly what's in each container.

Protein Portions

  • Aim for 30-40g protein per meal
  • 4-6 oz cooked chicken = ~35-50g protein
  • 4 oz ground beef (93%) = ~24g protein
  • 1 cup Greek yogurt = ~17-20g protein

Carb Portions

  • 1 cup cooked rice = ~45g carbs
  • 1 medium sweet potato = ~26g carbs
  • 1 cup cooked oats = ~27g carbs

Making It Sustainable

Don't Over-Complicate

Eating the same meals doesn't have to be boring, but variety shouldn't compromise consistency. It's okay to eat similar foods during the week and save elaborate cooking for weekends.

Prep What You Actually Eat

Don't prep foods you hate just because they're "healthy." Find nutritious foods you enjoy and build your prep around those.

Build the Habit

Same day, same time each week. Sunday afternoon prep becomes as automatic as Sunday training. Schedule it like you schedule workouts.

Involve Others

If you live with someone, prep together. Double the hands, half the time. Even if they don't eat exactly what you eat, the process is shared.

When Prep Fails

It happens. Life gets busy. Have backup options:

  • Rotisserie chicken: Pre-cooked protein from any grocery store
  • Canned tuna/salmon: Shelf-stable protein
  • Pre-washed salad kits: Quick vegetables
  • Frozen meals: Have a few quality frozen options for emergencies
  • Eggs: 10 minutes from fridge to plate
The Bottom Line

Meal prep isn't about perfection—it's about making good nutrition the easy choice. 2-3 hours once a week sets up your entire week. Start simple: prep protein, prep carbs, have vegetables available. The rest is just assembly.
