Bar Path Analysis

Understand optimal bar movement patterns for each lift.

11 min read Updated Dec 2024

Bar path—the line the bar traces through space during a lift—reveals technique efficiency. A clean bar path means force is going where it should. A messy one means energy is wasted. Here's how to analyze and optimize yours.

Bench Press Bar Path

The bench press has the most complex bar path of the big three. Contrary to intuition, the optimal path is NOT a straight line.

The bar should travel back toward your face as you press, not straight up

Key Points for Bench Bar Path

  • Touch point: Lower chest, around nipple line or slightly below
  • Press direction: Back and up—toward your face, not straight up
  • Lockout position: Directly over shoulder joint
  • The curve: Creates a "J" or reverse "J" shape when viewed from the side

Squat Bar Path

Unlike bench, the squat bar path SHOULD be as vertical as possible. The bar should travel straight down and straight up over midfoot.

The bar should travel straight up and down over midfoot throughout the lift

Common Squat Bar Path Errors

  • Forward drift: Usually from weak quads or excessive forward lean—leads to "good morning" squat
  • Backward drift: Often from sitting too far back or losing upper back tightness
  • Side-to-side wobble: Indicates asymmetry or uneven loading—address imbalances

Deadlift Bar Path

The deadlift bar path is the simplest: straight up, staying as close to the body as possible.

Keep the bar as close to your body as possible throughout the pull
Conventional

Bar travels up shins, over knees, up thighs. Keep arms vertical.

Sumo

More vertical torso = more vertical bar path. Bar stays between legs.

How to Analyze Your Bar Path

Video Setup

Filming Guidelines

  • Side view: Essential for squat and bench. Camera at hip height, perpendicular to movement
  • Front view: Essential for deadlift. Shows bar drift and asymmetry
  • 45-degree angle: Good supplementary view, shows depth and bar path
  • Slow motion: 120fps+ helps see bar speed changes and sticking points

Analysis Apps

  • Iron Path: Free, traces bar path automatically
  • WL Analysis: Detailed tracking for Olympic lifts and powerlifts
  • Kinovea: Free desktop software, manual tracking with measurement tools
  • Coach's Eye: Side-by-side comparison, drawing tools

Fixing Common Issues

Bench: Bar Drifts Toward Belly

Usually means you're pressing straight up instead of back. Cue: "press toward your face" or "push yourself away from the bar."

Squat: Bar Moves Forward Out of Hole

Often weak quads or losing upper back tightness. Strengthen quads with front squats, leg press. Cue: "chest up" and "drive through the floor."

Deadlift: Bar Swings Away from Body

Weak lats or not engaging them properly. Cue: "bend the bar around your legs" or "protect your armpits." Add lat work and paused deadlifts.

Individual Variation

Optimal bar paths vary based on limb lengths, mobility, and style. A long-torso lifter will have different squat mechanics than a short-torso lifter. Use general principles as guidelines, then find what works for your body.

The Bottom Line

Bar path reveals efficiency. For squat and deadlift, aim for vertical. For bench, learn the J-curve. Film yourself regularly from consistent angles, and compare over time. Small improvements in bar path can mean big improvements in performance.
