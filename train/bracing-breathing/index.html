Bracing & Breathing for Heavy Lifts

Create a stable core to protect your spine and maximize force production.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

Proper bracing is the difference between a strong, safe lift and a dangerous one. Learn to create 360-degree intra-abdominal pressure and protect your spine under heavy loads.

What Is Bracing?

Bracing creates a rigid cylinder of pressure around your spine. By filling your torso with air and tensing the surrounding muscles, you create internal pressure (intra-abdominal pressure) that stabilizes your spine like an internal weightlifting belt.

360-degree bracing creates pressure in all directions, stabilizing the spine from every angle.

The Valsalva Maneuver

The Valsalva maneuver is the breathing technique used for heavy lifting:

  1. Take a big breath into your belly (not chest)
  2. Close your glottis (like you're about to cough)
  3. Brace your core hard against that trapped air
  4. Perform the lift while holding this brace
  5. Exhale at the top (or during the sticking point)
Is It Safe?

The Valsalva maneuver temporarily raises blood pressure. For healthy individuals, this is not dangerous during brief, heavy efforts. However, if you have cardiovascular concerns, consult a doctor. The spine protection benefits outweigh the temporary BP increase for most lifters.

Step-by-Step Bracing

1Breathe Into Your Belly

Take a deep diaphragmatic breath. Your belly should expand, not your chest. Think about filling your entire torso with air—front, sides, and back.

2Expand in All Directions

Push your abs out, push into your obliques, push into your lower back. If wearing a belt, you should feel pressure against the entire belt—360 degrees.

3Lock It Down

Close your throat to trap the air. Tense all your core muscles hard. You should feel rigid, like someone could punch your stomach and it wouldn't collapse.

4Maintain Through the Lift

Hold the brace throughout the hardest portion of the lift. Don't exhale until you've passed the sticking point or completed the rep.

5Reset Between Reps

Exhale at the top, take a new breath, rebrace, then perform the next rep. Each rep gets a fresh brace for maximum stability.

Belly Breathing vs. Chest Breathing

Left: Belly breathing fills the abdomen with air for core stability. Right: Chest breathing raises shoulders but provides less spinal support.

When to Breathe During Lifts

Squat

  • Breathe and brace at the top, standing
  • Hold through the descent and ascent
  • Exhale at the top after completing the rep
  • Rebrace before the next rep

Deadlift

  • Breathe and brace before pulling (at the bottom)
  • Hold through the entire pull
  • Exhale at lockout or on the way down
  • Reset and rebrace for each rep

Bench Press

  • Breathe at lockout before lowering
  • Hold through descent and press
  • Exhale at lockout
  • Can breathe during pause if needed (competition pause)

Bracing With a Belt

A lifting belt enhances bracing by giving you something to push against:

  • The belt doesn't do the work—your muscles do
  • Push your entire torso into the belt (front, sides, back)
  • Belt should be tight but allow a full breath
  • Same breathing pattern with or without belt
Practice Without a Belt First

Learn to brace properly without a belt. The belt is a tool that enhances good bracing—it can't fix bad bracing. Once your technique is solid, adding a belt provides additional feedback and support.

Common Bracing Mistakes

  • Breathing into the chest: Shoulders rise, less core pressure. Practice diaphragmatic breathing.
  • Only bracing the front: Push out in ALL directions—sides and back too.
  • Exhaling during the hard part: Losing the brace under load is dangerous. Hold until you're past the sticking point.
  • Not resetting between reps: Each heavy rep deserves a fresh breath and brace.
  • Sucking in the stomach: You want expansion, not contraction. Push OUT.

Drills to Practice

  1. Dead bug with bracing: Brace hard, move limbs while maintaining pressure
  2. Planks with breathing: Hold plank, practice belly breaths without losing position
  3. Belt breathing: Wear a belt and practice expanding into all sides
  4. Light squat holds: Hold the bottom position while practicing breathing and bracing
The Bottom Line

Bracing is the foundation of safe, strong lifting. Breathe into your belly, create 360-degree pressure, and maintain that brace through the hardest part of every heavy rep. It takes practice to make it automatic, but it's worth every rep.
