Mobility & Warm-Up for Lifters

What actually matters for getting ready to lift heavy.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

The goal of a warm-up isn't to exhaust yourself before training—it's to prepare your body to express the strength you've built. Here's what actually matters, and what you can skip.

What a Warm-Up Should Do

  • Increase tissue temperature: Warmer muscles are more pliable and contract more efficiently
  • Activate relevant muscle groups: Wake up the muscles you'll use
  • Rehearse movement patterns: Groove the technique before adding load
  • Address limiting factors: Work on specific mobility restrictions
  • Prepare mentally: Transition from life to training mode
The Minimum Effective Dose

You don't need 30 minutes of mobility work before every session. For most lifters, 10-15 minutes of targeted warm-up is plenty. The actual lifting—ramping up with progressively heavier sets—is itself part of the warm-up.

The Three-Phase Approach

Phase 1: General Warm-Up (3-5 minutes)

Light activity to increase heart rate and blood flow. Nothing fancy:

  • Rowing machine
  • Bike
  • Brisk walking
  • Jumping jacks

Goal: break a light sweat. That's it.

Phase 2: Dynamic Movement (5-8 minutes)

Move through ranges of motion you'll use in training:

Hip Circles

Standing hip circles in both directions. 10 each way. Opens up the hip capsule for squatting.

Leg Swings

Forward/back and side-to-side. 10 each direction. Dynamic hamstring and adductor prep.

World's Greatest Stretch

Lunge, plant hand, rotate thoracic spine toward front leg. 5 each side. Hits hips, thoracic spine, and shoulders.

Cat-Cow

Gentle spinal flexion and extension. 10 cycles. Wakes up the spine before loading.

Arm Circles

Small to large circles, forward and back. For press days. Gets blood flowing to shoulders.

Band Pull-Aparts

15-20 reps with light band. Upper back activation. Great before any pressing.

Phase 3: Movement-Specific Ramp-Up

This is the most important part. Progressive loading sets prepare you better than any stretch:

  • Empty bar × 10-15 (focus on positions)
  • 40% × 5
  • 60% × 3
  • 75% × 2
  • 85% × 1 (if going heavy)
  • Working sets

Each set is preparation for the next. By the time you hit working weight, you've done significant work—and your body is ready.

Mobility Work That Matters

Not everyone needs the same mobility work. Focus on your limiting factors:

For Squat Depth Issues

Ankle Mobility

Knee-over-toe stretches with foot flat. Banded ankle distractions. 2-3 minutes per side. Limited ankle mobility is a common squat limitation.

Hip Flexor Stretch

Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch with posterior pelvic tilt. Tight hip flexors pull you forward and limit depth.

Goblet Squat Hold

Sit in the bottom of a goblet squat for 30-60 seconds. Use elbows to push knees out. Builds comfortable positions.

For Overhead Position (Weightlifting)

Thoracic Spine Extension

Foam roller extension (arms overhead). Cat-camels. PVC pipe pass-throughs. Upper back mobility limits overhead position.

Lat Stretch

Child's pose with hands reaching forward. Hanging from a bar. Tight lats restrict overhead position.

For Bench Press

Pec Stretch

Doorway stretch or floor pec stretch. Allows better shoulder position for arch and tightness.

Shoulder Circles

Light band or no weight. Full range circles. Lubricates the joint before pressing.

What You Can Skip

Let's be honest about what doesn't matter much:

  • 30+ minutes of foam rolling: A few minutes is fine for feel-good effect, but it doesn't create lasting tissue changes
  • Static stretching before training: Save it for after or separate sessions—may reduce force output
  • Complex "activation" routines: Your muscles will activate when you load them
  • Mobility work for areas that aren't limiting you: Focus on your actual restrictions
Foam Rolling Reality

Foam rolling feels good and may reduce perceived muscle soreness. But claims about "breaking up fascia" or causing structural tissue changes aren't supported by research. Use it if you enjoy it; don't stress if you skip it.

Sample Warm-Up Routines

Squat Day (12-15 min)

  1. 3 min bike or row
  2. Hip circles: 10 each direction
  3. Leg swings: 10 each way
  4. Goblet squat hold: 45-60 sec
  5. Bodyweight squats: 10 reps
  6. Empty bar squats: 2×10
  7. Progressive loading to working sets

Bench Day (10-12 min)

  1. 2 min row or arm circles
  2. Band pull-aparts: 20 reps
  3. Shoulder circles: 10 each direction
  4. Push-ups: 10 reps
  5. Doorway pec stretch: 30 sec each side
  6. Empty bar bench: 2×10
  7. Progressive loading to working sets

Deadlift Day (10-12 min)

  1. 3 min bike or walking
  2. Cat-cow: 10 cycles
  3. Hip hinges (bodyweight): 10 reps
  4. Leg swings: 10 each way
  5. Light RDLs: 10 reps
  6. Progressive loading from 135/60kg up

Long-Term Mobility Development

If you have significant mobility restrictions, the warm-up isn't enough. Dedicated mobility work—separate from training—is more effective:

  • 5-10 minutes daily, targeting specific restrictions
  • End ranges held for 1-2 minutes
  • Loaded stretching can be more effective than passive
  • Consistency over weeks produces change

The goal is to expand your available range so that your training positions feel comfortable, not forced.

When Something Doesn't Feel Right

Some days, joints feel stiff or a muscle feels tight. The warm-up is where you assess this:

  • If it loosens up with movement: proceed as normal
  • If it stays tight but isn't painful: train but stay aware
  • If there's pain: modify or skip the movement

The warm-up is diagnostic. Use it to gauge readiness and adjust accordingly.

The Bottom Line

A good warm-up is short, targeted, and prepares you for the specific work ahead. Get your temperature up, move through relevant ranges, then let the barbell do the rest. Save extensive mobility work for dedicated sessions.
