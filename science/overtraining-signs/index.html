Signs of Overtraining & When to Back Off

14 min read • Recovery Science

Understanding Overtraining

Overtraining isn't just "being tired from training." It's a chronic state where accumulated stress exceeds your recovery capacity, leading to performance decrements, health problems, and prolonged dysfunction. True overtraining syndrome can take months to recover from.

The distinction between productive training stress and harmful overtraining lies in recovery. Training breaks you down; recovery builds you back stronger. When recovery can't keep pace, you start sliding backward.

Normal Training

Stress + Recovery = Adaptation

Functional Overreaching

Temporary decrease, rebounds with rest

Overtraining Syndrome

Chronic dysfunction, months to recover

As training stress accumulates beyond recovery capacity, performance declines and recovery time extends

Categories of Overtraining Symptoms

Overtraining affects your entire system. Symptoms typically appear in clusters across multiple categories—if you're only experiencing one symptom, you're probably just tired, not overtrained.

Physical Symptoms

  • Persistent muscle soreness (beyond normal DOMS)
  • Elevated resting heart rate
  • Sleep disturbances despite fatigue
  • Loss of appetite or digestive issues
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Lingering minor injuries that won't heal

Performance Symptoms

  • Decreased strength on main lifts
  • Slow bar speed at normal percentages
  • RPE creep (weights feel harder)
  • Coordination/technique breakdown
  • Inability to complete programmed volume
  • Plateau despite consistent training

Psychological Symptoms

  • Lack of motivation to train
  • Irritability and mood swings
  • Depression or apathy
  • Anxiety about training
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Mental fatigue and brain fog

Immune/Health Symptoms

  • Frequent colds or infections
  • Slow wound healing
  • Menstrual irregularities (amenorrhea)
  • Decreased libido
  • Hair loss
  • Hormonal disruption

The Warning Signs Timeline

Overtraining doesn't happen overnight. It develops progressively, with warning signs appearing before full syndrome sets in. Catching it early makes recovery much faster.

Recognizing early signs and taking action prevents progression to full overtraining syndrome

Self-Assessment Methods

Track objective data to catch overtraining before it becomes severe. These methods help remove bias from your self-assessment.

Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

Measure your resting heart rate first thing in the morning, before getting out of bed. An elevation of 5-10+ bpm above your baseline for several days suggests accumulated fatigue. Use a fitness tracker or take your pulse manually for 60 seconds.

RHR Tracking Protocol

  • Measure at the same time daily (immediately upon waking)
  • Establish your baseline over 7-14 days of normal training
  • One elevated day: probably fine, note it
  • 2-3 elevated days: consider reducing volume
  • 5+ elevated days: take a deload

Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

HRV measures the variation in time between heartbeats—a marker of nervous system recovery. Higher HRV indicates better recovery. Many apps and devices now track this automatically.

Training Log Analysis

Track RPE for your main lifts. "RPE creep"—where the same weights feel harder over time—is an objective sign of accumulated fatigue.

When the same weight feels progressively harder, fatigue is accumulating faster than recovery

Risk Factors for Overtraining

Some factors make athletes more susceptible to overtraining. Being aware of these can help you adjust training load proactively.

Risk Factor Why It Matters
High life stress Stress is stress—work, relationships, and training all draw from the same recovery pool
Poor sleep Most recovery happens during sleep. Chronic sleep debt = chronic under-recovery
Inadequate nutrition Undereating (especially protein and carbs) limits recovery capacity
Rapid volume increases Adding too much work too quickly overwhelms adaptation capacity
Training age Beginners recover faster; advanced lifters need more recovery time
Female-specific factors Menstrual cycle phase, relative energy deficiency (RED-S), hormonal status

What to Do When You Recognize Signs

Mild Symptoms (Early Catching)

Action: Light Adjustment

  • Reduce training volume by 20-30% for 3-5 days
  • Prioritize 8+ hours of sleep
  • Increase carbohydrate and protein intake
  • Add an extra rest day this week
  • Keep intensity but cut sets/reps

Moderate Symptoms (2+ Weeks)

Action: Full Deload

  • Take a structured deload week: 50% volume, 70-80% intensity
  • Sleep: treat 9 hours as medicine
  • Light movement only: walks, stretching, mobility
  • Address any life stressors if possible
  • No HIIT or conditioning work

Severe Symptoms (4+ Weeks)

Action: Extended Recovery

  • Complete rest from lifting for 1-2 weeks minimum
  • Light activity only (walking, swimming)
  • Consider seeing a doctor for blood work (hormones, thyroid, etc.)
  • Work with a coach or therapist on training approach going forward
  • Return to training gradually with significantly reduced volume

Prevention Strategies

The best treatment for overtraining is prevention. Build these practices into your training approach.

Programmed Deloads

Don't wait until you're overtrained to deload. Schedule regular deload weeks every 4-8 weeks of hard training. This prevents accumulation of fatigue before it becomes problematic.

Progressive Overload Within Limits

Increase volume gradually—no more than 10% per week. Big jumps in training load are a common cause of overreaching. Patience with progression prevents problems.

Sleep Prioritization

7-9 hours of quality sleep is non-negotiable for serious athletes. Track your sleep and treat it as importantly as your training program.

Nutrition Support

Eat enough to support your training. Undereating is a common cause of overtraining symptoms, especially in female athletes. Adequate protein (1.6-2.2g/kg) and carbohydrates (3-5g/kg for strength athletes) are essential.

Key Takeaways

  • Overtraining is systemic: It affects performance, mood, immunity, and overall health
  • Watch for clusters: Multiple symptoms across categories indicate a problem
  • Track objectively: RHR, HRV, and RPE trends reveal fatigue before you feel it
  • Act early: A 3-day adjustment is better than a 3-month recovery
  • Prevention beats treatment: Regular deloads, adequate sleep, and proper nutrition prevent overtraining
  • Recovery is training: Rest is not weakness—it's when you actually get stronger
