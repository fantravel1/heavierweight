Bench Press Guide for Women

Build a stronger bench with proper setup, technique, and programming designed for female lifters.

14 min read Updated Dec 2024

The bench press is often the most stubborn lift for women. While squats and deadlifts tend to progress quickly, the bench can feel like it's stuck forever. The good news? With proper technique and smart programming, you can break through.

Why Bench Press Matters

For powerlifters, the bench is one of three competition lifts. But even for non-competitors, it's the best exercise for building upper body pressing strength. Strong pressing carries over to daily life and other athletic pursuits.

Women often start with a significant strength gap in upper body movements compared to lower body. This isn't a limitation—it's an opportunity. You likely have more room for improvement on bench than any other lift.

The Setup: Foundation of a Good Bench

A strong bench starts before you even touch the bar. Your setup determines how much force you can generate and how safe the lift will be.

Foot Position

Feet should be firmly planted on the floor, creating a stable base. Options include:

  • Flat feet: Heels down, knees at roughly 90 degrees. Most stable for beginners.
  • Heels up: On toes, tucked back toward hips. Creates more arch but requires practice.

Either is legal in competition (federation dependent). Use what feels strongest and most stable for you.

The Arch

Yes, arching is legal, safe, and beneficial. Here's why:

  • Shortens the range of motion (lift less distance = lift more weight)
  • Creates a more stable shoulder position
  • Allows better lat engagement
  • Puts chest in a stronger pressing position
Building Your Arch

Lie on the bench and drive your traps into the pad. Pull your shoulder blades together and down. Bridge up on your traps and feet, then lower your butt to the bench while keeping your upper back tight. Your lower back should have a natural arch—not forced or extreme.

Grip Width

Competition maximum is 81cm between index fingers (the rings on most bars). Within that limit:

  • Wider grip: Shorter range of motion, more pec dominant
  • Narrower grip: Longer range of motion, more tricep dominant

Most women bench best with hands slightly outside shoulder width. Experiment to find your strongest position—usually where your forearms are vertical at the bottom of the lift.

Unracking

A bad unrack ruins the lift before it starts. Tips:

  • Set j-hooks so you barely have to press to clear them
  • Take a breath, squeeze the bar hard
  • Press out in a slight arc (not straight up)
  • Lock out over your shoulders, not your face
  • Get a liftoff from a spotter if available—especially for heavy singles

Execution: The Lift Itself

The Descent

Lower the bar with control to your sternum or just below (touch point varies by arch and grip). Keep these cues in mind:

  • "Bend the bar": Externally rotate as if bending the bar into a U-shape. This engages your lats.
  • "Pull the bar down": Use your lats to control the descent, don't just drop it.
  • Elbow angle: Typically 45-75 degrees from your torso, not flared at 90.

The Pause (Competition)

In powerlifting, the bar must pause motionless on your chest until you receive the "press" command. Practice this in training:

  • Keep tension—don't relax at the bottom
  • Stay tight in your setup
  • Hold for 1-2 seconds in training to build strength out of the hole

The Press

Drive the bar up and slightly back toward your face (the bar path is a slight diagonal, not straight up). Cues:

  • "Drive your back into the bench": Use leg drive to create force through your body.
  • "Spread the bar apart": Push outward as you press to engage triceps.
  • Stay tight: Don't let your butt come off the bench.
Leg Drive

Leg drive doesn't mean kicking your legs. It means driving your feet into the floor to create tension through your body. Think of pushing yourself up the bench (toward the head) rather than lifting your butt. Your glutes should stay on the bench.

Common Mistakes

Flared Elbows

Elbows at 90 degrees puts your shoulders in a vulnerable position. Tuck slightly (45-75 degrees) to protect your shoulders and engage your lats.

Bouncing Off the Chest

Dropping the bar and bouncing is dangerous and doesn't build strength. Control the descent and practice with pauses.

Butt Coming Up

If your butt lifts, your arch is too aggressive or your leg drive technique is wrong. This is a red light in competition.

Inconsistent Touch Point

The bar should touch the same spot every rep. Video yourself to check consistency.

Programming for Women

Women often respond well to higher frequency and higher volume on bench compared to men. This is partly because we can recover faster from upper body work (relatively lower absolute loads).

Frequency

Benching 2-4 times per week is common for serious lifters. This might include:

  • Day 1: Competition bench (heavy)
  • Day 2: Close-grip or paused bench (moderate)
  • Day 3: Volume bench or accessory pressing (lighter)

Volume

10-20 working sets per week is a reasonable range for most intermediate lifters. Start on the lower end and add volume as you adapt.

Accessory Work

Exercises that build bench strength:

  • Close-grip bench: Tricep strength
  • Dumbbell press: Pec development and stability
  • Overhead press: Shoulder strength
  • Tricep extensions: Lockout strength
  • Rows: Back strength for a stable base
  • Face pulls: Shoulder health

Bench Press Variations

Paused Bench

Hold for 2-3 seconds on chest. Builds strength out of the hole and prepares you for competition.

Close-Grip Bench

Hands at shoulder width or narrower. Emphasizes triceps.

Spoto Press

Pause 1-2 inches off the chest. Builds strength in the mid-range sticking point.

Floor Press

Pressing from the floor limits range of motion. Good for tricep development and shoulder-friendly pressing.

Feet-Up Bench

Removes leg drive. Builds raw pressing strength and core stability.

The Bottom Line

The bench press rewards patience and technique. Master your setup, practice paused reps, and don't be afraid of volume. Your bench will grow—just slower than your squat and deadlift. That's normal. Keep pressing.
