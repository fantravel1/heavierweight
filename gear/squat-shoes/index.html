Best Squat Shoes for Women

How to choose the right lifting shoes for your squat style and goals.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

The right footwear can make a significant difference in your squat. Whether you need a raised heel or prefer to go flat depends on your anatomy, squat style, and sport. Here's everything you need to know.

Why Squat Shoes Matter

Squat shoes (also called weightlifting shoes or lifters) have three key features that affect your squat:

  • Raised heel: Typically 0.5-1 inch, allows more ankle dorsiflexion and a more upright torso
  • Hard sole: Incompressible surface for force transfer (unlike squishy running shoes)
  • Secure fit: Straps keep the foot locked in place

Do You Need Squat Shoes?

The raised heel helps if you:

  • Have limited ankle mobility
  • Squat high bar and want a more upright torso
  • Do Olympic weightlifting (essentially required)
  • Find yourself falling forward or onto your toes
  • Have long femurs relative to your torso

You might prefer flat shoes if you:

  • Squat low bar with significant forward lean
  • Have good ankle mobility naturally
  • Feel more stable and powerful with feet flat
  • Prefer a wider stance (sumo-style squatting)
The Test

Try squatting with small plates under your heels. If this dramatically improves your depth and comfort, squat shoes will likely help. If it feels worse or the same, flat shoes might be better for you.

Flat Shoe Options

If you prefer no heel, these are solid choices:

  • Converse Chuck Taylors: Classic, cheap, flat sole. Approved in most federations.
  • Vans Old Skool: Similar to Converse, slightly wider.
  • Notorious Lift Slippers: Deadlift-specific, minimal sole.
  • SABO Deadlift: Purpose-built for pulling, metatarsal strap.
  • Barefoot: Allowed in training, not competition.

Squat Shoes for Deadlifts?

Most lifters prefer flat shoes or slippers for deadlifts—the raised heel increases range of motion, which you don't want. However, some sumo pullers with very upright torsos use heeled shoes successfully.

Common approach: Squat shoes for squats, flat shoes for deadlifts. Some lifters own both; others squat in flats to keep things simple.

Sizing Tips

  • Nike: Generally true to size
  • Adidas: Often runs narrow, consider half size up
  • Reebok: True to size, generous width
  • Do-Win: Runs small, size up

Order from retailers with good return policies. The right fit is snug but not painful—your foot shouldn't slide, but your toes shouldn't be crushed.

Breaking Them In

Unlike leather belts, most modern squat shoes don't need much break-in. Wear them for a few training sessions to make sure there are no hot spots. If they're uncomfortable after 2-3 sessions, they might not be the right shoe for you.

The Bottom Line

If you squat high bar or do Olympic lifting, squat shoes are worth the investment. For low bar powerlifting, it depends on your mobility and preference. Start with the plate test to see if a raised heel helps you, then choose based on your budget and foot shape.
