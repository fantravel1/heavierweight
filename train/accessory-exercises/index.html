Accessory Exercises

How to choose and program accessory work to fix weak points and build a stronger total.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

Your main lifts build strength. Your accessories build your main lifts. Smart accessory selection addresses weak points, prevents injury, and adds the volume needed for growth without burning you out.

What Are Accessories?

Accessory exercises are supplementary movements that support your main lifts (squat, bench, deadlift, or the Olympic lifts). They include:

  • Variations of main lifts: Pause squats, close-grip bench, deficit deadlifts
  • Compound movements: Rows, overhead press, lunges, pull-ups
  • Isolation exercises: Tricep extensions, leg curls, bicep curls, face pulls

Why Accessories Matter

You can't just do the main lifts forever. Accessories serve several purposes:

  • Target weak points: If your lockout is weak, tricep work helps
  • Add volume: More work for a muscle without the fatigue of heavy compounds
  • Prevent imbalances: Balance pushing with pulling, quads with hamstrings
  • Reduce injury risk: Strengthen supporting muscles and tendons
  • Build muscle: Hypertrophy from higher-rep accessory work

Identify Your Weak Points

Before choosing accessories, identify where your lifts break down:

Where You Fail Likely Weak Point Accessories to Try
Squat: out of the hole Quads, positioning Pause squats, front squats, leg press
Squat: folding forward Upper back, core SSB squats, good mornings, rows
Bench: off chest Pecs, technique Paused bench, dumbbell press, flies
Bench: at lockout Triceps Close-grip bench, skull crushers, dips
Deadlift: off floor Quads, positioning Deficit deadlifts, front squats, leg press
Deadlift: at lockout Glutes, upper back Block pulls, hip thrusts, rows
Video Your Lifts

You can't identify weak points if you can't see them. Video every heavy set from the side and front. Watch where the bar slows, where your position breaks down. That's what you need to fix.

Accessories by Lift

For the Squat

Pause Squat

Strength out of the hole, positioning

Hold 2-3 seconds at the bottom. Builds confidence and power from depth.

Front Squat

Quads, upper back, core

Keeps you upright, builds quad strength. Great for those who fold forward.

Bulgarian Split Squat

Single-leg strength, balance

Addresses asymmetries and builds stability.

Leg Press

Quad volume without spinal load

Add leg volume when your back is fatigued.

For the Bench Press

Close-Grip Bench

Triceps, lockout strength

Hands at shoulder width. Direct carryover to lockout.

Dumbbell Press

Pecs, stability, range of motion

Greater stretch at the bottom builds chest strength.

Tricep Pushdowns

Tricep volume

High-rep isolation work without fatiguing other muscles.

Face Pulls

Rear delts, shoulder health

Essential for balancing all the pressing. Do them every session.

For the Deadlift

Romanian Deadlift

Hamstrings, posterior chain

Builds the muscles that drive hip extension.

Deficit Deadlift

Strength off the floor

Stand on 1-3" platform. Makes the floor feel easy.

Barbell Row

Upper back, grip

Builds the back strength to maintain position.

Hip Thrust

Glutes, lockout power

Isolation for the glutes. Helps with lockout strength.

How to Program Accessories

Sets and Reps

Accessories typically use moderate to higher reps:

  • Compound accessories: 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps
  • Isolation exercises: 3-4 sets of 10-20 reps
  • Focus on feeling the muscle work, not max weight

Volume Guidelines

For most lifters, 2-4 accessories per session is appropriate. Total accessory work might be:

  • 3-5 exercises after your main lift(s)
  • Each muscle group hit with 10-20 sets per week (including main lifts)
  • Adjust based on recovery and how you respond

Progression

Don't obsess over progressive overload on accessories like you do main lifts. Focus on:

  • Executing with good form
  • Feeling the target muscle work
  • Gradually increasing weight or reps over time
  • Adding a rep here, 5lbs there—not PRs every week
Don't Overdo It

More is not always better. Accessories should support your main lifts, not exhaust you before them. If your accessory work is hurting your squat/bench/deadlift performance, cut back.

Sample Accessory Template

After your main lift(s), pick 3-4 accessories:

  1. Compound accessory that targets your weak point
  2. Opposite movement (pull if you pushed, etc.)
  3. Isolation for a lagging muscle
  4. Core or shoulder health work

Common Mistakes

  • Too many accessories: 8+ exercises per session is too much
  • Going too heavy: Accessories are for building, not testing
  • Ignoring back work: Row as much as you press
  • Skipping prehab: Face pulls, rotator cuff work matter
  • Randomizing: Keep accessories consistent long enough to progress
The Bottom Line

Choose accessories based on your weak points, not what looks cool. Do them consistently, progress them gradually, and remember—they exist to serve your main lifts. When your squat goes up, your accessories are working.
