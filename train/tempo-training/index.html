Tempo Training

How to read tempo notation and use controlled tempos for strength and hypertrophy.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

Tempo training prescribes how fast or slow you move through each phase of a lift. It's a powerful tool for building control, muscle, and strength—if you understand how to use it.

Reading Tempo Notation

Tempo is written as four numbers, like 3-1-2-0. Each number represents seconds spent in a different phase of the lift.

3-1-2-0
3 Eccentric (lowering)
1 Bottom (pause)
2 Concentric (lifting)
0 Top (pause)
Each number in tempo notation represents seconds for that phase
The "X" in Tempo

Sometimes you'll see "X" instead of a number, like 3-1-X-0. "X" means "explosive"—move as fast as possible. This is common for the concentric phase when training for power.

Which Phase Comes First?

The first number is always the eccentric (lowering), even if the lift starts with a concentric. This can be confusing:

Squat: 3-1-2-0

1. Lower for 3 seconds (eccentric first, even though it's the "down" phase)
2. Pause at bottom for 1 second
3. Stand up for 2 seconds
4. No pause at top, straight to next rep

Deadlift: 3-1-2-0

1. First rep starts with concentric (floor to lockout)—but tempo starts next rep
2. Lower for 3 seconds
3. Pause at floor for 1 second
4. Lift for 2 seconds
5. No pause at lockout

Common Tempo Prescriptions

Different tempos serve different training purposes

Why Use Tempo Training?

1. Increased Time Under Tension

More time under load means more mechanical tension on the muscle—a key driver of hypertrophy. A 3-0-2-0 tempo more than doubles the time under tension compared to normal lifting.

2. Better Technique

Slowing down exposes weaknesses. You can't muscle through a sticking point when moving slowly. This builds better positions and control.

3. Reduced Momentum

No bouncing out of the hole. No dive-bombing the bar. Tempo work forces muscle to do the work, not momentum.

4. Mind-Muscle Connection

Slower movement allows you to feel what's working. This awareness translates to better activation even when lifting at normal speeds.

Tempo by Lift

Squat Tempos

  • Hypertrophy: 3-1-2-0 (slow down, pause, controlled up)
  • Pause squats: 2-3-X-0 (long pause at bottom, explosive up)
  • Tempo squats: 4-0-4-0 (very controlled, great for learning)

Bench Tempos

  • Competition prep: 2-1-X-0 (natural down, pause, explosive up)
  • Hypertrophy: 3-0-2-1 (slow eccentric, controlled up, squeeze at top)
  • Spoto press: 2-2-X-0 (pause just off chest)

Deadlift Tempos

  • RDL/Accessories: 3-0-1-1 (slow lower, quick lift, pause at top)
  • Deficit work: 2-0-X-0 (controlled down, explosive up)
  • Touch-and-go: 1-0-X-0 (quick tempo, multiple reps)
When NOT to Use Tempo

Don't use tempo on maximal efforts (above 90% 1RM). The point of max attempts is to lift the most weight possible—artificial slowing reduces performance. Tempo work is for submaximal training.

Programming Tempo Work

  • Hypertrophy blocks: Use slower tempos (3-0-2-0) for most accessory work
  • Technique phases: Tempo squats teach positions without heavy load
  • Deloads: Slower tempo at lighter weight maintains tension without fatigue
  • Weakness work: Pause where you're weak to build strength through that range

Sample Application

Week 1-4: Hypertrophy Block

Squat: 4x8 @ 65% with 3-0-2-0 tempo
Bench: 4x10 @ 60% with 3-0-2-1 tempo
RDL: 3x12 with 3-1-1-0 tempo
Focus: Time under tension, technique, muscle building

Week 5-8: Strength Block

Squat: 5x5 @ 75% with 2-1-X-0 tempo (pause work)
Bench: 5x5 @ 75% with 2-1-X-0 tempo (competition pause)
Deadlift: 5x3 @ 80% with no tempo (competition style)
Focus: Strength from the pause, explosive concentric

Summary

  • Tempo notation: Eccentric-Bottom-Concentric-Top (in seconds)
  • "X" means explosive
  • Slower tempos increase time under tension for hypertrophy
  • Use pauses to build strength through sticking points
  • Don't use tempo for max attempts
  • Count in your head—"one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand"
