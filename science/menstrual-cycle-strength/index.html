How Your Menstrual Cycle Affects Strength

What the research actually shows about cycle-syncing, hormones, and strength performance.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

"Cycle-syncing" has become a fitness buzzword, with claims that you should completely restructure training around your menstrual cycle. But what does the research actually say? The answer is more nuanced—and more empowering—than the headlines suggest.

The Basics: Cycle Phases

To understand the research, you need to understand the phases. A typical cycle lasts 28 days (but 21-35 is normal):

Follicular Phase (Days 1-14)

Early follicular (menstruation): Days 1-5. Low estrogen and progesterone.
Late follicular: Days 6-14. Estrogen rises, peaks around ovulation.

Luteal Phase (Days 15-28)

Early luteal: Progesterone rises, estrogen drops then rises slightly.
Late luteal (PMS): Both hormones drop if no pregnancy occurs.

What the Research Says

The Theoretical Argument

Estrogen has anabolic properties—it enhances muscle protein synthesis and recovery. Progesterone may have catabolic effects. In theory, this suggests the follicular phase (high estrogen) might be better for strength and the luteal phase (high progesterone) might be better for lower-intensity work.

A 2020 meta-analysis examined 78 studies on menstrual cycle effects on exercise performance. The conclusion? Effects exist but are highly individual and generally small.

McNulty et al. Sports Medicine (2020)

Strength Performance Studies

Studies measuring actual strength performance (not just hormones) show mixed results:

  • Some find slightly higher strength in the follicular phase
  • Some find no difference between phases
  • Some find individual women have consistent patterns while others don't
  • Effect sizes are generally small (1-5% difference)
Key Finding

The difference between your best and worst cycle phases is likely smaller than the difference between a good and bad training day for other reasons (sleep, stress, nutrition).

Training Adaptations

More interesting is whether you should periodize training by cycle phase. Some studies suggest:

Women who trained with higher frequency during the follicular phase showed slightly greater strength gains than those who trained evenly across the cycle.

Wikström-Frisén et al. (2017) - Follicular-phase focused training study

However, this study had limitations (small sample, specific protocol), and not all studies replicate these findings.

Practical Implications

What Makes Sense

  1. Track your cycle and symptoms: Awareness helps you notice patterns specific to you
  2. Note performance and energy: Some women consistently feel stronger at certain times
  3. Adjust within reason: If you feel great, go heavy. If you feel awful, an easier session is fine.
  4. Don't overthink it: The effects are small enough that consistent training matters more than perfect periodization

What Doesn't Make Sense

  • Avoiding strength training during your period (no evidence this is harmful)
  • Only training heavy in the follicular phase
  • Completely restructuring your program every month
  • Assuming your experience matches the "average" from studies
Watch Out

If you're on hormonal birth control, your cycle is artificially regulated and this research may not apply the same way. Synthetic hormones create different patterns than natural cycling.

Symptom Management for Training

During Menstruation

Despite what some claim, training during your period is generally fine and may even help with cramps. Practical tips:

  • Warm up a bit longer if you feel stiff
  • Stay hydrated (you may retain less water)
  • NSAIDs before training if cramps are an issue (consult your doctor)
  • Period underwear or cups may be more comfortable than pads for lifting

During PMS (Late Luteal)

This is when many women feel worst. Water retention, mood changes, and fatigue are common. Strategies:

  • Accept that weights may feel heavier—that's okay
  • Focus on technique over intensity if needed
  • Consider scheduling deloads during this time if you plan that far ahead
  • Avoid judging your body by how it looks—bloating is temporary

What Elite Athletes Do

Studies on Olympic athletes show varied approaches:

  • Most don't significantly alter training around their cycle
  • Many use hormonal birth control to regulate or suppress cycles
  • Those who track often make minor adjustments (not major program changes)
  • Competition dates take priority over cycle timing
Jennifer Thompson's Approach

"I've competed at worlds during every phase of my cycle. You train through everything and compete when the meet is scheduled. Your body adapts."

The Bottom Line

Your menstrual cycle affects your training—but probably less than sleep, stress, nutrition, and program quality. Here's what the evidence supports:

  1. Track your cycle and note how you feel during training
  2. Be flexible—if you feel great, push. If not, adjust.
  3. Don't skip training based on cycle phase alone
  4. Prioritize consistency over perfect periodization
  5. Trust your body—you know it better than any study

The research is still evolving (most sports science was done on men), but we know enough to say: don't let your cycle be an excuse OR a prison. Train smart, listen to your body, and keep getting stronger.

Research Gap

Only 6% of sports science research is women-only. Much of what we "know" about training is extrapolated from male subjects. We need more research on female athletes—and the good news is that it's happening.
