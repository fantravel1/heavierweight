Squat Depth Guide

How deep is deep enough? Visual standards for competition and training.

8 min read Updated Dec 2024

"Did I hit depth?" is one of the most common questions in powerlifting. Understanding the standard—and learning to feel it—is essential for competition success and effective training.

The Competition Standard

In powerlifting (IPF and most federations), the squat must reach a depth where:

The top surface of the leg at the hip joint is lower than the top of the knee.

This is often called "hip crease below the knee" or simply "below parallel."

Visual Depth Standards

From left to right: High (red lights), Parallel (risky), Good depth (white lights), ATG (deeper than needed).
The Key Landmark

The "hip crease" is where your torso meets your thigh when you're folded at the hip—not your hip bone, not your belt, but the actual fold of the joint. This crease must be lower than the top of your kneecap.

Why Depth Matters

For Competition

High squats get red lights and don't count. Three judges vote, and you need at least 2 white lights. If you're consistently high in training, you'll bomb out in competition.

For Training

Training above depth builds strength in a limited range. Full depth squats build complete leg development and the strength you can actually use in competition.

For General Fitness

Full range of motion squats build more muscle, improve mobility, and carry over better to athletics and daily life than partial squats.

Common Reasons for Missing Depth

  • Limited hip mobility: Hips can't fold deep enough
  • Limited ankle mobility: Knees can't travel forward, torso compensates
  • Stance issues: Too narrow or too wide for your anatomy
  • Weight too heavy: Body stops short to protect itself
  • Poor proprioception: You don't know where depth is
  • Fear: Getting stuck in the hole is scary

How to Improve Depth

1. Use a Depth Target

Squat to a box set at proper depth. Touch and go (don't sit), or pause briefly. This teaches your body where to stop.

2. Improve Mobility

  • Hip mobility: 90/90 stretches, pigeon pose, deep squat holds
  • Ankle mobility: Wall ankle stretches, squat wedges, heel-elevated squats
  • Squat shoes: Heel elevation helps many lifters hit depth easier

3. Adjust Your Stance

Try wider or narrower stances. Turn your toes out more. Find what allows YOUR hips to open fully.

4. Video Every Session

Film from the side, at hip height. Review after each set. You can't fix what you can't see.

How Deep Is Too Deep?

For powerlifting, there's no penalty for going deeper than required—but going too deep can:

  • Make it harder to reverse the weight (losing tension at bottom)
  • Cause butt wink if you lack the mobility
  • Use energy you could save for the ascent

Aim for just below parallel—enough to clearly pass, not so deep that you lose position.

Competition Tips

In competition, go slightly deeper than you think you need to. Judges see a different angle than your training videos. What looks good from the side might look high from the judge's position. Build in a margin of safety.
