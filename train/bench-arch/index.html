Building a Safe Bench Press Arch

12 min read • Technique

Why Arch?

A proper bench press arch shortens the range of motion, places your shoulders in a safer position, and allows you to use leg drive effectively. It's not cheating—it's optimal technique for powerlifting. However, building a good arch takes time, mobility work, and practice.

The goal isn't to arch as extremely as possible. The goal is to create a stable, powerful position that you can maintain throughout your set while keeping your shoulders healthy.

An arch reduces range of motion and puts your shoulders in a more favorable position

Anatomy of a Good Arch

A powerlifting arch isn't just "bending backward." It involves multiple contact points, specific muscle engagement, and structural support.

Five critical contact points: head, upper back, glutes, and both feet

The Contact Points

1. Head

The back of your head stays on the bench throughout the lift. Some federations require this; all federations allow it. Lifting your head breaks stability and is a common red light.

2. Upper Back/Traps

This is your primary base. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and down ("in your back pockets"). Your upper traps should be in firm contact with the bench, supporting most of your upper body weight.

3. Glutes

Your butt must stay on the bench in all federations. This is non-negotiable. The arch happens through your thoracic spine, not by lifting your hips. If your glutes come up, it's a red light and the lift doesn't count.

4. Feet

Feet position varies by federation (flat vs. heels up), but they should be driving into the floor throughout the lift. Your feet are the start of leg drive, which transfers force through your body to the bar.

Building Arch Mobility

Most people lack the thoracic spine mobility for a good arch. This takes consistent mobility work over weeks and months. Don't force it—build it gradually.

Essential Mobility Drills

1

Foam Roller Thoracic Extension

Place a foam roller perpendicular to your spine at mid-back. Support your head with your hands. Extend over the roller, opening your chest toward the ceiling. Move the roller up and down your thoracic spine. 2-3 minutes daily.

2

Bench T-Spine Extension

Kneel in front of a bench with your elbows on the pad, hands together in prayer position. Sink your chest toward the floor while keeping your elbows on the bench. Hold 30-60 seconds, repeat 2-3 times.

3

Cat-Cow with Emphasis on Extension

Standard cat-cow, but spend more time in the "cow" (extension) position. Focus on extending through your upper back, not just your lower back. 10-15 reps, focusing on the extension phase.

4

Supine Arch Practice

Lie on the floor with knees bent. Practice arching your upper back while keeping your glutes and head on the ground. This mimics the bench position without load. Hold for time, building duration.

Common Arch Mistakes

Arching Through the Lower Back

The arch should come from thoracic extension, not lumbar hyperextension. If your lower back is doing most of the work, you'll get pain and lose stability. Focus on opening your chest and upper back.

Losing Position on Unrack

If you unrack yourself with a big arch, you'll often lose your setup as you reach for the bar. Always get a handoff from a spotter to maintain your position. This is not optional for a significant arch.

Glutes Coming Up

If your butt leaves the bench, the lift is red-lighted. This often happens when lifters try to arch beyond their mobility or lose tension at the bottom. Practice keeping glute contact at all times.

Feet Sliding or Unstable

Your feet are the foundation of leg drive. If they're slipping or poorly positioned, you lose power transfer. Use grippy shoes, position feet deliberately, and practice driving through your heels or balls of feet.

Improving Your Arch Over Time

Building a bigger, more stable arch takes months of consistent work. Here's a progressive approach:

Weeks 1-4: Foundation

  • Daily thoracic mobility work (5-10 min)
  • Practice setup sequence with empty bar
  • Focus on maintaining position, not maximizing arch
  • Video every session to check position

Weeks 5-8: Building

  • Continue mobility work
  • Gradually increase arch height as mobility allows
  • Practice holding arch for longer sets
  • Add light pause work to test position stability

Weeks 9+: Refining

  • Maintain mobility with weekly sessions
  • Work on leg drive integration
  • Test arch under heavier loads
  • Fine-tune feet and grip positioning

Key Takeaways

  • Arch = thoracic extension: Not lower back hyperextension
  • Five contact points: Head, upper back, glutes, and both feet
  • Setup sequence matters: Follow the same steps every time
  • Always get a handoff: Don't unrack alone with a significant arch
  • Mobility takes time: Build gradually over weeks and months
  • Glutes stay down: This is the most common competition red light
