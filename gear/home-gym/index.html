Home Gym Setup for Powerlifting

Build your ideal training environment from budget to elite.

13 min read Updated Dec 2024

A home gym eliminates commute time, wait times, and gym politics. For serious powerlifters, it can be a game-changer. Here's how to build one at any budget.

Space Requirements

Garage bays (typically 10' x 20') work well; spare bedrooms are tight but workable

Budget Tiers

Budget Starter
$1,000-2,000
  • Basic squat stands or half rack
  • Economy barbell
  • 300lb bumper/iron set
  • Flat bench
  • Horse stall mats
Solid Setup
$2,500-4,000
  • Full power rack
  • Quality power bar
  • 500lb plate set
  • Competition bench
  • Platform + mats
Competition Ready
$5,000-10,000+
  • Competition rack
  • IPF-spec bar + deadlift bar
  • Calibrated plates
  • Competition bench
  • Full platform + accessories

Essential Equipment

1. Power Rack / Squat Stand

Priority #1

The rack is your foundation. Don't cheap out here—it needs to safely handle heavy weight.

  • Half rack / squat stand: $200-600, space-efficient, less safe for failed reps
  • Full power rack: $400-1500+, safer, more versatile, takes more space
  • Key features: Westside hole spacing, quality J-cups, safety straps or pins
  • Brands: Rogue, Rep Fitness, Titan, Fringe Sport

2. Barbell

Start with a quality power bar; specialty bars are nice-to-haves later

3. Weight Plates

Plate Options

  • Iron plates: Cheapest per pound, loud, can damage floors
  • Bumper plates: Rubber-coated, quieter, can drop, take up more sleeve space
  • Calibrated plates: Competition-accurate, expensive, thin profile
  • How much: Start with 300-400lbs, build to 500-700lbs over time

4. Bench

Bench Requirements

  • Width: 12" (30cm) for competition legality, wider for training is fine
  • Height: ~17" (43cm) for competition, adjustable is convenient
  • Pad: Firm enough to push off, grippy surface
  • Weight capacity: 1000lb+ rated for safety margin
  • Brands: Rogue, Rep, Titan all make solid options ($200-600)

5. Flooring

Floor Protection

  • Horse stall mats: 4'x6'x3/4", ~$50 each, durable, heavy
  • Rubber gym tiles: Interlocking, easier to install, more expensive
  • Deadlift platform: Plywood + rubber for deadlift area, protects floor from drops
  • Coverage: At minimum, cover rack footprint + 2' around

Nice-to-Have Additions

Build over time; buy essentials first, add accessories as budget allows

Buying Strategy

New vs. Used

  • Buy new: Barbells (used bars can be bent/damaged), benches (wear affects performance)
  • Buy used: Plates (iron doesn't wear out), racks (check welds), dumbbells
  • Where to find used: Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, garage sales, gym liquidations

Timing

  • Best deals: January (New Year's resolution failures), summer (people moving)
  • Black Friday: Good for new equipment from major brands
  • Patience pays: Set alerts, check daily, good deals go fast
Quality Over Quantity

Start with fewer, better pieces rather than lots of cheap equipment. A quality rack, bar, plates, and bench will last decades. Cheap equipment breaks, bends, or becomes unusable. Invest in the essentials; add accessories later.

Safety Considerations

Training Alone Safely

  • Safety straps/pins: Set them properly before every heavy set
  • Learn the roll of shame: Practice bailing from bench at light weights
  • Know your limits: Don't attempt true maxes alone
  • Phone accessible: Keep it nearby, not across the room
  • Tell someone: Let housemates know you're training

  • Rogue Ohio Power Bar: $350

  • Rogue competition bench: $550

The Bottom Line

A home gym pays for itself in 1-3 years of gym membership savings, then it's free training forever. Start with the essentials: rack, bar, plates, bench, flooring. Add over time. Buy quality where it matters (bar, rack), save on plates by buying used. Train safely—set your safeties and know how to bail.
