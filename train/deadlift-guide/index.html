Deadlift Guide: Conventional vs Sumo

Find your strongest pulling stance and master the technique that's right for your body.

16 min read Updated Dec 2024

Conventional or sumo? It's one of the most common questions in powerlifting. The answer isn't about which is "better"—it's about which is better for YOU.

The Quick Comparison

Conventional

  • Feet hip-width apart
  • Hands outside legs
  • More hip hinge dominant
  • Greater range of motion
  • Emphasizes back and hamstrings

Sumo

  • Wide stance, toes out
  • Hands inside legs
  • More upright torso
  • Shorter range of motion
  • Emphasizes hips and quads

Conventional Deadlift Technique

Setup

  1. Stand with feet hip-width apart, toes slightly out
  2. Bar over mid-foot (about 1 inch from shins)
  3. Bend at hips, then knees to grip the bar
  4. Grip just outside your legs (double overhand, mixed, or hook grip)
  5. Shoulders slightly in front of the bar
  6. Back flat, chest up, lats engaged

The Pull

  • "Push the floor away": Initiate with leg drive
  • Bar stays close: Drag it up your legs
  • Hips and shoulders rise together: Don't let hips shoot up first
  • Lockout: Stand tall, squeeze glutes, don't hyperextend
Common Conventional Mistake

The "stripper deadlift"—hips shoot up, back rounds, and you good-morning the weight. Fix by thinking "chest up" and "push through the floor" rather than pulling with your back.

Sumo Deadlift Technique

Setup

  1. Wide stance—how wide depends on your mobility and structure
  2. Toes pointed out significantly (45+ degrees)
  3. Shins should be vertical or near-vertical at the start
  4. Grip inside your legs, arms straight down
  5. Chest up, hips close to the bar
  6. Knees pushed out over toes

The Pull

  • "Spread the floor": Push feet apart (they won't move, but the intent matters)
  • "Open your hips": Drive knees out as you pull
  • Stay patient off the floor: Sumo is slow to break, fast at lockout
  • Lockout: Drive hips through, squeeze glutes
Common Sumo Mistake

Hips shooting up or knees caving in. Sumo requires hip strength and mobility. If your hips rise first, you're essentially doing a wide-stance conventional. Focus on keeping your chest up and driving your knees out.

How to Choose Your Stance

Body Structure Considerations

Your anatomy influences which stance works better:

  • Longer torso, shorter legs: Often favor conventional
  • Shorter torso, longer legs: Often favor sumo
  • Longer arms: Advantage in conventional (less range of motion)
  • Good hip mobility: Makes sumo more accessible
  • Strong back: May favor conventional
  • Strong hips/quads: May favor sumo

The Test

The best way to know? Try both. Spend 4-8 weeks training each stance and see which:

  • Feels more natural
  • Allows you to lift more weight
  • Has a sticking point you can train through
  • Causes less discomfort or strain

Why Many Women Pull Sumo

You'll notice many female powerlifters pull sumo. This isn't coincidence:

  • Hip structure: Women often have hip anatomy that favors external rotation
  • Proportions: Relatively longer legs and shorter torsos are common
  • Hip strength: Women often have strong hips relative to their back
  • Mobility: Women tend to have better hip mobility on average

But this is a generalization. Plenty of elite female deadlifters pull conventional. Choose based on YOUR body, not averages.

Grip Options

Double Overhand

Both palms facing you. Safest, but grip often fails before strength does. Good for warm-ups and building grip strength.

Mixed Grip

One palm forward, one back. Prevents bar roll but creates asymmetry. Alternate which hand is supinated in training to balance development.

Hook Grip

Thumb wrapped under fingers. Secure like mixed grip but symmetrical. Hurts at first—your thumbs adapt over time. Preferred by most competitive lifters.

Hook Grip Tips

Tape your thumbs for heavy singles. Use it for all your pulls so your thumbs adapt. The pain fades after a few weeks of consistent use. It's worth it.

Programming

Frequency

Most lifters deadlift 1-2 times per week. The deadlift is taxing—you don't need high frequency to progress. Quality over quantity.

Volume

5-10 working sets per week is typical. This includes variations. More isn't always better with deadlifts—recovery matters.

Variations to Include

  • Deficit deadlifts: Harder off the floor (stand on plates)
  • Block/rack pulls: Overload lockout strength
  • Paused deadlifts: Build strength at sticking point
  • Romanian deadlifts: Hamstring and posterior chain work
  • The opposite stance: Conventional lifters should still sumo sometimes, and vice versa

Common Questions

Is sumo cheating?

No. It's a legal lift in powerlifting. Both stances require skill and strength. This debate is mostly from people who don't compete. Pull whatever you're strongest at.

Should I train both stances?

Yes. Even if you compete with one stance, training the other builds overall strength and addresses weaknesses. Use your non-competition stance for accessory work.

My back always rounds—is that bad?

Some upper back rounding is acceptable for experienced lifters. Lower back rounding is risky and usually means the weight is too heavy or your starting position is wrong. Video yourself and be honest.

The Bottom Line

There's no universally superior stance. Experiment with both, be patient while learning, and choose based on your body and your results—not internet opinions. Then train it consistently and watch your deadlift grow.
