Master the Hip Hinge

The foundational movement pattern for deadlifts, RDLs, good mornings, and more.

8 min read Updated Dec 2024

The hip hinge is arguably the most important movement pattern in strength training. Master it, and you'll unlock proper deadlift mechanics, safer lifting, and more powerful posterior chain development.

What Is a Hip Hinge?

A hip hinge is a movement pattern where you bend at the hips while keeping your spine neutral. Unlike a squat (which bends primarily at the knees), a hinge loads the posterior chain—glutes, hamstrings, and back.

The hip hinge: bending at the hips while maintaining a neutral spine. The green dot marks the hip joint as the pivot point.

Why It Matters

The hip hinge is the foundation for:

  • Conventional deadlifts — the primary hip hinge movement
  • Romanian deadlifts (RDLs) — pure hinge pattern
  • Good mornings — loaded hip hinge
  • Kettlebell swings — explosive hinge
  • Bent-over rows — hinged position for pulling
  • Picking things up safely — everyday application

Squat University: How to Hip Hinge Properly

How to Hip Hinge

Step 1: Stand Tall

Feet hip to shoulder width apart, slight bend in knees. Stand tall with your chest up and core engaged.

Step 2: Push Hips Back

Initiate the movement by pushing your hips backward—like you're trying to touch a wall behind you with your butt. This is the key: the hips move first and move back.

Step 3: Maintain Neutral Spine

As you hinge, your back stays flat. No rounding, no excessive arching. Your spine angle changes, but its shape doesn't.

Step 4: Feel the Hamstrings

As you push your hips back, you should feel a stretch in your hamstrings. This is the "loading" of the posterior chain.

Step 5: Return by Driving Hips Forward

Reverse the movement by squeezing your glutes and driving your hips forward. Stand tall at the top.

Key Cues

  • "Hips back" — Push butt to the wall behind you
  • "Soft knees" — Slight bend, not locked, not deep
  • "Long spine" — Keep back flat throughout
  • "Load the hamstrings" — Feel the stretch
  • "Squeeze and stand" — Glutes drive you up

Common Mistakes

Left: Correct hip hinge with neutral spine. Middle: Dangerous rounded back. Right: This is a squat, not a hinge.
  • Rounding the back: Keep your chest up and back flat. If you can't, you've gone too deep.
  • Squatting instead of hinging: If your knees bend a lot and your torso stays upright, you're squatting. Push hips BACK, not down.
  • Not going back enough: The hips need to travel backward. Think "butt to the wall."
  • Hyperextending at the top: Stand tall, don't lean back excessively.

Hip Hinge Drills

1. Wall Touch Drill

Stand facing away from a wall, about 6 inches away. Push your hips back until your butt touches the wall. Step forward an inch and repeat. This teaches the "hips back" pattern.

2. Dowel/PVC Pipe Drill

Hold a dowel along your spine—touching your head, upper back, and tailbone. Hinge while maintaining all three contact points. If you lose contact, your spine is moving.

3. Romanian Deadlift (RDL)

The RDL is a pure hip hinge. Start with light weight, lower until you feel hamstrings stretch, then stand. Perfect for grooving the pattern.

Practice Daily

Spend 5 minutes daily practicing bodyweight hip hinges. The pattern needs to become automatic before you load it. Once it's ingrained, your deadlifts will feel natural.

From Hinge to Deadlift

Once you've mastered the hip hinge, the deadlift is just a hip hinge with weight in your hands:

  1. Set up with bar over midfoot
  2. Hip hinge down to grip the bar
  3. Brace, create tension
  4. Stand up by driving hips forward

The pattern is identical—the bar just adds load.
