RPE Training Guide

Autoregulate your training based on daily readiness.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

Some days you walk into the gym and everything feels light. Other days, your warm-up weight feels like a max. RPE lets you adjust for this reality instead of blindly following percentages.

What Is RPE?

RPE stands for Rate of Perceived Exertion. In strength training, it's a 1-10 scale that measures how hard a set was based on how many reps you had left "in the tank."

Instead of prescribing "squat 80% for 5 reps," a program might say "squat for 5 reps at RPE 8"—meaning you should have about 2 reps left in reserve after your set.

The RPE Scale

RPE Reps in Reserve Description
10 0 Maximum effort. Could not do another rep.
9.5 0 Could maybe do 1 more with significant form breakdown.
9 1 Could definitely do 1 more rep.
8.5 1-2 Could do 1-2 more reps.
8 2 Could do 2 more reps.
7.5 2-3 Could do 2-3 more reps.
7 3 Could do 3 more reps. Starting to feel challenging.
6 4+ Could do 4+ more reps. Warm-up territory.
The Key Insight

RPE adjusts for daily readiness. If 80% normally feels like RPE 8, but today it feels like RPE 9, you're not at your best. Instead of forcing the weight and grinding ugly reps, you'd use slightly less weight to hit the intended effort level.

Why Use RPE?

Accounts for Daily Variation

Your strength fluctuates daily based on sleep, stress, nutrition, recovery, menstrual cycle, and countless other factors. RPE lets you train appropriately for TODAY, not some theoretical average.

Prevents Overtraining

Blindly hitting prescribed percentages when you're beat up leads to accumulated fatigue and eventually injury. RPE gives you permission to back off when needed.

Allows Pushing When Ready

On great days, strict percentages hold you back. If 80% feels like RPE 7, you could handle more. RPE lets you capitalize on good days.

Develops Self-Awareness

Learning to accurately assess effort makes you a better lifter. You develop intuition about your body that serves you for years.

How to Use RPE

Start with Ramp-Up Sets

Warm up with progressively heavier singles or doubles. Note how each weight feels. This tells you about your readiness for the day.

Example: Finding Your Working Weight

Program calls for: 3×5 at RPE 8

  • Bar × 10 (warm-up)
  • 60kg × 5 (RPE 5)
  • 80kg × 3 (RPE 6)
  • 95kg × 1 (RPE 7)
  • 105kg × 1 (RPE 8) ← This tells you ~105kg is your RPE 8 single today
  • Work sets: 95kg × 5 × 3 (should be around RPE 8 for sets of 5)

Rate After the Set

Immediately after completing a set, ask: "How many more reps could I have done with good form?" That number determines your RPE.

Adjust as Needed

If your first work set is harder or easier than intended, adjust weight for subsequent sets. The goal is consistent effort, not consistent weight.

Common RPE Prescriptions

RPE 6-7: Volume/Hypertrophy Work

High reps, moderate effort. Focus on technique and accumulating volume. 3-4 reps in reserve.

RPE 8: Standard Working Sets

The sweet spot for most training. Hard enough to drive adaptation, sustainable enough to recover from. 2 reps in reserve.

RPE 9: Heavy Singles/Intensity Work

Near-maximal effort. Use sparingly. 1 rep in reserve. Common for competition prep.

RPE 10: True Max

All-out effort. Reserve for competition or rare testing. Nothing left.

Learning to Rate Accurately

Most beginners underestimate their RPE—what they call RPE 8 is often actually RPE 6. This skill takes time to develop:

Tips for Calibration

  • Video your sets: Bar speed tells the truth. Slow grinding = high RPE.
  • Take sets to failure occasionally: Knowing what true RPE 10 feels like calibrates everything else.
  • Track RPE alongside weight: Over time, you'll see patterns that refine your ratings.
  • Be honest: Rating sets accurately matters more than ego.

Signs You're Underestimating

  • Every set feels "easy" but you're not progressing
  • You finish sets and immediately feel ready for another
  • Video shows fast bar speed on what you rated RPE 8-9

Signs You're Overestimating

  • Sets are frequently grinders with form breakdown
  • You're constantly fatigued and not recovering
  • Progress stalls or goes backward

RPE vs. Percentages

Both have their place:

  • Percentages: Good for beginners who can't yet rate RPE accurately, or for specific peaking protocols
  • RPE: Better for experienced lifters, accounts for daily variation, requires self-awareness
  • Both: Many programs use percentages as a starting point with RPE for adjustment
RPE Charts

Many coaches use RPE-to-percentage charts as a guide. For example, RPE 8 for 5 reps might correspond to roughly 80% of your 1RM. These charts help translate between systems but remember they're estimates—your actual numbers may differ.

Practical Application

Sample RPE-Based Session

Squat Day

  1. Warm-up: Bar × 10, 50% × 5, 65% × 3
  2. Work up in singles to find today's RPE 8 single
  3. Drop to ~85% of that weight for work sets
  4. 3×5 at RPE 8 (adjust weight between sets if needed)
  5. Back-off set: 1×8 at RPE 7

When to Use RPE

  • Main compound lifts (squat, bench, deadlift, press)
  • Heavy accessory work (rows, RDLs)
  • Any lift where effort matters for adaptation

When Percentages Are Fine

  • Light accessory work
  • Deload weeks (where effort is intentionally low)
  • When you're new and still learning the movements

Common Mistakes

  • Ego rating: Calling sets easier than they are to feel stronger
  • Not adjusting: Hitting prescribed weight regardless of how it feels
  • Overcomplicating: Obsessing over RPE 8 vs 8.5 instead of just training hard
  • Ignoring context: RPE 8 during a deload should feel very different than RPE 8 during a strength block
The Bottom Line

RPE is a tool for smarter training. It lets you push hard on good days and back off on bad ones while maintaining consistent effort. Learn to rate accurately, adjust as needed, and use it to train harder AND smarter over time.
