Grip Training for Strength Athletes

Build grip strength so it never limits your deadlift.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

Grip fails before the legs and back do. That's frustrating when your body can lift the weight but your hands can't hold it. Here's how to build grip strength so it stops being a limiting factor.

Grip Types in Lifting

Grip Type Description Best For
Double Overhand Both palms facing you Warm-ups, grip training
Mixed Grip One palm forward, one back Heavy deadlifts, competition
Hook Grip Thumb under fingers Olympic lifting, some deadlifters

Why Double Overhand Fails First

Double overhand grip allows the bar to roll toward your fingers. As weight increases, the rotational force becomes too much for your forearms to counter. That's why most lifters switch to mixed or hook grip for heavy work.

But training with double overhand for as long as possible builds the baseline grip strength that supports all other grips.

The Double Overhand Rule

Use double overhand for all warm-up sets and as many working sets as possible. Only switch to mixed/hook when double overhand genuinely fails. This builds grip over time without neglecting it.

Mixed Grip

Mixed grip prevents bar rotation by having opposing forces on each side. It's secure and allows most lifters to hold significantly more than double overhand.

  • Pros: Secure, no pain like hook grip, no equipment needed
  • Cons: Asymmetrical stress, bicep tear risk (rare but real)
  • Tip: Keep the underhand arm straight and locked

Alternating Sides

Some lifters alternate which hand is supinated to balance stress. This can feel awkward—most find one side more natural. If you're not having asymmetry issues, sticking with your preferred side is fine.

Hook Grip

Hook grip wraps the thumb around the bar, then locks the fingers over the thumb. It's symmetrical and very secure—but it hurts until you adapt.

  • Pros: Symmetrical, very secure, no bicep tear risk
  • Cons: Painful initially, requires adaptation
  • Tip: Tape your thumbs; it gets easier over time

Learning Hook Grip

  1. Start with lighter weights to learn the position
  2. Use hook grip on all deadlift warm-ups
  3. Tape thumbs if needed (athletic tape works)
  4. Expect 2-4 weeks of discomfort before adaptation

Grip Training Exercises

Double Overhand Deadlift Holds

After your last deadlift set, hold the bar at lockout for time with double overhand grip.

  • Start with 50-60% of your max
  • Hold for 20-30 seconds
  • Progress weight or time

Fat Grip Training

Thicker bars or grip attachments (Fat Gripz) make the bar harder to hold, forcing adaptation.

  • Use on rows, curls, or carries
  • Not for heavy deadlifts (too slippery)
  • Great for accessory work

Farmer's Walks/Carries

Walk while holding heavy dumbbells or farmers handles. Challenges grip under dynamic load.

  • 50-100% of bodyweight per hand is a good target
  • Walk 30-60 seconds or a set distance
  • Also builds traps, core, and conditioning

Dead Hangs

Hang from a pull-up bar for time. Simple and effective.

  • Aim for 30-60 second holds
  • Add weight when bodyweight becomes easy
  • Good shoulder health bonus

Plate Pinches

Pinch two plates together (smooth sides out) and hold.

  • Targets pinch strength specifically
  • Start with 2x10lb plates
  • Hold for 15-30 seconds

Towel Training

Wrap a towel over a bar and grip the towel. Creates a thick, unstable grip.

  • Use for rows, pull-ups, or curls
  • Very challenging
  • Builds crushing and pinch strength

Programming Grip Work

Grip training doesn't need to dominate your program. A few sets at the end of sessions is enough:

  • 2-3x per week
  • After main work (don't fatigue grip before deadlifts)
  • 1-3 exercises, 2-3 sets each
  • Progress over time

Sample Grip Work

  • Monday (after deadlifts): Double overhand holds 3×20-30 sec
  • Wednesday (after rows): Farmer's walks 3×40 sec
  • Friday (after pull-ups): Dead hangs 3×max time

When to Use Straps

Straps eliminate grip as a factor—useful for building back strength without grip being the limiter.

Good Uses for Straps

  • High-rep deadlift sets where grip fails before back
  • Romanian deadlifts and rows
  • Shrugs
  • When training around a hand injury

Don't Use Straps For

  • All your deadlift sets (you need to train grip)
  • Competition prep (straps aren't allowed)
  • Every pulling movement
Balance Is Key

Use straps strategically to train your back without grip limiting you. But also train grip directly and use raw grip for as much work as possible. Both matter.

Other Grip Tips

  • Chalk: Always use it. Dry hands grip better.
  • Bar care: Knurling matters. Clean your bar.
  • Hand position: Grip low in the fingers, not deep in the palm
  • Avoid gloves: They add bar diameter and reduce feel
  • Callus management: File calluses to prevent tears

Progress Markers

Track your grip strength improvements:

  • How much can you double overhand deadlift?
  • How long can you hold your working weight?
  • What weight can you farmer's walk?
  • How long can you dead hang?

When these numbers go up, your deadlift won't be limited by grip.

The Bottom Line

Train grip intentionally, use double overhand as long as possible, add specific grip work 2-3x weekly, and use straps only when it makes sense. Grip strength is trainable—train it, and it stops being a limiting factor.
