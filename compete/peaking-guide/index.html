Competition Peaking Guide

How to taper and peak so you show up at your strongest on meet day.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

You've trained for months. Now it's time to translate that training into peak performance on meet day. The final 2-4 weeks are about shedding fatigue while maintaining strength—here's how to do it.

What Is Peaking?

Peaking is the strategic reduction of training stress (tapering) combined with specific preparation for competition. The goal is to arrive at the meet with:

  • Accumulated fatigue dissipated
  • Strength and skill maintained
  • Nervous system fresh and primed
  • Confidence high from hitting heavy weights in training

Done right, you'll feel stronger on meet day than you did at any point in training.

The Fitness-Fatigue Model

Training builds both fitness and fatigue. Fatigue masks fitness. When you reduce training stress, fatigue drops faster than fitness—revealing the strength you've built. That's peaking in a nutshell.

General Peaking Timeline

A typical peak for powerlifting or weightlifting lasts 2-4 weeks:

4-3 Weeks Out

  • Last heavy volume week
  • May include final heavy singles or doubles
  • Accessories start to reduce
  • Focus on competition-specific movements

2 Weeks Out

  • Volume drops significantly (40-60% of normal)
  • Intensity stays high—practice openers
  • Minimal accessories, if any
  • Sessions are shorter

1 Week Out

  • Volume drops further (20-40% of normal)
  • Final heavy session 7-10 days out typically
  • Light movement work only in final days
  • Focus shifts to recovery and logistics

Final Week Protocol

Here's what the last 7 days might look like for a Saturday meet:

Sunday (7 days out)

Final heavy session. Take openers or slightly above. All three lifts. This is your last hard work.

Monday-Tuesday (6-5 days out)

Rest or very light activity. Focus on sleep and nutrition.

Wednesday (4 days out)

Light movement session. Empty bar or 50% for a few sets. Keep things loose. No grinding.

Thursday (3 days out)

Rest. If cutting weight, water manipulation may begin. Pack your gear.

Friday (1 day out / Weigh-in day)

Weigh in. Rehydrate and refuel if you cut. Light walkthrough in competition venue if possible.

Saturday (Meet day)

Execute. Trust your training. Hit PRs.

How Much Volume to Cut

General guidelines for the taper:

  • Week 2 out: 50-60% of normal training volume
  • Week 1 out: 20-40% of normal training volume
  • Intensity: Stays high (90%+ on key sessions)
  • Frequency: Can reduce slightly, but not drastically

The goal is reducing workload while keeping the nervous system primed. Cut sets and accessories first, not the heavy work.

Opener Selection

Your openers should be:

  • 100% guaranteed: Something you could hit any day, even tired or sick
  • Approximately 90%: Of your expected max
  • Smooth and fast: Builds confidence and gets you on the board
The Opener Rule

If there's any doubt about making your opener, it's too heavy. Missing your opener derails your meet. Conservative openers set up bigger second and third attempts.

Attempt Selection Strategy

A typical 9-for-9 approach:

  • Opener (1st attempt): ~90% of goal—guaranteed make
  • Second attempt: ~95-97%—should feel strong
  • Third attempt: ~100%+ —PR territory, go for it

Jumps between attempts: 5-8% is typical. Don't make massive jumps—they're harder to adjust to.

Mental Preparation

Visualization

In the final weeks, spend time visualizing:

  • Each lift executed perfectly
  • The commands and your response
  • The feeling of completing successful attempts
  • How you'll handle waiting between attempts

Process Over Outcome

Focus on what you can control:

  • Your warm-up routine
  • Your setup on each lift
  • Your cues and technique
  • Your breathing and bracing

The outcome follows from executing the process. Trust your preparation.

Sleep and Recovery

The final two weeks, prioritize sleep above almost everything:

  • Target: 8-10 hours per night
  • Consistency: Same bedtime and wake time
  • Naps: If needed and they don't disrupt night sleep
  • Pre-meet night: May not sleep well—that's normal and won't ruin your meet

Nutrition During the Peak

If you're not cutting weight:

  • Maintain normal caloric intake
  • Don't try new foods or supplements
  • Stay hydrated
  • Carbs are your friend—don't restrict them

If you're cutting weight, work with an experienced coach or follow a proven protocol. Weight cutting is beyond the scope of this guide—see our weight class guide for more.

Common Peaking Mistakes

  • Training too heavy too close to meet: Last heavy session should be 7-10 days out
  • Not reducing volume enough: You should feel undertrained, almost restless
  • Trying new techniques: Competition is not the time to experiment
  • Changing equipment: Use the same gear you've trained in
  • Overcomplicating nutrition: Eat normally, sleep well
  • Aggressive openers: Start conservative, build momentum

Signs Your Peak Is Working

  • Feeling restless from reduced training (good sign)
  • Weights feeling lighter in your last sessions
  • Better sleep and energy
  • Reduced aches and pains
  • Eager to lift heavy

If You Feel Weak During the Taper

Some lifters panic when the taper starts. Weights might feel heavy because:

  • You're used to being fatigued—fresh feels "off"
  • Reduced frequency makes technique feel rusty
  • Anxiety is playing tricks on you

Trust the process. The peak works even when it doesn't feel like it. You're not losing strength in a week.

The Bottom Line

Peaking is about letting go and trusting your training. Reduce volume dramatically while keeping intensity high. Take 7-10 days of easy work before the meet. Choose conservative openers. Sleep, eat, and trust the process. The work is done—now express it.
