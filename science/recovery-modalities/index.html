Recovery Modalities: What Works

An evidence-based look at recovery methods for strength athletes.

14 min read Updated Dec 2024

The recovery industry wants to sell you expensive gadgets and therapies. But what actually works? Here's an evidence-based breakdown of common recovery modalities—what helps, what doesn't, and what depends on context.

The Baseline

Before spending money on recovery modalities, make sure you've optimized the fundamentals: adequate sleep (7-9+ hours), sufficient nutrition (protein and calories), smart programming with built-in recovery, and stress management. These matter more than any gadget or therapy.

The Modalities

Sleep Strong Evidence

The most important recovery modality, period. Growth hormone release, protein synthesis, neural recovery, and tissue repair all occur primarily during sleep. There is no substitute or workaround.

  • Recommendation: 7-9+ hours, consistently
  • Priority: Non-negotiable

Nutrition Strong Evidence

Adequate protein (1.6-2.2g/kg) and sufficient calories fuel recovery and adaptation. Undereating slows recovery more than any modality can accelerate it.

  • Recommendation: Protein distributed across meals, adequate calories
  • Priority: Non-negotiable

Active Recovery Good Evidence

Light movement (walking, easy cycling, swimming) increases blood flow and can reduce perceived soreness without adding training stress. Better than complete inactivity.

  • Recommendation: 20-30 minutes low-intensity movement on rest days
  • Priority: Helpful, low cost

Foam Rolling/Self-Myofascial Release Moderate Evidence

May temporarily increase range of motion and reduce perception of soreness. Doesn't appear to accelerate actual tissue recovery. Most benefit is likely neurological (feels good, reduces tension).

  • Recommendation: Use if you enjoy it, 1-2 minutes per muscle group
  • Priority: Optional, low cost

Massage Moderate Evidence

Reduces perception of muscle soreness (DOMS). May have modest effects on inflammation markers. Primary benefit seems to be relaxation and parasympathetic activation. Expensive for regular use.

  • Recommendation: Use occasionally if budget allows, especially during high-stress periods
  • Priority: Nice to have, not essential

Cold Water Immersion/Ice Baths It Depends

Reduces inflammation and may speed recovery from high-intensity exercise. BUT: inflammation is part of adaptation. Regular cold exposure after strength training may blunt hypertrophy gains. Use strategically.

  • Best use: Between competitions or when recovery > adaptation (in-season)
  • Avoid: Immediately after hypertrophy-focused training
  • Priority: Context-dependent

Contrast Therapy (Hot/Cold) Limited Evidence

Alternating hot and cold may enhance blood flow. Evidence is mixed and not compelling for strength athletes. If you enjoy it, fine—but don't expect magic.

  • Recommendation: Low priority, use if enjoyable
  • Priority: Optional

Compression Garments Moderate Evidence

May reduce perceived soreness and swelling. Effects are modest. Some athletes find them comfortable during travel or sleeping.

  • Recommendation: Try them, see if you feel a difference
  • Priority: Low—nice to have

Pneumatic Compression (NormaTec, etc.) Moderate Evidence

May enhance blood flow and reduce perception of fatigue. Expensive devices. Evidence is similar to regular compression—modest benefits. Popular with athletes who can afford it.

  • Recommendation: Luxury item, not necessary
  • Priority: Low unless money is no object

Sauna Moderate Evidence

May improve cardiovascular health markers and promote relaxation. Some evidence for improved heat tolerance. Not specifically recovery-enhancing but general health benefits. Dehydration risk if not managed.

  • Recommendation: Use for relaxation, rehydrate well after
  • Priority: Optional, health benefits beyond recovery

Stretching It Depends

Static stretching does NOT reduce injury risk or speed recovery. It may temporarily reduce power output if done before lifting. Can be useful for maintaining mobility or relaxation.

  • Recommendation: Use for mobility maintenance, not recovery
  • Timing: After training or separate from lifting
  • Priority: Low for recovery specifically

Cryotherapy Chambers Limited Evidence

Very expensive, very short exposure. Evidence does not show superiority over cold water immersion. May be more about marketing than effectiveness.

  • Recommendation: Save your money
  • Priority: Low—ice bath is cheaper and equally effective

Electrical Stimulation (TENS, EMS) Limited Evidence

May provide temporary pain relief. Does not appear to enhance actual tissue recovery or adaptation. Not harmful, but not particularly helpful either.

  • Recommendation: Use for pain management if it helps
  • Priority: Low for recovery purposes

The Recovery Hierarchy

Prioritize in this order:

  1. Sleep: 7-9+ hours, consistent schedule
  2. Nutrition: Adequate protein and calories
  3. Programming: Smart training with built-in recovery
  4. Stress management: Life stress impairs recovery
  5. Active recovery: Light movement on off days
  6. Everything else: Marginal benefits after the above are optimized
The 1% Gains

Most recovery modalities beyond the fundamentals provide 1-3% benefits at best. That matters for elite athletes competing at the highest levels. For most of us, sleep and food matter more. Don't neglect the basics while chasing marginal gains.

When Extra Modalities Make Sense

  • Competition phase: Recovery between events matters more than adaptation
  • Very high training loads: Elite athletes may benefit from extra recovery support
  • Travel and stress: Massage, compression can help manage lifestyle factors
  • Psychological benefit: If a modality helps you relax, that has value

What to Skip

  • Expensive gadgets that promise miracle recovery
  • Any modality that replaces sleep or nutrition
  • Treatments with no evidence and high cost
  • Anything that takes time away from actual recovery (sleep, relaxation)
The Bottom Line

Sleep, food, and smart programming are 90%+ of recovery. Everything else is marginal. If the fundamentals are in place and you have extra budget/time, foam rolling, massage, and active recovery are reasonable additions. Cold exposure is context-dependent. Most expensive gadgets and therapies are not worth it for recreational lifters.
