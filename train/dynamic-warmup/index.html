Dynamic Warm-Up for Strength Training

A complete protocol to prepare your body for heavy lifting.

8 min read Updated Dec 2024

Skipping your warm-up is leaving strength on the platform. A proper dynamic warm-up increases core temperature, activates key muscle groups, and prepares your nervous system for maximal effort. Here's a complete 10-15 minute protocol.

Why Dynamic Over Static

Static stretching before lifting can temporarily reduce power output and strength. Dynamic warm-ups actively prepare your body through movement:

  • Increases heart rate and blood flow to muscles
  • Raises tissue temperature for better contractile function
  • Activates the nervous system for faster motor recruitment
  • Takes joints through full ROM under active control
  • Mentally prepares you for the training session
Save Static Stretching for After

Static stretching has value for flexibility work, but do it after training or on separate days. Pre-workout, keep everything moving.

Progress from general to specific as you approach your working sets

Movement-Specific Prep
2-3 minutes

Practice the movement patterns you're about to load. Use just bodyweight or an empty bar.

Bar Warm-Up Sets

After your dynamic warm-up, progressively build to your working weight. This is NOT part of your working sets.

Sample Bar Warm-Up for 100kg Working Sets

Empty bar (20kg) × 10
40kg × 8
60kg × 5
80kg × 3
90kg × 1-2
Then begin working sets at 100kg

General guidelines:

  • Start with an empty bar (or lighter variation of the movement)
  • Take 3-5 progressively heavier warm-up sets
  • Decrease reps as weight increases
  • Rest 1-2 minutes between warm-up sets near working weight
  • Don't fatigue yourself—these are activation sets, not work sets
Source: Calgary Barbell - Warming Up for Heavy Lifts

Putting It Together

Complete warm-up timeline: 10-15 minutes before touching working weights

Common Mistakes

  • Rushing through it: A 2-minute warm-up isn't enough. Take the time.
  • Static stretching pre-workout: Save it for after training.
  • Same warm-up every day: Adjust based on what you're training.
  • Skipping activation: Glutes and core need to be awake before loading.
  • Jumping to heavy weights: Build up progressively in bar work.
The Bottom Line

A proper warm-up takes 10-15 minutes but adds years to your lifting career. Your joints will thank you, your numbers will improve, and you'll feel more prepared for every session. Don't skip it.
