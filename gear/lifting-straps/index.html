Lifting Straps Guide

When and how to use straps without compromising grip development.

8 min read Updated Dec 2024

Lifting straps are one of the most debated pieces of equipment. Some say they're a crutch that weakens grip. Others say they're essential for training longevity. The truth? They're a tool—and like any tool, effectiveness depends on how you use it.

What Are Lifting Straps?

Straps are pieces of fabric or leather that wrap around your wrist and the bar, creating a secure connection that doesn't depend on grip strength. They allow you to hold more weight than your hands can grip on their own.

Basic strap wrapping technique: loop, feed under, wrap around, grip

Types of Straps

Lasso/Loop Straps

The most common type. A loop on one end goes around your wrist, then the strap wraps around the bar.

+ Versatile
+ Easy to find
+ Secure
- Takes time to set up
- Can't quick-release

Figure-8 Straps

Pre-formed figure-8 shape. Wrist goes through one loop, bar through the other. Common in strongman.

+ Super secure
+ Fast to set up
+ Best for max pulls
- Can't bail easily
- Only for deadlifts

Olympic/Speed Straps

Single loop design. Simpler and faster than lasso straps. Popular with Olympic lifters.

+ Quick to use
+ Easy to release
+ Works for cleans
- Less secure
- May slip at high weights
Figure-8 straps offer maximum security; Olympic straps allow quick release

When to Use Straps

Good Uses

  • Accessory work: Rows, RDLs, shrugs, lat pulldowns—grip shouldn't limit these
  • High-rep pulling: When grip fatigues before target muscles
  • Touch-and-go deadlifts: Multiple reps without resetting grip
  • Olympic lifting: Snatch pulls, clean pulls, hang work
  • When injured: Callus tears, finger injuries, wrist issues
  • Extremely heavy singles: When you need to focus on form, not grip

When NOT to Use Straps

  • Competition deadlifts: Straps aren't allowed in powerlifting
  • Every pulling exercise: Your grip needs work too
  • Weights you can easily grip: Save straps for when you need them
  • As an excuse: Don't avoid grip training entirely
The 80/20 Rule

Do 80% of your pulling work strapless to build grip. Use straps for the 20% where grip is the limiting factor and you want to overload the target muscles. This builds both grip strength and overall pulling strength.

Will Straps Weaken My Grip?

Only if you use them for everything. The research shows:

  • Using straps sometimes has no negative effect on grip strength
  • Using straps exclusively can reduce grip development
  • Dedicated grip training overcomes any potential negative effect

The solution: Use straps strategically while also training grip directly.

Source: Calgary Barbell - Strap Technique

How to Wrap Straps Properly

Lasso Strap Method

  1. Put your hand through the loop so it sits on your wrist
  2. Position the strap so it hangs from the thumb-side of your wrist
  3. Feed the strap under the bar from inside (toward you) to outside
  4. Wrap the strap around the bar 1-3 times
  5. Grip the bar AND the wrapped strap together
  6. Roll the bar toward you to tighten before lifting

Figure-8 Method

  1. Put your wrist through one loop
  2. Reach through the second loop to grab the bar
  3. The bar is now "locked" into the figure-8
  4. Adjust so the strap is tight against your wrist
Source: Strength Side - Figure-8 Strap Setup

Strap Materials

Material Pros Cons
Cotton Soft, comfortable, cheap Wears out faster, can stretch
Nylon Durable, grips well on knurling Can be stiff, less comfortable
Leather Very durable, breaks in nicely Expensive, stiff when new
Canvas Extremely durable, great grip Rough on hands, less flexible

Competition Rules

  • Powerlifting (IPF, USAPL, USPA): Straps NOT allowed for deadlift
  • Olympic Weightlifting: Straps NOT allowed in competition
  • Strongman: Straps ALLOWED for deadlift events (federation dependent)
  • CrossFit: Typically allowed in training, varies by competition
Training for Competition

If you compete in powerlifting, do most deadlift work without straps. Use straps for accessory pulling and high-volume work. Your competition grip (double overhand, mixed, or hook) needs practice to be reliable on meet day.

Recommended Progression

  1. Start strapless: Build base grip strength first
  2. Add straps for accessories: Rows, RDLs, shrugs
  3. Use for high-rep deadlifts: When doing 8+ reps
  4. Save strapless work for: Competition-style pulling, heavy singles
  5. Train grip separately: Dead hangs, farmer carries, plate pinches

Summary

  • Straps are a tool, not a crutch—use them strategically
  • Best for accessory work and high-rep pulling
  • Do most competition deadlift work strapless
  • Different types for different uses (lasso, figure-8, Olympic)
  • Won't hurt grip if you also train grip directly
