Post-Competition Training

Transition from peak back to productive training.

11 min read Updated Dec 2024

You just competed. Now what? The post-meet period is crucial for recovery, reflection, and setting up your next training block. Rush back too fast and you risk injury and burnout. Wait too long and you lose momentum.

The Post-Meet Timeline

Typical timeline; adjust based on meet difficulty and individual recovery

Week 1: Complete Rest

What to Do

  • No barbell work: Your joints, tendons, and CNS need recovery
  • Light movement only: Walking, swimming, yoga if it feels good
  • Eat freely: Replenish after any water/weight cut, enjoy food without tracking
  • Sleep extra: Aim for 9+ hours; naps are great
  • Stay off social media: Don't compare to others training; you just peaked
The Post-Meet Blues

Many lifters feel a "letdown" after competing. You've been building toward this for months, and now it's over. This is normal. Don't try to fix it by jumping back into hard training. Let yourself decompress.

Week 2: Light Movement

Ease Back In

  • Very light barbell work: 50-60% for sets of 8-10, no grinding
  • Focus on feel: Move through ranges of motion, don't chase numbers
  • Address any aches: If something hurt at the meet, get it looked at
  • Bodybuilding-style training: Machines, isolation work, pump training
  • RPE cap: Nothing above RPE 6

Weeks 3-4: Rebuild Volume

Transition Phase

  • Reintroduce compounds: SBD at 65-75%, moderate sets and reps
  • Build volume gradually: Add 1-2 sets per session each week
  • Technique focus: This is a great time to work on weak points
  • Introduce variations: Pause work, tempo work, close stance/grip
  • No maxing: Stay submaximal, RPE 6-7

Week 5+: New Training Block

By week 5, you should be ready to start a new structured training block. Your body has recovered, and you're ready to build toward your next goal.

Choose based on your competition schedule and goals

Mental Reset

Reflect on the Meet

  • What went well? Celebrate the wins, even small ones
  • What needs work? Identify 1-2 specific areas to improve
  • Attempt selection: Were your openers right? Did you leave kilos on the platform?
  • Meet day execution: Nutrition, warm-ups, timing—what would you change?

Set New Goals

  • Next meet: When and where? Have something on the calendar
  • Number goals: Specific weight targets for each lift
  • Process goals: Technical improvements, consistency targets
  • Timeline: Work backward from meet date to plan training blocks

Common Post-Meet Mistakes

What NOT to Do

  • Testing maxes too soon: You just maxed out. You're not stronger yet. Wait.
  • Jumping into heavy training: Your CNS and joints need recovery time
  • Ignoring injuries: That "minor" tweak from meet day can become chronic
  • No time off: Even 3-4 days completely away from the gym helps
  • Immediately cutting weight: Let your body normalize before any deficit
  • Comparing to meet day: Those were peak numbers. Training should feel harder now.

If the Meet Went Poorly

Bad meets happen. Missed lifts, underwhelming total, failed attempts—it stings. Here's how to handle it:

  1. Take extra rest: Don't try to "make up for it" by training harder immediately
  2. Analyze objectively: What actually went wrong? Technique? Peaking? Nerves?
  3. Separate performance from preparation: Was training good but meet day bad? That's fixable.
  4. Get perspective: One meet doesn't define you. It's data, not destiny.
  5. Make changes: If something in your prep wasn't working, now's the time to adjust
The Bottom Line

The post-meet period sets up your next training cycle. Take a real break (week 1), ease back in gradually (weeks 2-4), then launch into a structured block. Reflect on the meet, set new goals, and resist the urge to test maxes before you've built new strength. The meet was the harvest—now it's time to plant again.
