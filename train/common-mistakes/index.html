Common Lifting Mistakes

Visual guide to form errors and how to fix them.

15 min read Updated Dec 2024

Even experienced lifters make form errors under fatigue or heavy load. Here are the most common mistakes in each lift, what causes them, and how to fix them.

Squat Mistakes

Knee Cave (Valgus Collapse)

What it looks like: Knees collapse inward during the ascent, especially out of the hole.

Why it happens: Weak glutes/hip abductors, poor motor control, weight too heavy, or stance issues.

How to Fix It

  • Cue "knees out" or "spread the floor"
  • Use a light band around knees during warm-ups
  • Strengthen glutes with banded walks, clamshells
  • Reduce weight until you can maintain position
Good Morning Squat (Excessive Forward Lean)

What it looks like: Hips shoot up faster than the chest, causing excessive forward lean—like turning a squat into a good morning.

Why it happens: Weak quads relative to posterior chain, poor bracing, or bar position too high.

How to Fix It

  • Cue "chest up" or "lead with the chest"
  • Strengthen quads with front squats, leg press
  • Focus on bracing hard before descent
  • Try lower bar position if high bar

Bench Press Mistakes

Excessive Elbow Flare

What it looks like: Elbows flare out to 90° from the torso, forming a T-shape.

Why it's bad: Puts excessive stress on the shoulder joint, increases injury risk, and is mechanically inefficient.

How to Fix It

  • Cue "tuck elbows" or "break the bar"
  • Touch bar lower on chest (below nipple line)
  • Think of rowing the bar to your chest
  • 45-75° angle is ideal for most lifters
Bouncing Off Chest

What it looks like: Bar crashes into chest and bounces back up using momentum rather than muscle.

Why it's bad: Reduces muscle stimulus, risks rib/sternum injury, and won't pass in competition (which requires a pause).

How to Fix It

  • Practice pause bench on every rep (1-2 sec pause)
  • Control the descent—don't dive bomb
  • Think "touch and press," not "bounce and throw"
  • Use lighter weight until you can control it

Deadlift Mistakes

Rounded Lower Back

What it looks like: Lower back rounds into flexion, creating a C-curve in the lumbar spine.

Why it's bad: Places shear stress on spinal discs, increases injury risk significantly.

How to Fix It

  • Set up with chest proud, "show the logo on your shirt"
  • Brace hard before pulling—fill the cylinder
  • Hip hinge mobility work if you can't get into position
  • Reduce weight until you can maintain neutral spine
Hitching / Ramping

What it looks like: Bar stalls mid-thigh, lifter rests bar on thighs and uses knee re-bend to hitch it up, or "ramps" the bar up the legs.

Why it's bad: Illegal in competition (red light), indicates the weight is too heavy, and develops bad habits.

How to Fix It

  • If you can't lock out, the weight is too heavy—period
  • Practice block/rack pulls from sticking point
  • Strengthen glutes and upper back for lockout
  • Keep bar close but don't rest on thighs
Source: Juggernaut Training Systems - Deadlift Technique Errors

Universal Mistakes

Not Bracing Properly

The most common mistake across all lifts. Proper bracing (360° core pressure) is the foundation of safe, strong lifting. If you're not bracing, you're leaving pounds on the platform and risking injury. See our Bracing & Breathing Guide.

Ego Lifting

Adding weight before you've mastered the movement pattern is the fastest path to injury and stalled progress. Technique breaks down = weight is too heavy. Lower the weight, fix the form, then build back up. Your joints will thank you.
