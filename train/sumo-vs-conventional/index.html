Sumo vs Conventional Deadlift

14 min read • Technique

Two Legal Stances, Different Demands

Both sumo and conventional deadlifts are legal in powerlifting competition. Neither is "cheating"—they're simply different techniques that favor different body types and leverage advantages. The best stance for you depends on your proportions, mobility, and individual strengths.

Many lifters try both and settle on one, while others train both and compete with whichever is stronger that day. Understanding the differences helps you make an informed choice.

Top-down view showing the fundamental difference in foot and hand placement

Key Differences

Conventional

  • Feet hip-width apart
  • Hands grip outside legs
  • More hip hinge dominant
  • Greater range of motion
  • Higher back demands
  • Longer bar path
  • Stronger off the floor for most

Sumo

  • Feet wide (1.5-2x shoulder width)
  • Hands grip inside legs
  • More quad/hip dominant
  • Shorter range of motion
  • Higher hip mobility demands
  • More upright torso
  • Often harder off the floor, easier at lockout
Side view showing the difference in torso angle and range of motion

Who Should Pull Conventional?

Conventional deadlift tends to favor lifters with:

Body Type Indicators for Conventional

  • Long arms relative to torso: Natural leverage advantage
  • Shorter legs / longer torso: Better hip hinge position
  • Limited hip mobility: Less external rotation demand
  • Strong posterior chain: Back, glutes, hamstrings dominant
  • Wider hips: May feel cramped in sumo stance

Who Should Pull Sumo?

Sumo deadlift tends to favor lifters with:

Body Type Indicators for Sumo

  • Shorter arms relative to torso: Sumo reduces reach disadvantage
  • Longer legs / shorter torso: More upright position helps
  • Good hip mobility: Can achieve wide stance with knees tracking
  • Strong quads and hip extensors: Sumo is more quad-involved
  • Narrower hips: Often allows better sumo positioning
Relative muscle emphasis differs significantly between stances

How to Test Which Works for You

The best way to determine your optimal stance is to train both seriously for a period of time. Here's a testing protocol:

8-Week Stance Testing Protocol

  • Weeks 1-2: Train both stances at moderate weights (70-75%), focus on technique
  • Weeks 3-4: Build to heavier singles (85-90%) in both stances
  • Week 5-6: Primary stance training with secondary stance as accessory
  • Week 7: Test day—work up to heavy singles in both stances, same session
  • Week 8: Evaluate results and choose primary competition stance

What to Evaluate

Factor What to Look For
Strength Which stance allows you to lift more weight?
Consistency Which stance feels more repeatable and stable?
Speed off floor Where does the bar break the floor faster?
Lockout Which stance has a smoother, stronger lockout?
Recovery Which stance leaves you less beat up?
Comfort Which feels more natural and sustainable?

Common Mistakes by Stance

Conventional Mistakes

Rounding the Lower Back

The more horizontal torso position puts significant stress on the lower back. If you can't maintain a neutral spine, the weight is too heavy or your positioning is off.

Hips Rising Too Fast

If your hips shoot up and the bar stays on the floor, you're turning the lift into a stiff-leg deadlift. Push through the floor and keep your chest up.

Sumo Mistakes

Knees Caving In

Sumo requires strong hip external rotation. If your knees collapse inward, you lose power and risk injury. Push knees out over toes throughout the lift.

Hips Too Far From Bar

Sumo works best with hips close to the bar. If you're reaching forward, you're losing the leverage advantage. Sit your hips down and forward toward the bar.

Can You Train Both?

Yes. Many lifters benefit from training both stances:

  • Primary/Secondary: Main stance for heavy work, alternate stance for accessory volume
  • Block Rotation: Alternate which stance is primary each training block
  • Competition Flexibility: Having both stances competition-ready gives you options

However, if you're new to deadlifting, pick one stance and get good at it before adding complexity. Mastery of one is better than mediocrity in both.

Key Takeaways

  • Neither is cheating: Both stances are legal and valid
  • Body type matters: Your proportions influence which works better
  • Test both seriously: Give each stance honest effort before deciding
  • Muscle emphasis differs: Conventional = more back; Sumo = more quads/adductors
  • ROM differs: Sumo has shorter range of motion for most lifters
  • Mobility requirements: Sumo demands more hip mobility
  • You can train both: But master one first
