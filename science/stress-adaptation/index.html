Training Science

General Adaptation Syndrome

How your body responds to training stress and why progressive overload works.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

Every time you train, you're applying stress that your body must adapt to. Understanding how this process works—and what happens when you get it wrong—is the foundation of effective programming.

Hans Selye's GAS Model

In the 1930s, endocrinologist Hans Selye discovered that organisms respond to any stress in a predictable pattern. He called this the General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS), and it applies directly to strength training.

The three phases of adaptation and what happens after supercompensation

The Three Phases

1. Alarm Phase

Immediately after training, performance drops. You're fatigued, muscles are damaged, nervous system is taxed. This is normal and necessary.

2. Resistance Phase

Your body adapts to the stress. Muscle repairs stronger, nervous system recovers, performance returns and exceeds baseline.

3. Supercompensation

Peak adaptation window. You're now stronger than before. Ideally, apply new stress here to drive further adaptation.

The Training Window

The goal of programming is to apply the next training stress during supercompensation—when you're at your strongest. Too early = accumulated fatigue. Too late = detraining back to baseline.

Supercompensation in Practice

Proper timing accumulates adaptations; poor timing accumulates fatigue

Factors That Affect Recovery

Recovery is Individual

How quickly you recover depends on many factors:

  • Training age: More experienced = faster recovery (better work capacity)
  • Chronological age: Older = potentially slower recovery
  • Sleep: 7-9+ hours = faster recovery
  • Nutrition: Adequate protein and calories = faster recovery
  • Life stress: Work, relationships, anxiety = slower recovery
  • Training volume/intensity: Heavier loads = longer recovery
  • Menstrual cycle phase: Some phases may affect recovery speed

Fitness-Fatigue Model

A more nuanced view of adaptation recognizes that training produces two effects: fitness gains and fatigue. Performance equals fitness minus fatigue.

Fatigue dissipates faster than fitness, creating the supercompensation window

Key Insight

Fatigue builds quickly but also dissipates quickly. Fitness builds slowly and dissipates slowly. This is why tapers work: you reduce training to shed fatigue, but fitness stays elevated, revealing peak performance.

Practical Applications

For Weekly Programming

  • Heavy day: Large stress, requires more recovery
  • Light day: Maintains fitness, allows recovery to continue
  • Rest days: Full recovery, fatigue dissipation
Vary training stress to allow recovery while maintaining training frequency

For Block Programming

  • Accumulation blocks: High volume, moderate intensity → builds fitness, accumulates fatigue
  • Intensification blocks: Moderate volume, high intensity → maintains fitness, manages fatigue
  • Realization blocks: Low volume, peak intensity → sheds fatigue, reveals fitness

Signs of Proper Adaptation

You're Adapting Well If:

  • Weight or reps are gradually increasing over weeks
  • RPE stays consistent as loads increase
  • You feel recovered by your next session
  • Sleep quality remains good
  • Appetite and mood are stable
  • Technique stays sharp as fatigue accumulates
Signs of Poor Adaptation (Overreaching)
  • Weights feel heavier despite rest
  • Persistent muscle soreness
  • Declining motivation
  • Sleep disruption
  • Increased resting heart rate
  • Mood changes, irritability
  • Stalled or declining performance

Managing Cumulative Fatigue

Fatigue doesn't just dissipate between sessions—it can accumulate across weeks if training stress consistently exceeds recovery capacity.

Strategic deloads shed accumulated fatigue before it becomes problematic

Applying This to Your Training

  1. Track your performance: Know if you're adapting or accumulating fatigue
  2. Autoregulate: Adjust training based on daily readiness, not just the program
  3. Plan deloads: Every 4-6 weeks, reduce volume/intensity to recover
  4. Respect recovery: Sleep, nutrition, and stress management are training
  5. Periodize intelligently: Vary stress across weeks and blocks
  6. Peak strategically: Shed fatigue before competition to reveal fitness
The Bottom Line

Training is stress. Adaptation is the response to stress. Your job is to apply enough stress to drive adaptation, then recover enough to realize those adaptations. Too much stress without recovery = overtraining. Too little stress = no adaptation. Find the balance through smart programming and listening to your body.
