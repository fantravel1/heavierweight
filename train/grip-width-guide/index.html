Grip Width Guide

Optimize your hand position for squat, bench, and deadlift.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

Grip width affects leverage, muscle activation, and injury risk. A few centimeters can make a significant difference in your lifting. Here's how to find your optimal position for each of the big three.

Squat Grip

For squats, grip width primarily affects upper back tightness and shoulder mobility demands. The goal is to create a solid shelf for the bar while keeping the upper back tight.

Squat grip width comparison: hands relative to knurl rings (81cm marks)

Finding Your Squat Grip

  • Start wide: Begin with hands near the rings if you're new or have limited mobility
  • Gradually narrow: Over time, work hands in as mobility improves
  • Check wrist position: Wrists should be neutral, not bent back under the bar
  • Feel the shelf: A tighter grip creates a better "meat shelf" for the bar
Wrist Pain Solution

If squats hurt your wrists, you're likely supporting bar weight with your hands. The bar should rest on your back, not your wrists. Widen your grip, point elbows down more, or work on thoracic mobility.

Bench Press Grip

Bench grip width has the biggest impact on ROM, muscle activation, and competition legality. The IPF allows a maximum of 81cm between index fingers (to the knurl rings).

Narrower grip increases ROM but shifts emphasis to triceps
Source: Calgary Barbell - Bench Grip Width

The 45¬∞ Rule

A common guideline: at the bottom of the bench, your forearms should be roughly vertical (perpendicular to the floor). This positions the bar directly over your wrists and elbows, maximizing force transfer.

At the bottom of the press, forearms should be roughly vertical

Longer Arms

Typically benefit from wider grip to keep forearms vertical and reduce ROM.

Shorter Arms

Can use medium grip effectively. Wide grip may cause forearms to angle in.

Shoulder Issues

Narrower grip reduces shoulder stress. Prioritize health over competition advantage.

Deadlift Grip

Deadlift grip involves two decisions: width and style (double overhand, mixed, or hook). Each affects leverage, grip strength demands, and injury risk differently.

Top-down view: conventional places hands outside legs, sumo places hands inside

Grip Styles

Choose based on grip strength needs and injury risk tolerance
Source: Juggernaut Training Systems - Hook Grip Tutorial
Mixed Grip Safety

The supinated (underhand) arm in mixed grip has a small risk of bicep tear if you try to curl the bar during the pull. Keep the underhand arm completely straight and let it hang passively. Many lifters alternate which arm is under to prevent asymmetries.
