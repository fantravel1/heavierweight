The Conjugate Method

Max effort, dynamic effort, and repetition method for continuous progress.

14 min read Updated Dec 2024

The conjugate method—popularized by Westside Barbell—uses concurrent training of multiple strength qualities. Instead of focusing on one quality at a time, you develop max strength, speed-strength, and muscle simultaneously.

The Three Methods

All three methods are trained concurrently, each addressing different strength qualities

Max Effort Method

The Core Principle

Work up to the heaviest weight you can lift for 1-3 reps on a given exercise. The exercise rotates every 1-3 weeks to prevent accommodation. You're always training at or near maximal effort, but never with the same movement pattern for too long.

Max Effort Lower Body Exercises

  • Box squat (various heights)
  • Safety squat bar squat
  • Front squat
  • Deficit deadlift
  • Block pull (various heights)
  • Good morning variations
  • Sumo vs. conventional alternation

Max Effort Upper Body Exercises

  • Floor press
  • Board press (2-3 boards)
  • Close-grip bench
  • Incline press
  • Swiss bar press
  • Pause bench variations
  • Overhead press
Exercise Rotation

Rotate exercises every 1-3 weeks. When a lift stops improving or starts to regress, switch to a new variation. Keep a log of your max effort PRs to track progress across all variations.

Dynamic Effort Method

Dynamic effort develops rate of force development through speed work

Wave Loading for Dynamic Effort

Dynamic effort uses 3-week waves, cycling intensity:

  • Week 1: 50% bar weight + 25% band/chain tension
  • Week 2: 55% bar weight + 25% band/chain tension
  • Week 3: 60% bar weight + 25% band/chain tension

Then reset and repeat with new grip widths or stances.

Repetition Method

After max effort or dynamic effort work, you perform accessories using the repetition method. This builds muscle, addresses weak points, and accumulates volume without high nervous system stress.

Accessory Categories

  • Posterior chain: GHR, reverse hyper, back extensions, pull-throughs
  • Triceps: Extensions, dips, close-grip pressing
  • Upper back: Rows, face pulls, lat work
  • Anterior chain: Belt squat, leg press, lunges
  • Core/abs: Standing abs, weighted planks, carries

Accommodating Resistance

Bands and chains are central to conjugate training. They teach you to accelerate through the entire lift by increasing resistance as you approach lockout.

Both have value; bands typically for DE work, chains for either

Adapting Conjugate for Women

Key Considerations

  • Volume tolerance: Women often recover well from higher volumes—can add more accessory work
  • Frequency: Can often handle higher frequency than traditional Westside prescribed
  • Upper body emphasis: Many women benefit from extra upper body volume given typical strength ratios
  • Technique priority: Don't let max effort work degrade technique—some days, stop short of true 1RM
Max Effort Caution

True max effort work is fatiguing. Beginners and early intermediates may not need weekly max effort sessions to progress. Consider max effort every 2 weeks or limiting to singles at 90-95% rather than true maxes until technique is rock solid.

Who Should Use Conjugate?

Good Candidates

  • Intermediate to advanced lifters who have plateaued on linear programs
  • Geared (equipped) lifters—the system was designed for gear
  • Athletes who need to maintain strength year-round without peaking
  • Those who get bored with repetitive training

Not Ideal For

  • Beginners—still need to build baseline strength and technique
  • Those without access to specialty bars and bands/chains
  • Lifters preparing for raw competitions (may need more specificity)
  • Anyone unable to train 4 days per week consistently

Modified Conjugate for Raw Lifters

True Westside was designed for equipped lifting. For raw lifters, some modifications help:

  1. More competition lift practice – Include SBD specificity, not just variations
  2. Longer exercise rotations – 3 weeks instead of 1 week per variation
  3. Less band tension – Raw lifters don't need to overcome gear resistance
  4. Higher rep max efforts – 3-5RM instead of always 1RM
  5. Peaking phases – Add specificity blocks before competition
The Bottom Line

Conjugate is a powerful system for building well-rounded strength, but it requires equipment, experience, and the ability to train 4 days per week. It's not magic—the principles (variation, accommodating resistance, concurrent training) can be applied to any program. Take what works, leave what doesn't.
