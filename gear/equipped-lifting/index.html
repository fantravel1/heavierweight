Equipped Powerlifting Basics

Understanding squat suits, bench shirts, and deadlift suits.

14 min read Updated Dec 2024

Equipped powerlifting uses supportive gear—squat suits, bench shirts, and deadlift suits—to lift more weight than you could raw. It's a different sport with its own technique, strategy, and learning curve.

Raw vs. Equipped

Equipment rules vary by federation—always check specific rulebook

How Supportive Gear Works

Gear stores energy on the way down and releases it on the way up

Squat Suits

How They Work

Squat suits are made of thick, rigid material (canvas, polyester, denim blends) that resists hip flexion. As you descend, the suit stretches and stores energy, then helps drive you back up. The tighter the suit, the more support—but also harder to hit depth.

Single-Ply vs. Multi-Ply

Single-Ply
  • One layer of material
  • Adds ~50-100lbs typically
  • Easier to learn
  • More federations allow
  • Less extreme technique changes
Multi-Ply
  • 2-3+ layers of material
  • Adds 100-200+ lbs
  • Steeper learning curve
  • Fewer federations
  • Significant technique changes

Squat Suit Technique Differences

  • Wider stance: Most equipped squatters use a wider stance to load the suit
  • Sit back more: Load the hips to stretch the suit
  • Fight to depth: The suit resists—you must force yourself down
  • Stay tight: Loose form = suit doesn't work properly

Bench Shirts

How They Work

Bench shirts have extremely tight sleeves and a rigid chest panel. The material resists as you lower the bar, storing energy. The shirt provides massive support off the chest but offers less help as you approach lockout.

Shirts provide maximum assistance off the chest, diminishing toward lockout

Bench Shirt Technique

  • Touch point changes: Often touch lower than raw, sometimes on belly
  • Tuck elbows hard: Keep elbows tucked to load the shirt
  • Control the descent: Fight the shirt down—don't let it throw you
  • Heave off chest: Explosive drive to use stored energy
  • Flare to lockout: Elbows flare as shirt assistance fades
Bench Shirts Are Difficult

Bench shirts have the steepest learning curve of any equipment. Expect months of practice before you can touch the bar to your chest consistently. Many lifters need training partners to help get the shirt on and positioned.

Deadlift Suits

How They Work

Deadlift suits support the hips at the start of the pull. They're generally less supportive than squat suits (since there's no eccentric stretch) but still provide significant hip support off the floor. Some lifters use squat suits for deadlifts.

Conventional vs. Sumo in Gear

  • Conventional: Suit helps lock hips, may limit getting into position
  • Sumo: Suits can make getting wide and low very difficult—suit fights the position
  • Strategy: Some equipped lifters switch stances from their raw preference

Knee Wraps

How They Work

Knee wraps are long elastic wraps wound tightly around the knee. Like suits, they store energy during descent and release it during ascent. They can add 20-50+ lbs to your squat. Most equipped federations allow 2-2.5m wraps.

Wrapping Techniques

  • Spiral wrap: Each pass overlaps the previous—consistent support
  • X-pattern: Crosses behind the knee—more pop but can slip
  • Hybrid: Combination for maximum support
  • Tightness: Tighter = more support but harder to hit depth and more painful

Getting Started in Equipped

Progress through equipment systematically—don't skip steps

Finding Equipment

Popular Brands

  • Titan: Wide range, good for beginners and elite
  • Inzer: Classic brand, durable and proven
  • Metal: IPF-approved options
  • SBD: Modern options, IPF-approved

Sizing Considerations

  • Measure carefully: Follow brand-specific sizing charts exactly
  • Start looser: Your first suit should be workable, not competition-tight
  • Break-in period: New gear needs 2-4 weeks of training to break in
  • Buy used first: Try used gear to find what works before investing
The Bottom Line

Equipped lifting is essentially a different sport from raw. The gear allows heavier weights but requires completely different technique and months of practice. Build a solid raw base first, find experienced equipped lifters to learn from, and be patient—mastering gear takes years, not weeks.
