Getting Started

Your First Powerlifting Meet

Everything you need to know before stepping on the platform for the first time.

20 min read Updated Dec 2024

You've been training. You've been getting stronger. And now you're thinking about competing. Good. Powerlifting meets are where you test yourself against the barbell—and they're more fun than you might expect.

This guide covers everything you need to know for your first meet: choosing a federation, understanding equipment rules, planning attempts, and knowing what to expect on the day.

When Are You Ready?

There's no perfect time to do your first meet. If you can squat, bench, and deadlift with reasonable technique, you're ready. You don't need to hit specific numbers—the meet is about executing your best lifts on the day, not winning.

The "Ready" Mindset

Most lifters wish they'd done their first meet sooner. The experience teaches you things about yourself that training alone never will. Don't wait until you're "strong enough"—you're strong enough now.

Choosing a Federation

Powerlifting has multiple federations with different rules, equipment standards, and testing policies. Here are the main ones:

IPF/USAPL (USA)

  • Drug-tested, strict equipment rules
  • High competition standards
  • Path to national/international competition
  • Most common for serious competitors

USPA

  • Both tested and untested divisions
  • More equipment flexibility
  • Generally more accessible meet scheduling

Other Federations

There are many others (RPS, APF, WRPF, etc.) with varying rules. For your first meet, pick whatever is most accessible in your area. You can always switch later.

Pick Your Weight Class

IPF women's weight classes: 43kg, 47kg, 52kg, 57kg, 63kg, 69kg, 76kg, 84kg, 84+kg

For your first meet, compete at your natural weight. Don't cut weight—it adds unnecessary stress and can hurt your performance. Just lift in whatever class you fall into.

Equipment You'll Need

Equipment rules vary by federation. Here's what's typically required/allowed:

Required

  • Singlet (powerlifting-specific, one-piece)
  • T-shirt for under singlet (some feds require, some don't)
  • Long socks for deadlift (to cover shins)
  • Flat-soled shoes or squat shoes

Allowed (Check Your Fed's Approved List)

  • Belt (10-13mm, lever or prong)
  • Wrist wraps (length varies by federation)
  • Knee sleeves (must be approved brand)
  • Chalk (provided at most meets)
Equipment Check

Most federations have an approved equipment list. Check before you buy. USAPL/IPF is most strict—only specific brands are allowed. Other feds are more flexible.

Understanding the Lifts

Competition lifts have specific commands and standards. You must wait for the command before moving.

Squat

  1. Unrack and wait for "Squat" command
  2. Squat to depth (hip crease below top of knee)
  3. Stand up and wait for "Rack" command

Bench Press

  1. Unrack (with or without liftoff)
  2. Wait for "Start" command
  3. Lower bar to chest with control
  4. Wait for "Press" command (bar must pause motionless)
  5. Press up until arms are locked
  6. Wait for "Rack" command

Deadlift

  1. Lift the bar (no start command)
  2. Stand fully erect with knees and hips locked
  3. Wait for "Down" command
  4. Lower with control (can't drop)

Planning Your Attempts

You get three attempts for each lift. Strategy matters.

First Attempt (Opener)

Pick something you can hit on your worst day. This should be roughly your 3-rep max or 90% of your gym max. You want to go 3/3 on openers to build confidence and get on the board.

Second Attempt

Small jump to a weight you've definitely hit before. Maybe 5-10kg increase depending on the lift.

Third Attempt

Go for a PR if seconds felt good. But be realistic—competition is different from the gym.

First Meet Goal: 9/9

Going 9 for 9 (making all nine attempts) teaches you more than going 3/9 trying for big PRs. You can always come back and lift more. Build the habit of hitting lifts.

Meet Day Timeline

Day Before

Pack your bag, check equipment, eat normal foods, get to bed early

Morning

Eat familiar breakfast, arrive 2 hours before your flight starts, check in, weigh in

Equipment Check

Have your equipment inspected by officials (required at most meets)

Rules Meeting

Attend the rules briefing—even if you know the rules

Warm Up

Start warming up when there are about 10-15 lifters before you

Lifting

Squat → Bench → Deadlift. You'll have 8-12 minutes between attempts.

What to Bring

Meet Day Bag

  • Singlet and t-shirt
  • Belt, wraps, sleeves
  • Multiple pairs of socks (deadlift socks)
  • Squat shoes and flat shoes
  • Food and water
  • Caffeine source if you use it
  • Foam roller / lacrosse ball
  • Phone charger
  • Attempt card (if not provided)
  • Cash for entry/membership fees

Common First-Meet Mistakes

  • Opening too heavy: Ego lifts don't help. Start conservative.
  • Cutting weight: Don't. Just don't.
  • Not practicing commands: Train with pause commands before the meet.
  • Skipping food: You need fuel. Eat between lifts.
  • Going alone: Bring a friend or handler if possible.
  • Overthinking: You've done these lifts thousands of times. Trust yourself.

After the Meet

Regardless of how it went, you just did something most people never will. Take time to reflect:

  • What went well?
  • What would you do differently?
  • What do you want to work on for next time?

Most lifters leave their first meet already planning their next one. That's normal. Welcome to the sport.

The Real Secret

Everyone's nervous at their first meet. The lifters around you are your community, not your competition. They'll cheer for you, help you warm up, and celebrate your lifts. Powerlifting is remarkably supportive. Embrace it.
