Mental Cues and Focus

Build your personal cue library and learn to access the right mental state for maximum performance.

Cues are short, punchy mental prompts that trigger the right movement patterns or mindset. The best cues are personal—they resonate with YOUR body and brain. This guide helps you build a cue library and use it effectively.

Types of Cues

Cues fall into three categories, each serving a different purpose. Most lifters benefit from a mix, but one type usually resonates most.

Research suggests external cues are most effective for force production, but use what works for YOU

The Focus Funnel

As you approach the bar, your focus should narrow from broad awareness to a single point of focus. Think of it as a funnel—wide at the top, tight at the bottom.

Narrow your focus as you approach—don't think about technique mid-lift

Cue Library by Lift

Squat Cues

Setup

"Big air, brace hard"

Lock in core pressure before unracking

Descent

"Sit back between heels"

Prevents forward drift, loads posterior chain

Hole

"Spread the floor"

External cue for knee tracking and hip activation

Drive

"Push the floor away"

External cue for leg drive—you move, floor stays

Sticking Point

"Chest up, hips through"

Prevents good-morning squat at mid-range

Lockout

"Stand tall"

Complete hip extension, show command

Bench Cues

Setup

"Shoulders back and down"

Set scapular retraction before unracking

Unrack

"Pull the bar out"

Maintain tightness, don't push and lose position

Descent

"Row it to chest"

Controlled eccentric, maintains lat engagement

Touch

"Load the spring"

Build tension at chest, don't sink

Press

"Rip the bar apart"

External cue for lat activation and elbow tuck

Lockout

"Punch the ceiling"

Drive through triceps to lockout

Deadlift Cues

Setup

"Wedge in"

Pull slack, create tension before lift

Brace

"Lock the lats"

Protect the bar from drifting forward

Break

"Push the floor away"

Leg drive, not back pull—floor moves, you don't

Knee Pass

"Hips to the bar"

Aggressive hip drive once bar clears knees

Lockout

"Squeeze glutes, stand tall"

Complete hip extension, shoulders back

Descent

"Hinge then drop"

Push hips back, then release—don't fight it

Arousal Management

Different lifters need different arousal levels. Some perform best calm and focused; others need to get amped. Know your optimal zone.

The inverted U: too little OR too much arousal hurts performance. Find your sweet spot.
Practice both directions—you need to be able to dial up OR down depending on situation
Source: Juggernaut Training Systems - Mental game for strength sports

Building Your Cue Library

Start with 2-3 cues per lift. Test them in training—if a cue helps, keep it. If it makes you overthink, drop it. Write your working cues on your attempt card so you have them on meet day. The best cue is the simplest one that triggers the right action.

Key Takeaways

  • External cues ("push the floor") typically outperform internal cues for force production
  • Narrow your focus as you approach the bar—don't overthink mid-lift
  • 2-3 cues per lift maximum—simpler is better
  • Know your optimal arousal level and have tools to adjust both directions
  • Build a consistent pre-lift routine and practice it in training
  • The best cue is personal—test and refine your own library
