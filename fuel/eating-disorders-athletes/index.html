Disordered Eating and Athletes

Recognizing warning signs and knowing when to seek help.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024
Important Note

This article is for awareness and education, not diagnosis or treatment. If you or someone you know is struggling with disordered eating, please seek help from a qualified healthcare provider who specializes in eating disorders and athletes.

Strength sports can be part of a healthy relationship with your body—or they can trigger or worsen disordered eating patterns. Understanding the difference between dedication and dysfunction is crucial.

The Risk in Strength Sports

Athletes have higher rates of disordered eating than the general population. Factors in strength sports that increase risk:

  • Weight classes: Pressure to make weight or move down
  • Body composition focus: Emphasis on muscle and leanness
  • Control culture: "Discipline" around food can become obsession
  • Performance justification: Unhealthy behaviors framed as "for the sport"
  • Social media: Comparison with curated images

This doesn't mean strength sports are inherently harmful—for many, they're the opposite. But awareness of the risks matters.

The Spectrum

Eating issues exist on a spectrum:

  • Healthy relationship: Flexible eating, food fuels training, no guilt or obsession
  • Disordered eating: Rigid rules, anxiety around food, occasional concerning behaviors
  • Clinical eating disorders: Anorexia, bulimia, binge eating disorder, ARFID, etc.

Even sub-clinical disordered eating can impair performance, health, and quality of life. You don't need a formal diagnosis to deserve help.

Warning Signs in Athletes

  • Rigid food rules that cause distress if broken
  • Obsessive calorie counting or macro tracking
  • Avoiding social situations involving food
  • Excessive exercise beyond training requirements
  • Training through injury or illness to "burn calories"
  • Extreme pre-competition weight cuts
  • Hiding eating habits from others
  • Self-worth tied entirely to weight or appearance
  • Binge eating followed by guilt and restriction
  • Menstrual irregularities (in women)
  • Declining performance despite increased training
  • Mood changes related to eating or not eating

RED-S: Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport

RED-S (formerly "Female Athlete Triad") occurs when energy intake doesn't meet training demands. Effects include:

  • Hormonal disruption (missed periods, low testosterone)
  • Bone density loss and stress fractures
  • Decreased immunity
  • Impaired recovery
  • Decreased training adaptation
  • Depression and irritability

This can happen from intentional restriction or unintentionally not eating enough for training demands. Either way, it's harmful and treatable.

When Weight Class Sports Become Dangerous

Weight cutting is part of some sports. But there's a line between reasonable and harmful:

Potentially Okay

  • Small water cut (2-5% bodyweight) with proper rehydration time
  • Competing at a weight you can maintain without extreme restriction
  • Gradual, moderate weight loss over extended periods

Warning Signs

  • Extreme restriction for extended periods to make weight
  • Cutting more than 5-8% bodyweight via water manipulation
  • Using laxatives, diuretics, or other dangerous methods
  • Cutting weight despite health consequences (missed periods, etc.)
  • Feeling like you "can't" compete at a natural weight
  • Weight cuts triggering or worsening disordered eating
A Note on Weight Classes

You don't have to compete in the lowest weight class possible. Many athletes perform better competing at a higher, more natural weight where they can fuel properly and train optimally. If weight cutting is damaging your health or relationship with food, consider moving up a class.

Getting Help

If You're Struggling

Seeking help is strength, not weakness. Disordered eating is treatable. Many athletes have recovered and gone on to thrive in their sport with a healthy relationship with food.

Steps to Take

  1. Acknowledge the problem: Denial is part of the disorder
  2. Talk to someone: A trusted friend, coach, or family member
  3. Seek professional help: Therapist and/or dietitian specializing in athletes and eating disorders
  4. Consider taking a break: Time away from sport may be necessary for recovery
  5. Be patient: Recovery takes time but is possible

Finding a Specialist

Look for dietitians with certifications like CEDS (Certified Eating Disorder Specialist) who also understand athletic populations.

For Coaches and Training Partners

If you notice warning signs in someone you train with:

  • Express concern privately: "I've noticed some changes and I'm worried about you"
  • Avoid commenting on weight or appearance
  • Don't police their eating: Let professionals handle treatment
  • Offer support: "I'm here if you want to talk"
  • Encourage professional help: Without ultimatums if possible
  • Educate yourself: Learn about eating disorders to better support

Building a Healthy Relationship

For those without current issues, or in recovery, here's what healthy looks like:

  • Food is fuel, not punishment or reward
  • Flexible eating—no "forbidden" foods
  • Eating based on hunger and energy needs
  • No extreme guilt after eating
  • Performance matters more than appearance
  • Rest days without compensatory restriction
  • Ability to eat socially without anxiety
  • Weight fluctuations don't cause spiraling
The Bottom Line

Strength sports should enhance your life, not destroy it. If your relationship with food is causing distress, affecting your health, or making you miserable, that's not an acceptable cost of sport. Help exists. Recovery is possible. You deserve to be both strong and well.
