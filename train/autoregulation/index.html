Autoregulation in Training

Adjust training in real-time based on how you're actually performing.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

Autoregulation means adjusting your training based on real-time feedback rather than rigidly following prescribed percentages. It accounts for the fact that your 80% today might feel like 75% or 85% depending on recovery, stress, and readiness.

Why Autoregulate?

Autoregulation ensures you hit the right stimulus regardless of daily fluctuations

Autoregulation Methods

1. RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)

RPE measures effort relative to your maximum on that day

2. RIR (Reps in Reserve)

RIR = Reps In Reserve

Same concept as RPE, just inverted. RIR 2 = RPE 8 (2 reps left in the tank). Some lifters find RIR more intuitive than RPE. Use whichever clicks for you.

3. Velocity-Based Training (VBT)

Objective Autoregulation

Bar speed doesn't lie. Use a velocity device to:

  • Stop sets when velocity drops below a threshold
  • Adjust load based on warm-up velocities
  • Estimate daily max without testing

4. APRE (Autoregulatory Progressive Resistance Exercise)

APRE adjusts load within the session based on performance

Implementing Autoregulation

RPE-Based Programming

RPE targets allow load to float with daily readiness while maintaining relative effort

Finding Your RPE 8

  1. Warm up normally: Bar, 40%, 60%, 75% (ish)
  2. First work set: Estimate where RPE 8 will be
  3. Evaluate: Was it actually RPE 8? Too easy? Too hard?
  4. Adjust: Add or remove weight for subsequent sets
  5. Track: Over time, your estimates improve

Common Mistakes

Autoregulation Pitfalls
  • Sandbagging: Always rating RPE lower than reality to stay comfortable
  • Ego lifting: Always pushing to RPE 10 because "hard = good"
  • Inconsistent standards: RPE 8 means different things on different days
  • Ignoring context: Using autoregulation to justify skipping hard work
  • No progression: Just doing "what feels right" without progressive overload

Calibrating Your RPE

Use multiple methods to develop accurate RPE perception

When to Override Autoregulation

Autoregulation is a tool, not a religion. Sometimes you should override the feedback:

Push Through When:
  • Warming up feels off but improves
  • Mental fatigue, not physical
  • Competition prep requires specificity
  • Pattern of always backing off
Back Off When:
  • Pain (not discomfort)
  • Technique is breaking down
  • Accumulated life stress
  • Poor sleep multiple nights

Autoregulation for Women

Menstrual Cycle Considerations

Some women experience significant strength and recovery variations across their cycle. Autoregulation handles this naturally—you don't need to plan different phases. Just train to RPE and let the load adjust. Track your cycle alongside training to identify personal patterns.

Building the Skill

  1. Start with back-off sets: After your main work, do one AMRAP to calibrate
  2. Video everything: Review bar speed and technique vs. your RPE rating
  3. Log honestly: Write true RPE, not what you wanted it to be
  4. Accept variance: Some days you're off. That's data, not failure
  5. Trust the process: It takes months to develop accurate perception
The Bottom Line

Autoregulation optimizes training by matching stress to recovery. It requires developing the skill of honest self-assessment, which takes time. Start by using RPE alongside percentage-based programming, then gradually shift to primarily autoregulated training as your perception improves.
