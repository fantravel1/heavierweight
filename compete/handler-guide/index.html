How to Be a Good Handler

Everything you need to know to help a lifter on meet day.

10 min read Updated Dec 2024

A good handler can be the difference between a great meet and a stressful one. Your job is to handle everything except the lifting—so your lifter can focus entirely on performing.

The Handler's Role

You're logistics, support, and calm presence all in one. Your lifter shouldn't have to think about:

  • What attempt to submit next
  • How much time until they lift
  • Whether they have water
  • What weight is on the warm-up bar
  • Anything except making the lift
The Best Handlers Are Invisible

Everything runs smoothly. The lifter never has to ask "what's next?" or worry about logistics. They just lift when you tell them it's time, and everything is handled.

Before the Meet

Know the Plan

  • Opening attempts for all three lifts
  • Planned second and third attempts (and contingencies)
  • When to adjust attempts and by how much
  • Warm-up protocol (weights, sets, timing)
  • What cues/reminders the lifter wants (if any)

Know the Rules

  • How attempt changes work in this federation
  • Time limits for attempt submissions
  • Where to submit attempts (table, app, etc.)
  • Warm-up room procedures
  • Whether handlers can be on the platform

Meet Day Tasks

At Weigh-In

  • Help carry gear if needed
  • Hold their spot in line
  • Have rehydration ready immediately after weigh-in
  • Know where to go for equipment check, rack height measurement

Pre-Competition

  • Find a good spot to set up gear
  • Keep track of schedule and flight times
  • Make sure lifter eats and drinks
  • Calculate warm-up start time
  • Scout the warm-up room (where are platforms, is it crowded?)

Warm-Up Management

This is one of your most important jobs. A well-managed warm-up sets up the entire session.

Warm-Up Duties

  • Secure a platform/rack in the warm-up room
  • Load all weights (lifter shouldn't touch plates)
  • Have chalk ready
  • Time rests between sets
  • Track what's happening on the competition platform
  • Tell them when to take each warm-up

Timing Warm-Ups

The goal: last warm-up finishes 2-3 attempts before they're called to lift.

  • Watch the flight ahead or the current flight
  • Count attempts remaining before your lifter
  • Work backwards to time warm-ups
  • Each attempt takes roughly 30-60 seconds on average
  • Better to be ready early than rushing

Don't Let Them Miss Their Lift

This is the cardinal sin of handling. If they're still warming up when they're called, that's on you. Track the competition platform constantly. Give yourself a buffer.

During Competition

Between Attempts

  • Get them water, food, or whatever they need
  • Submit the next attempt (you decide or they decide—know which)
  • Track how many attempts until they lift again
  • Keep them calm and focused
  • Handle any gear adjustments (belt tightness, etc.)

Attempt Changes

Know when and how to change attempts:

  • Most feds allow changes until the bar is loaded
  • Some feds use apps, others use paper cards
  • Know the process before it's urgent
  • If adjusting strategy, discuss with lifter between lifts

Calling Them to the Platform

  • "You're in the hole" (3 attempts out)
  • "You're on deck" (next lifter is on platform)
  • "You're up—let's go"
  • Walk them to the platform if needed
  • Give any final cues they've requested

Communication Style

Things to Say

  • "You're 5 attempts out, rest here"
  • "Take 95kg now, then one more at 105kg"
  • "Second attempt is in, 127.5kg, you're on deck"
  • "Nice lift, take a few minutes"

Things NOT to Say

  • "That looked heavy" (even if it did)
  • "I think you should go lighter" (unless asked)
  • "Everyone's watching" (creates pressure)
  • Unsolicited technique advice mid-meet
  • Your own nervous energy

Read Your Lifter

Some lifters want conversation between attempts. Others want silence. Some want hype; others want calm. Know what your lifter needs:

  • Ask before the meet: "How do you want me to be?"
  • Watch their body language
  • Match their energy, don't override it
  • Be calm even if they're nervous

When Things Go Wrong

Missed Lift

  • Stay calm—your reaction matters
  • "That's fine, two more chances" or just a nod
  • Discuss strategy calmly: repeat weight or adjust?
  • Don't analyze technique unless they ask
  • Focus forward, not backward

Red Lights

  • Know why if possible (ask head judge if unclear)
  • Communicate simply: "Depth" or "Soft lockout"
  • Help them adjust for the next attempt
  • Don't argue with judges or show frustration

Injury or Pain

  • Take it seriously—their health comes first
  • Support whatever decision they make
  • If they want to continue, help them modify if possible
  • Know that it's okay to bomb or withdraw

Handler Essentials Kit

  • Paper and pen (for tracking attempts)
  • Phone with attempt tracking app (if used)
  • Extra chalk
  • Water and snacks for yourself too
  • The lifter's attempt plan
  • Knowledge of their warm-up weights

If You're New to Handling

Don't be intimidated. Most handling is common sense plus paying attention. Key points:

  1. Watch the competition platform constantly during warm-ups
  2. Know the attempt plan before the meet
  3. Stay calm no matter what happens
  4. Ask other experienced handlers if you're unsure about something
  5. Your lifter will tell you if they need something different
The Bottom Line

Handle the logistics, match their energy, and stay calm. Your job is to make everything except the actual lifting as easy as possible. If they never have to worry about anything but their next lift, you're doing it right.
