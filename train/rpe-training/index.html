RPE Training for Powerlifters

13 min read • Training Methods

What Is RPE?

RPE stands for Rate of Perceived Exertion—a subjective scale used to gauge how difficult a set was based on how many reps you had left in the tank. Instead of programming exact percentages, RPE-based training lets you adjust intensity based on how you actually feel that day.

This approach, popularized by coach Mike Tuchscherer and his Reactive Training Systems (RTS), accounts for the reality that your strength fluctuates daily based on sleep, stress, nutrition, and recovery. A weight that's easy on Monday might grind you down on Friday.

RPE-based training adjusts load to match your daily readiness, ensuring consistent training stimulus

The RPE Scale

The RPE scale runs from 1 to 10, but for strength training we typically work between 6 and 10. Here's what each number means:

10
Maximal Effort

No reps left. You could not have done another rep even with a gun to your head. True failure.

9.5
Near Maximal

Maybe 1 more rep on a perfect day, but probably not. Very close to failure.

9
Very Hard

1 rep left in the tank. Last rep was a grind but you could do one more if you had to.

8.5
Hard

1-2 reps left. Definitely 1, maybe 2 more reps possible.

8
Challenging

2 reps left in the tank. Could definitely do 2 more with good form.

7.5
Moderate-Hard

2-3 reps left. Speed is starting to slow slightly.

7
Moderate

3 reps left. Weight moves well, could do 3 more reps.

6
Light-Moderate

4+ reps left. Warm-up territory. Good for speed work and technique.

RIR (Reps in Reserve) is the inverse of RPE: RPE 8 = 2 RIR = 2 reps left in the tank

Learning to Rate Accurately

Most beginners underestimate their RPE—calling sets "RPE 8" when they actually had 4+ reps left. Calibrating your internal sense takes time and practice.

Calibration Methods

Method 1: AMRAP Testing

Periodically take a set to true failure (RPE 10) with a given weight. Count actual reps, then work backwards. If you got 8 reps at RPE 10, then 6 reps would be RPE 8, 5 reps would be RPE 9, etc.

Method 2: Bar Speed Awareness

As sets get harder, bar speed slows. Learn to recognize when speed starts dropping—this usually indicates RPE 7-8. When bar speed slows significantly, you're at RPE 9+.

Method 3: Post-Set Analysis

After each set, honestly ask: "How many more reps could I have done with good form?" Write down your answer and track it over time. Review periodically to catch patterns of over or underestimating.

RPE and Rep Ranges

RPE correlates to roughly the same percentage of 1RM regardless of rep range. This is because a set of 8 at RPE 8 should be just as relatively hard as a set of 3 at RPE 8—both leave 2 reps in the tank.

RPE Approximate % 1RM (at various rep ranges)
10 100% @ 1, 95% @ 2, 92.5% @ 3, 90% @ 4, 87% @ 5...
9 96% @ 1, 92% @ 2, 89% @ 3, 86% @ 4, 84% @ 5...
8 92% @ 1, 89% @ 2, 86% @ 3, 84% @ 4, 81% @ 5...
7 89% @ 1, 86% @ 2, 84% @ 3, 81% @ 4, 79% @ 5...
6 86% @ 1, 84% @ 2, 81% @ 3, 79% @ 4, 76% @ 5...

Note: These are approximations. Individual variation is significant. Use these as starting points and adjust based on your actual performance.

Programming with RPE

Basic Structure

A typical RPE-based program prescribes reps and target RPE, then you select the weight:

Example Squat Session

  • Warm-up sets to RPE 6
  • Work up to top set: 4 reps @ RPE 8
  • Back-off sets: 4x4 @ RPE 7
  • Accessories: 3x10 @ RPE 7-8

Back-Off Sets

After your top set, back-off sets provide additional volume at a more sustainable intensity. Common approaches:

Percentage Drop

After top set at RPE 8-9, reduce weight by 5-10% for back-off sets at the same rep count. This usually lands around RPE 7.

Fatigue Percentage

RTS system uses "fatigue percentages." Example: "5 @ 8, -5% x 4 sets" means hit a top set of 5 at RPE 8, then do 4 more sets at 5% less weight.

Benefits of RPE Training

Auto-Regulation

Your program adapts to your daily readiness. Feeling great? You'll naturally use heavier weights. Tired or stressed? You'll use lighter weights. Either way, you get the intended training stimulus.

Sustainable Progression

Instead of blindly adding weight each week (which eventually fails), you progress when you're ready. A weight that was RPE 9 becomes RPE 8, signaling it's time to increase load.

Injury Prevention

By backing off on bad days instead of grinding through prescribed weights, you reduce injury risk. RPE respects your body's signals.

Common RPE Mistakes

Sandbagging

Consistently rating sets easier than they are to use lighter weights. Be honest. If you always end up at RPE 6 when you're supposed to hit RPE 8, you're sandbagging.

Ego Lifting

The opposite problem: rating sets as RPE 7 when they were clearly RPE 9. This leads to accumulated fatigue and eventual breakdown. Honesty is non-negotiable.

Set-to-Set Jumping

Making large weight jumps between sets trying to hit exact RPE. Small jumps (2.5-5kg) are more effective. You can't precisely hit RPE 8—anywhere from 7.5-8.5 is acceptable.

When to Use RPE vs. Percentages

Use RPE When... Use Percentages When...
You're experienced and can rate accurately You're new to lifting and still learning
Your recovery varies significantly Your schedule and recovery are consistent
You want flexibility in your training You want structure and simplicity
You're peaking and readiness fluctuates You're in a stable building phase
Training main lifts Training accessories (less precision needed)

Sample RPE-Based Week

Day 1: Squat

  • Squat: Work up to 4 @ RPE 8, then 4x4 @ RPE 7
  • Pause Squat: 3x5 @ RPE 7
  • Leg Press: 3x10 @ RPE 8

Day 2: Bench

  • Bench: Work up to 3 @ RPE 8, then 5x3 @ RPE 7
  • Close Grip Bench: 3x6 @ RPE 7
  • Rows: 4x8 @ RPE 7-8

Day 3: Deadlift

  • Deadlift: Work up to 3 @ RPE 8, then 3x3 @ RPE 7
  • RDL: 3x8 @ RPE 7
  • Back Extensions: 3x12 @ RPE 7

Key Takeaways

  • RPE = Reps in Reserve: RPE 8 means 2 reps left, RPE 9 means 1 rep left
  • Auto-regulation: RPE adjusts intensity to your daily readiness
  • Requires honesty: Accurate rating is essential—neither sandbagging nor ego lifting
  • Takes practice: Most lifters need 3-6 months to calibrate accurately
  • Most training at RPE 7-8: Reserve RPE 9-10 for testing and competition
  • Works with percentages: Use both systems together for optimal results
