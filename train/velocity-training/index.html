Velocity-Based Training

Use bar speed to autoregulate intensity and optimize training.

13 min read Updated Dec 2024

Velocity-based training (VBT) uses bar speed to objectively measure intensity and fatigue. Instead of guessing your RPE, you let the numbers tell you exactly how hard a set was.

Why Bar Speed Matters

As weight gets heavier relative to your max, the bar moves slower. This relationship is predictable and measurable, giving you an objective way to gauge intensity without testing maxes.

Heavier loads = slower bar speed. This relationship is consistent and predictable.

Minimum Velocity Threshold (MVT)

Your MVT is the slowest speed at which you can complete a lift. At true 1RM, most lifters hit 0.15-0.30 m/s on squat/deadlift and 0.10-0.20 m/s on bench. This is individual—tracking yours helps predict maxes.

Lift-Specific Velocities

General velocity ranges vary by lift due to range of motion and muscle involvement

VBT Devices

Linear Position Transducers (LPT)

The gold standard. A cable attaches to the bar and measures displacement. Examples: GymAware, Tendo Unit. Highly accurate but expensive ($500-2000+).

Accelerometer-Based Devices

Small units that attach to the bar and measure acceleration. Examples: PUSH Band, Beast Sensor, Vitruve. More affordable ($150-400), good accuracy for most purposes.

Camera/App-Based Systems

Use your phone camera to track bar movement. Examples: My Lift, WL Analysis, Iron Path. Free or cheap, accuracy varies. Good for learning concepts.

Practical Applications

1. Autoregulating Daily Intensity

Use warm-up velocities to adjust training load for the day

2. Velocity Stop Sets

Instead of prescribing sets and reps, prescribe a velocity cutoff. When bar speed drops below the threshold, the set ends. This autoregulates fatigue.

Example Protocol

  • Squat @ 75%: Continue reps until velocity drops below 0.50 m/s
  • Usually produces 4-6 reps depending on daily readiness
  • Stops you before grinding—better rep quality, less fatigue
  • Multiple sets with same cutoff accumulates quality volume

3. Estimating 1RM Without Testing

Predict maxes from submaximal work without grinding through true 1RMs

4. Velocity Loss for Fatigue Management

Track how much velocity drops across a set. More velocity loss = more fatigue. Use this to manage training stress:

  • 0-10% loss: Minimal fatigue, good for speed/power work
  • 10-20% loss: Moderate fatigue, good for strength development
  • 20-30% loss: High fatigue, hypertrophy focus
  • >30% loss: Very high fatigue, grinding reps (usually avoid)

VBT for Women

Considerations

  • Build your own profiles: Published velocity zones are often from male data—calibrate to yourself
  • Menstrual cycle effects: Some women show velocity variations across the cycle—track and learn your patterns
  • Fatigue tolerance: Women often tolerate higher velocity loss before technique breaks—but don't abuse this
  • Bench press: Women typically have lower absolute bench velocities due to shorter ROM—don't compare to men's data

Getting Started with VBT

  1. Choose a device: Start with a phone app if budget is tight, or invest in an accelerometer-based unit
  2. Build baseline data: Track velocities at various percentages for 4-6 weeks before making changes
  3. Find your MVT: When you do test maxes, record the velocity
  4. Start simple: Use velocity to guide warm-up load selection before using it for work sets
  5. Track trends: Day-to-day variation is normal; look at weekly/monthly patterns
The Bottom Line

VBT provides objective feedback that removes guesswork from training. It's not magic—it's measurement. Use it to autoregulate intensity, manage fatigue, and track readiness. Start by collecting data, then gradually integrate velocity targets into your programming.
