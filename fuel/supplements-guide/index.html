Supplements for Strength Athletes

What works, what doesn't, and where your money is best spent.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

The supplement industry is worth billions, and most of it is marketing. Here's an evidence-based look at what actually helps strength athletes—and what's a waste of money.

The Foundation First

Supplements are the 5% after you've nailed the 95%: consistent training, adequate protein, enough calories, good sleep. No supplement fixes a bad diet or inconsistent training.

Tier 1: Evidence-Based, Proven Effective

These supplements have strong research support for strength athletes:

Creatine Monohydrate

★★★★★ Strong Evidence

The most researched sports supplement. Increases strength, power, and muscle mass. Safe for long-term use. Works for most people.

  • Dose: 3-5g daily (no loading required)
  • When: Any time, consistency matters most
  • Form: Monohydrate is cheapest and most studied

Caffeine

★★★★★ Strong Evidence

Improves alertness, reduces perceived exertion, and may increase strength output. Most people already use it (coffee, tea, pre-workout).

  • Dose: 3-6mg/kg bodyweight (200-400mg for most)
  • When: 30-60 minutes before training
  • Note: Tolerance builds; cycle off occasionally

Protein Powder

★★★★★ Strong Evidence (for convenience)

Not magic—just a convenient protein source. Helpful when whole food intake falls short. Whey, casein, and plant-based options all work.

  • Dose: Whatever helps you hit protein targets
  • When: Any time; timing matters less than total intake
  • Type: Whey is most popular; plant-based works too

Tier 2: Useful for Some

These have evidence but are more situational:

Vitamin D

★★★★☆ Good Evidence (if deficient)

Many people are deficient, especially in winter or if you work indoors. Supports bone health, immune function, and possibly muscle function.

  • Dose: 2000-4000 IU daily (get levels tested)
  • When: With fat-containing meal for absorption
  • Note: Test levels; don't mega-dose blindly

Magnesium

★★★★☆ Good Evidence (if deficient)

Many athletes don't get enough from diet. Supports muscle and nerve function, sleep quality, and recovery.

  • Dose: 200-400mg daily
  • Form: Glycinate or citrate absorb well; oxide is cheap but less bioavailable
  • When: Evening (may help sleep)

Fish Oil (Omega-3)

★★★☆☆ Moderate Evidence

May reduce inflammation and support joint health. More relevant if you don't eat fatty fish regularly.

  • Dose: 2-3g EPA+DHA combined
  • Note: Quality matters; third-party tested brands
  • Alternative: Eat fatty fish 2-3x per week

Beta-Alanine

★★★☆☆ Moderate Evidence

May help with higher-rep sets (8+) by buffering acid buildup. Less relevant for pure strength work (1-5 reps).

  • Dose: 3-6g daily
  • Note: Causes tingling (harmless)
  • Best for: Higher-rep training, CrossFit

Tier 3: Probably Skip

Weak evidence, overhyped, or just not worth the money:

BCAAs

★★☆☆☆ Weak Evidence

If you're eating adequate protein, BCAAs add nothing. Complete proteins already contain BCAAs. Waste of money for most people.

Testosterone Boosters

★☆☆☆☆ No Evidence

None of the legal "test boosters" meaningfully increase testosterone. Save your money. If you have low T, see a doctor.

Fat Burners

★☆☆☆☆ No Evidence

Mostly caffeine with added marketing. The effect on fat loss is minimal to none. Caloric deficit is what matters.

Most Pre-Workout Blends

★★☆☆☆ Overpriced

Often underdosed on what works (creatine, caffeine) and full of things that don't. Coffee + creatine separately is cheaper and more effective.

Quick Reference

Worth Taking

Creatine monohydrate, Caffeine (if tolerated), Protein powder (if needed for convenience), Vitamin D (if deficient)

Consider Based on Individual Needs

Magnesium, Fish oil (if not eating fish), Beta-alanine (for higher-rep work)

Skip

BCAAs, Testosterone boosters, Fat burners, Most proprietary blends

Competition Considerations

If you compete in a drug-tested federation:

  • Some pre-workouts contain banned substances
  • Some "natural" supplements are contaminated
  • Use only third-party tested products (NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport)
  • Check USADA or WADA lists if unsure

Even legal supplements can cause positive tests if contaminated. Choose reputable brands.

Quality Matters

The supplement industry is poorly regulated. Look for:

  • Third-party testing: NSF, Informed Sport, or similar
  • Transparent labeling: No "proprietary blends" hiding doses
  • Reputable brands: Companies with track records
  • Avoid too-good-to-be-true claims: If it sounds like a steroid, it might be contaminated with one

Cost-Benefit Analysis

Before buying any supplement, ask:

  1. Is there evidence it works for my goal?
  2. Could I get this from food instead?
  3. Is the dose actually effective?
  4. Is it worth the cost compared to better food?

Often, spending that money on quality food, a gym membership, or coaching provides far better returns.

The Bottom Line

Creatine works. Caffeine works. Protein powder is convenient. Vitamin D and magnesium matter if you're deficient. Everything else is marginal at best. Fix your diet, training, and sleep before worrying about supplements.
