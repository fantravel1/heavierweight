Glossary

100+ strength training terms explained. From basic gym vocabulary to advanced programming concepts.

ABCDE FGHIJ KLMNO PQRST UVW
A

Accessory Work

Supplementary exercises that support main lifts. Examples: tricep extensions for bench press, Romanian deadlifts for conventional deadlift.

AMRAP

As Many Reps As Possible. A set performed to technical failure or a target RPE, used to gauge progress or autoregulate training.

Arch

The natural or exaggerated curve in the lower back during bench press. Creates a more stable pressing position and shortens range of motion.

Attempt

In competition, each lifter gets three attempts at each lift. The best successful attempt for each lift counts toward the total.

B

Belt

A thick leather or synthetic belt worn around the waist to increase intra-abdominal pressure and core stability. Typically 10mm or 13mm thick.

Block Pull

Deadlift variation where the bar starts on blocks or mats, reducing range of motion. Used to train lockout strength.

Brace

The act of creating tension in the core by taking a breath into the belly and tightening the abdominal muscles. Essential for heavy lifts.

Butt Wink

Posterior pelvic tilt at the bottom of a squat, causing the lower back to round. Some is normal; excessive butt wink may indicate mobility limitations.

C

Clean & Jerk

Olympic lift where the bar is pulled from floor to shoulders (clean) then pressed overhead (jerk). One of two competition lifts in Olympic weightlifting.

Compound Lift

Exercise that involves multiple joints and muscle groups. Examples: squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press.

Conventional Deadlift

Deadlift stance with feet hip-width apart and hands outside the legs. Emphasizes back and hamstrings.

Cut

Period of intentional fat loss, usually before a competition to make a weight class. Also called a "weight cut."

D

Deload

A planned period of reduced training intensity and/or volume to allow recovery. Typically one week every 4-8 weeks.

Deficit Deadlift

Deadlift performed while standing on plates or a platform, increasing range of motion. Builds strength off the floor.

DOMS

Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness. Muscle pain that peaks 24-72 hours after training. Normal, especially with new exercises.

Double Overhand Grip

Grip with both palms facing toward you. Most natural grip but limits deadlift weight due to grip strength.

E

Eccentric

The lowering or lengthening phase of a lift. The descent in a squat, lowering the bar in bench press, etc.

E1RM

Estimated One-Rep Max. Your predicted max based on submaximal performance. Various formulas exist (Epley, Brzycki, etc.).

F

Failure

Point at which you cannot complete another rep with good form. "Technical failure" vs "absolute failure."

Flight

In competition, a group of lifters who lift together. Flights are organized by weight class or entry total.

Front Squat

Squat with the bar held on the front of the shoulders. More quad-dominant and requires more mobility than back squat.

G

Good Morning

Hinge exercise with bar on back, bending at hips while keeping legs relatively straight. Builds posterior chain strength.

GPP

General Physical Preparedness. Overall fitness and work capacity. Contrast with SPP (Specific Physical Preparedness).

Grind

A slow, difficult rep where speed decreases significantly. "That was a grinder."

H

High Bar Squat

Squat with bar positioned on the traps, near the base of the neck. Creates a more upright torso position.

Hook Grip

Grip where the thumb wraps under the fingers. Secure like mixed grip but symmetric. Preferred by Olympic lifters and many powerlifters.

Hypertrophy

Muscle growth. Training for hypertrophy typically involves moderate weights, higher reps (8-15), and controlled tempo.

I

IPF

International Powerlifting Federation. The main international governing body for drug-tested powerlifting.

Isolation Exercise

Exercise targeting a single muscle group with movement at one joint. Examples: bicep curl, leg extension, tricep pushdown.

J

J-Hooks

The hooks on a squat rack or bench that hold the barbell. Adjustable for different lift heights.

K

Knee Sleeves

Neoprene sleeves worn around the knees for warmth, support, and proprioception. Competition-legal at specific thicknesses.

Knee Wraps

Elastic wraps wound tightly around the knees for extra support and rebound. Legal in "equipped" but not "classic/raw" powerlifting divisions.

L

Lat

Latissimus dorsi—the large back muscles. Critical for all three powerlifts and most pulling movements.

Leg Drive

Using the legs to create force in the bench press. Feet push into the floor to create tension through the body.

Lockout

The top position of a lift with joints fully extended. "Good lockout" means completing the lift with proper positioning.

Low Bar Squat

Squat with bar positioned on the rear delts, below the spine of the scapula. Allows more forward lean and typically more weight.

M

Meet

A powerlifting or weightlifting competition. "I have a meet in 12 weeks."

Mixed Grip

Deadlift grip with one palm facing forward, one facing back. Prevents bar roll but creates asymmetry.

MRV

Maximum Recoverable Volume. The highest amount of training you can do while still recovering. Individual and context-dependent.

N

Newbie Gains

Rapid strength and muscle increases in the first months of training. Enjoy them—they don't last forever.

O

Opener

First attempt in competition. Should be conservative—something you can hit on your worst day.

Overload

Training principle of progressively increasing demands on the body to stimulate adaptation.

P

Pause Rep

Rep with a deliberate pause at a specific point in the range of motion. Builds strength at that position.

Peak

The final phase of meet prep where volume decreases and intensity increases to maximize competition performance.

PR

Personal Record. Your best performance on a lift. Can be 1RM, rep PR, volume PR, etc.

Progressive Overload

Gradually increasing training demands over time—more weight, more reps, more sets, or better technique.

Q

Quad

Quadriceps—the four muscles on the front of the thigh. Primary movers in squats and leg press.

R

Raw

Lifting without supportive equipment (squat suits, bench shirts). Also called "classic" in IPF. Knee sleeves and belt are typically allowed.

Rep

One complete execution of an exercise. Short for "repetition."

RPE

Rate of Perceived Exertion. Scale from 1-10 measuring how hard a set felt. RPE 10 = maximum effort, RPE 8 = could do 2 more reps.

RIR

Reps In Reserve. How many more reps you could have done. RIR 2 = could have done 2 more reps = RPE 8.

S

Set

A group of consecutive reps. "3 sets of 5" = 3 groups of 5 reps each.

Snatch

Olympic lift where the bar is pulled from floor to overhead in one motion. Requires significant mobility and technique.

Spotter

Person who assists with a lift for safety, particularly on bench press and squat.

Sticking Point

The position in a lift where you're weakest and most likely to fail. Varies by individual.

Sumo Deadlift

Deadlift with wide stance and hands inside the legs. Shorter range of motion, more hip-dominant.

Superset

Two exercises performed back-to-back with no rest between them.

T

Tempo

The speed of each phase of a lift. Often written as 4 numbers (e.g., 3-1-2-0 = 3 sec down, 1 sec pause, 2 sec up, 0 sec at top).

Total

In powerlifting, the sum of your best squat, bench press, and deadlift. The number that determines your placing.

Training Max

A conservative estimate of your 1RM used for programming. Typically 85-90% of your true max.

U

Unrack

Taking the bar from the j-hooks to begin a lift.

V

Volume

Total amount of work done. Can be measured as sets × reps × weight, or simply total reps/sets.

Valsalva Maneuver

Breathing technique for heavy lifts: deep breath, hold, brace core, lift. Creates intra-abdominal pressure for stability.

W

Warm-Up Sets

Lighter sets performed before working sets to prepare the body and practice the movement pattern.

Wilks Score

Formula comparing lifters of different bodyweights. Higher Wilks = stronger relative to bodyweight. Being replaced by DOTS in IPF.

Working Set

Sets performed at target weight/intensity. The "real" sets as opposed to warm-ups.

Wrist Wraps

Supportive wraps worn around wrists for pressing movements. Provide stability and support.
