Pre-Lift Warm-Up Flow

10 min read • Training Preparation

The Purpose of Warming Up

A proper warm-up prepares your body for heavy lifting by increasing tissue temperature, improving joint mobility, activating key muscle groups, and priming your nervous system for maximal effort. Skip it, and you're leaving strength on the table while increasing injury risk.

The goal isn't to fatigue yourself before your work sets—it's to optimize your readiness. An effective warm-up takes 10-15 minutes and follows a specific progression from general to specific preparation.

The warm-up narrows from general to specific as you approach your working weights

Phase 1: General Preparation

2-5 minutes

Get your heart rate up and blood flowing to your muscles. This doesn't need to be complicated or intense—just enough to break a light sweat and feel warm.

1

Light Cardio

Choose low-impact options that don't tire your legs before squatting or deadlifting.

  • Rowing machine: 2-3 minutes at moderate pace
  • Stationary bike: 3-5 minutes easy spinning
  • Brisk walking on incline treadmill
  • Jumping jacks or light skipping (if joints tolerate)

Avoid on Leg Days

Skip running or aggressive stair climber before squats and deadlifts. These pre-fatigue your legs. Rowing or biking keeps legs fresh while still warming you up.

Phase 2: Movement Preparation

5-8 minutes

This phase addresses mobility restrictions and activates the muscles you'll need for your main lift. It's specific to the lift you're about to perform.

Each lift requires specific mobility, activation, and movement pattern work

Key Exercises by Focus Area

Hip Mobility (Squat & Deadlift)

  • 90/90 Hip Stretch: 30-60 sec each side, focus on breathing
  • Frog Stretch: Rock forward and back, 10-15 reps
  • Hip Flexor Stretch: Half-kneeling, 30 sec each side
  • Goblet Squat Hold: Use elbows to push knees out, 30 sec

Shoulder Mobility (Bench & Overhead)

  • Shoulder Dislocates: With band or PVC, 10-15 reps
  • Wall Slides: Arms against wall, slide up and down, 10 reps
  • Thread the Needle: T-spine rotation, 8 each side
  • Doorway Pec Stretch: 30 sec each arm, different angles
Source: Squat University - Complete Squat Warm-Up

Phase 3: Specific Preparation (Bar Work)

5-10 minutes

Now you're ready for the barbell. The goal is to progress from the empty bar to your working weight using calculated jumps that prepare your body without fatiguing you.

Sample warm-up progression to 140kg work sets—adjust percentages based on your max

Warm-Up Set Guidelines

The Rules

  • Start with the empty bar: Always. Use this to rehearse technique and assess how you feel
  • Take bigger jumps early, smaller jumps later: 30-40% jumps at first, then 10-15% closer to work weight
  • Reduce reps as weight increases: 8-10 with bar, down to 1-2 at 85%+
  • Focus on speed: Warm-up sets should feel fast and crisp
  • Rest adequately before work sets: 2-3 minutes after your heaviest warm-up

80-100kg Bar x10, 40kg x5, 60kg x3, 80kg x2
100-140kg Bar x10, 50kg x5, 80kg x3, 100kg x2, 120kg x1
140kg+ Bar x10, 60kg x5, 90kg x3, 110kg x2, 130kg x1, 140kg x1

Complete Flow Examples

SQUAT DAY
1

General Warm-Up (3 min)

Row machine, moderate pace

2

Hip & Ankle Mobility (3 min)

  • 90/90 hip stretch: 30 sec each side
  • Hip flexor stretch: 30 sec each side
  • Ankle dorsiflexion mobilization: 10 reps each
3

Activation (3 min)

  • Glute bridges: 2x10
  • Goblet squat hold: 30 sec
  • Banded clamshells: 1x10 each side
4

Bar Work (5-7 min)

  • Empty bar x 10 (focus on descent speed, bracing)
  • 60kg x 5
  • 90kg x 3
  • 110kg x 2
  • 130kg x 1
  • Rest 2-3 min → Work sets at 140kg
BENCH DAY
1

General Warm-Up (2 min)

Arm circles, jumping jacks, or light rowing

2

Shoulder Mobility (3 min)

  • Shoulder dislocates with band: 15 reps
  • Doorway pec stretch: 30 sec each arm
  • Thread the needle: 8 each side
3

Activation & Setup (3 min)

  • Band pull-aparts: 2x15
  • Push-ups: 10 (focus on scap retraction)
  • Practice arch and leg drive on bench
4

Bar Work (5 min)

  • Empty bar x 10 (practice pause, leg drive)
  • 40kg x 5
  • 60kg x 3
  • 75kg x 2
  • Rest 2 min → Work sets at 85kg

Common Warm-Up Mistakes

Too Much Static Stretching

Long static holds before lifting can temporarily reduce power output. Keep stretches brief (under 30 sec) or use dynamic alternatives. Save longer stretching for after training.

Not Enough Warm-Up Sets

Jumping from an empty bar to near-work weight is a recipe for poor performance and injury. Your body needs gradual progression to prime the nervous system for heavy loads.

Too Many Warm-Up Reps

Don't turn warm-ups into a workout. If you're doing 5x5 at 80% of your work weight, you're fatiguing yourself. Keep reps low as weight increases.

Adjusting for Cold Days & Stiffness

Some days you'll feel tighter or colder than usual. When this happens:

Extended Warm-Up Protocol

  • Add 2-3 minutes to general prep (more cardio time)
  • Spend extra time on your problem areas
  • Add an extra warm-up set or two at lighter weights
  • Take the first work set slightly lighter if needed
  • Don't rush—a longer warm-up is better than a strained muscle

Key Takeaways

  • 10-15 minutes is enough for a complete warm-up
  • Progress from general to specific: Cardio → mobility → activation → bar work
  • Match your warm-up to your lift: Squat, bench, and deadlift each have different requirements
  • Reduce reps as weight increases: 10 with bar, 5-3 in middle, 1-2 near work weight
  • Don't fatigue yourself: Warm-ups should make you ready, not tired
  • Listen to your body: Some days need more warm-up—that's okay
