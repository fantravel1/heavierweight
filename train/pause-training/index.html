Pause Training for Powerlifting

Eliminate weak points by building strength at your sticking points.

11 min read Updated Dec 2024

Pause training eliminates the stretch-shortening cycle, forcing your muscles to generate force from a dead stop. This builds strength exactly where you need it most—at your sticking points.

Why Pause Training Works

Paused reps force pure muscle-generated force by eliminating elastic energy

The Benefits of Pause Training

  • Builds position strength: Develop power exactly at your weak point
  • Improves technique: Forces control and tightness at bottom position
  • Increases time under tension: More muscle-building stimulus
  • Prepares for competition: Bench press requires pause in competition
  • Reduces injury risk: Forces controlled, quality reps

Pause Squats

Key is maintaining maximal tension throughout the pause

Pause Squat Programming

  • Pause length: 2-3 seconds (longer = harder)
  • Weight: 10-15% less than regular squat (start conservative)
  • Sets/Reps: 3-5 sets of 2-4 reps
  • Frequency: 1-2x per week as primary or secondary squat variation
  • When to use: Weak out of the hole, need positional strength, technique work

Pause Bench Press

Bench press already requires a pause in competition, so pause training is essential. Even in training, paused reps build chest strength and eliminate cheating.

Practice longer pauses in training than you'll face in competition
Standard Pause Bench

2-3 second pause on chest. Bar completely motionless. Maintain leg drive and arch throughout.

Spoto Press

Pause 1-2 inches above chest. Builds strength in the weak zone without touching down.

Long Pause Bench

5+ second pause. Extreme position strength builder. Use lighter weights.

Dead Bench

Start from pins at chest height. No eccentric. Pure concentric strength.

Pause Deadlifts

Pause Positions

Unlike squat and bench, deadlifts don't have a natural pause point. You choose where to pause based on your weakness:

  • 1 inch off floor: Builds starting strength, teaches tight start
  • Below knee: Strengthens initial pull, common sticking point
  • At knee: Addresses mid-range weakness
  • Above knee: Less common, mainly for lockout (block pulls often better)
Pause at or just below your typical sticking point to strengthen that position

Programming Pause Work

Multiple ways to incorporate pause training depending on your goals

Sample Week with Pause Emphasis

4-Day Pause-Focused Block

  • Day 1: Pause Squat 4x4 @ 75%, RDL 3x8
  • Day 2: Pause Bench 5x3 @ 77%, Close-grip bench 3x6, Rows
  • Day 3: Pause Deadlift (below knee) 4x3 @ 72%, Belt squat 3x10
  • Day 4: Competition Bench 4x2 @ 82%, DB press, Triceps
Common Pause Training Mistakes
  • Relaxing during pause: Stay maximally tight—the pause is not rest
  • Bouncing out: Complete dead stop, then drive
  • Too heavy: Drop 10-15% from regular work sets initially
  • Inconsistent pause length: Count the same every rep
  • Losing position: If position breaks, the weight is too heavy

Progressing Pause Training

  1. Start conservative: 10-15% less than touch-and-go weights
  2. Master 2-second pauses: Before increasing pause length
  3. Add weight first: Before making pauses longer
  4. Then extend pauses: 3-second, then 4-second pauses
  5. Cycle back: Periodically return to shorter pauses, heavier weights
The Bottom Line

Pause training builds honest strength at your weakest positions. It eliminates cheating, improves technique, and prepares you for competition. Start with 2-3 second pauses at manageable weights, and progress slowly. The pause isn't rest—stay tight throughout.
