Accessory Programming for Powerlifting

Strategic accessory selection to build your big three.

15 min read Updated Dec 2024

Accessories build what the competition lifts can't—they address weak points, prevent imbalances, and add training volume without the fatigue cost of heavy compounds. Here's how to program them effectively.

The Accessory Hierarchy

Prioritize top of pyramid; accessories support but never replace main lifts
The 80/20 Rule

About 80% of your results come from the competition lifts and close variations. Accessories are the remaining 20%—important for long-term development, but never at the expense of the main work.

Volume & Intensity Guidelines

Primary Accessories (Close to Competition Lifts)

  • Sets per week: 6-12 sets per muscle group
  • Rep ranges: 5-8 reps typically
  • Intensity: RPE 7-8 (challenging but clean)
  • Examples: Front squat, close-grip bench, RDL, rows

Secondary Accessories (Isolation/Support)

  • Sets per week: 4-8 sets per muscle group
  • Rep ranges: 8-15 reps typically
  • Intensity: RPE 6-8 (controlled, feel the muscle)
  • Examples: Curls, tricep extensions, face pulls, leg curls
Accessory Overload

More accessories ≠ better results. If you're too fatigued for your main lifts, you have too many accessories. Cut back. Main lifts should always feel prioritized.

Programming Accessories Across a Block

Hypertrophy Block (4-6 weeks)

  • Higher accessory volume (8-12 sets per muscle)
  • Rep ranges 8-15
  • RPE 6-8
  • Focus: Building muscle, work capacity

Strength Block (4-6 weeks)

  • Moderate accessory volume (6-10 sets)
  • Rep ranges 5-8
  • RPE 7-8
  • Focus: Transferring size to strength

Peaking Block (2-4 weeks)

  • Reduced accessory volume (4-6 sets)
  • Maintain, don't build
  • Drop accessories that cause fatigue
  • Focus: Recovery, competition lifts
The Peaking Rule

During peaking, accessories should maintain muscle, not build it. Cut volume significantly but keep some work to prevent detraining. Focus on recovery and feeling fresh for competition lifts.

Accessory Selection Principles

  1. Address your weakest point first – Put the most important accessory right after your main lift when you're freshest
  2. Less is often more – 3-4 well-chosen accessories beat 7-8 random ones
  3. Specificity decreases down the pyramid – Close variations > compound accessories > isolation
  4. Progressive overload still applies – Track your accessory numbers; add weight or reps over time
  5. Don't chase fatigue – Accessories should challenge muscles, not destroy your recovery

Common Accessory Mistakes

Mistake 1: Too Many Accessories

If your accessory work takes 45+ minutes, you probably have too many. Main lifts + 3-4 accessories is plenty for most sessions.

Mistake 2: Wrong Intensity

Going to failure on every set of accessories accumulates fatigue without proportional benefit. RPE 7-8 is usually sufficient.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Weak Points

Doing accessories you like instead of accessories you need. If your lockout is weak, you need hip thrusts—not more quad work.

Mistake 4: No Progression

Using the same weight for months. Accessories should progress over time. Track them and aim to improve.

The Bottom Line

Accessories are tools to build weak points and support main lift progress. Choose them strategically based on YOUR weak points, not what's popular. Quality over quantity. And never let accessories compromise your competition lifts.
