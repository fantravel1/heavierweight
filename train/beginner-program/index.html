Beginner Strength Program

A simple, effective program for women new to strength training.

12 min read Updated Dec 2024

Starting a strength program can feel overwhelming. This is a proven, simple approach that builds fundamental movement patterns and sets you up for long-term progress.

Program Philosophy

This program is built on principles that work for beginners:

  • Compound movements: Exercises that train multiple muscles at once
  • Progressive overload: Gradually increasing weight over time
  • Appropriate frequency: 3 days per week allows recovery
  • Simplicity: Few exercises, done well, with focus on technique
Who Is This For?

True beginners with less than 6 months of consistent barbell training. If you're not sure whether a program is "too easy," it's probably right for you. Beginners can make progress on simple programs—complexity comes later.

The Program Structure

This is a 3-day per week program alternating between two workouts: Day A and Day B.

  • Week 1: A - B - A
  • Week 2: B - A - B
  • Week 3: A - B - A
  • (Continue alternating)

Take at least one rest day between training days. For example: Monday - Wednesday - Friday, or Tuesday - Thursday - Saturday.

Day A

Squat3 × 5
Bench Press3 × 5
Barbell Row3 × 5

Day B

Squat3 × 5
Overhead Press3 × 5
Deadlift1 × 5

Exercise Notes

Squat

Full depth (hip crease below top of knee), high bar or low bar position. This is the most important exercise—you'll do it every session. If you can't squat with a barbell yet, start with goblet squats.

Bench Press

Flat bench, full range of motion, controlled descent. Get a spotter for heavy sets or use safety pins.

Overhead Press

Standing, strict press (no leg drive). Start from the front rack position and press straight up. This is often the slowest-progressing lift—that's normal.

Deadlift

Conventional or sumo, whichever feels more natural. Only one set of 5 because deadlifts are taxing and squats are training similar muscles. Add more volume once you're intermediate.

Barbell Row

Bent over, pulling to your lower chest or stomach. Keep your back flat and don't use too much body english. Alternative: dumbbell rows if barbell rows don't feel right.

Starting Weights

Start lighter than you think you need to:

  • Squat: Empty bar (45 lb) or lighter if needed
  • Bench: Empty bar or lighter
  • Overhead Press: Empty bar or lighter (this is hard for most beginners)
  • Deadlift: 65-95 lb typically
  • Row: 45-65 lb typically

The first few weeks should feel easy. This gives you time to learn technique while still making progress.

Progression

Add weight every session when possible:

ExerciseWeight Increase
Squat5 lb per session
Deadlift10 lb per session
Bench Press2.5-5 lb per session
Overhead Press2.5 lb per session
Row5 lb per session

You'll need fractional plates (1.25 lb or smaller) for upper body lifts. Most gyms don't have them—consider buying your own.

When Progression Stalls

If you fail to complete all sets and reps at a weight:

  1. Repeat the same weight next session
  2. If you fail again, repeat one more time
  3. If you fail a third time, reduce weight by 10% and work back up

This is called a "deload and work back up." It's normal and part of the process.

Warm-Up

Before each workout:

  • 5-10 minutes light cardio (walking, bike, rowing)
  • Dynamic stretching for hips and shoulders
  • Warm-up sets for each exercise

Example Warm-Up for Squat

If your working weight is 95 lb:

  • Empty bar × 10
  • 65 lb × 5
  • 85 lb × 3
  • 95 lb × 5, 5, 5 (working sets)

Rest Between Sets

  • Early weeks: 2-3 minutes between sets
  • As weights get heavier: 3-5 minutes between sets

There's no rush. Rest long enough that you can complete your next set with good form.

Optional Accessories

After 4-6 weeks, you can add accessories at the end of workouts:

  • Core: Planks, ab wheel rollouts, dead bugs
  • Arms: Bicep curls, tricep pushdowns
  • Back: Face pulls, lat pulldowns

Keep accessories simple: 2-3 sets of 8-15 reps. They're supplementary—the main lifts are what matters most.

How Long to Run This Program

Most beginners can run this program for 3-6 months before progress slows significantly. Signs you're ready for an intermediate program:

  • You've deloaded multiple times on the same lift
  • Progress is measured in weeks, not sessions
  • You're feeling beat up from the frequency

Don't rush to an intermediate program. The linear progress phase is precious—milk it for as long as possible.

Sample Progress (First 8 Weeks)

Squat: 45 lb → 105 lb
Bench: 45 lb → 65 lb
Deadlift: 95 lb → 175 lb
OHP: 35 lb → 50 lb
Row: 45 lb → 85 lb

Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Starting too heavy: Leads to early stalls and bad technique
  • Adding too much volume: The program is intentionally minimal
  • Skipping sessions: Consistency matters more than perfection
  • Not eating enough: You need fuel to build muscle and get stronger
  • Program hopping: Stick with this for at least 3 months
The Bottom Line

Simple works for beginners. Show up three times a week, add weight when you can, learn the movements, and eat enough. The complexity can come later—right now, build your foundation.
