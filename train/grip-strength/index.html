Grip Strength Training

Build an unbreakable grip that never limits your deadlift or row strength.

Your grip is often the weakest link in heavy pulling movements. A strong grip doesn't just prevent the bar from slipping—it creates a more stable base for your entire upper body and can add pounds to your deadlift.

Types of Grip Strength

Grip strength isn't one thing—it's several distinct capacities. Training all of them builds complete grip strength.

For deadlifts, SUPPORT grip is most important—but all types contribute to overall grip strength

Deadlift Grip Options

Hook grip is worth learning—it's secure and symmetric, protecting your biceps

Grip Training Exercises

For Support Grip (Deadlift-Specific)

Dead Hangs

Hang from pull-up bar for max time. Add weight with belt when bodyweight becomes easy.

3 sets to near-failure, 2-3x/week

Farmer Walks

Heavy dumbbells or farmer handles. Walk until grip fails. Great for conditioning too.

3-4 sets of 30-60 seconds

Barbell Holds

Deadlift to lockout, hold for time. Use your deadlift grip. Start at 80% 1RM.

3 sets of 10-30 seconds

Fat Gripz Work

Add fat grips to rows, curls, or holds. Thicker bar = harder grip challenge.

Use on 1-2 accessories per session

For Crushing Grip

Hand Grippers

Captain of Crush or similar. Progress through ratings. Full close with control.

5-10 closes per hand, 3x/week

Towel Hangs

Loop towel over bar, hang from towel ends. Forces crushing and support together.

3 sets to failure

For Pinch Grip

Plate Pinches

Pinch two plates (smooth sides out) and hold. Start with 2x10lb plates.

3 sets of 15-30 seconds

Hex Dumbbell Holds

Hold hex dumbbell by the head (not handle) for time. Great pinch builder.

3 sets per hand, 15-20 seconds
Add grip work at the end of sessions—never train grip before pulling movements
Source: Juggernaut Training Systems - Building grip for heavy pulls

When to Use Straps

Straps are tools, not crutches. Use them for high-rep accessory work (rows, RDLs) to save grip for heavy pulls. Train grip separately. For competition deadlifts, always train without straps at least once per week to build competition-specific grip.

Key Takeaways

  • Support grip (isometric holds) is most important for deadlifts
  • Hook grip is worth learning—it's secure and protects your biceps
  • Train grip 2-3x per week at the END of sessions
  • Progressive overload applies to grip too—add time or weight
  • Use straps strategically for accessories, not as a replacement for grip training
  • Include extensor work (finger extensions, rubber bands) to prevent imbalances
