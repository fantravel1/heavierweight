Unilateral Training for Powerlifters

12 min read • Accessory Programming

Why Single-Limb Work Matters

Every lifter has asymmetries. Your dominant side is stronger, more coordinated, and often takes over during bilateral movements. In the squat, one leg may drive harder. In the bench, one arm may lock out first. These imbalances aren't just aesthetic concerns—they can limit your total and increase injury risk over time.

Unilateral training addresses these imbalances by forcing each limb to work independently. There's nowhere for your weaker side to hide when it's doing the work alone.

In bilateral movements, your stronger side can compensate. Unilateral work eliminates this option.

Identifying Your Imbalances

Before programming unilateral work, you need to know where your asymmetries lie. Most lifters have some intuition about their weak side, but objective assessment reveals the full picture.

Self-Assessment Methods

Single-Leg Tests

  • Bulgarian Split Squat Test: Compare max reps at a given weight, each leg. More than 10-15% difference indicates significant imbalance
  • Single-Leg RDL: Note which side has worse balance and coordination, not just strength
  • Step-Up Test: Use a moderate box height and compare control and ease

Single-Arm Tests

  • Single-Arm Dumbbell Press: Compare weight and stability each side
  • Single-Arm Row: Note differences in range of motion and control
  • Farmer's Carry: Walk with weight in one hand—compare torso lean and control

The Best Unilateral Exercises for Powerlifters

Not all single-limb exercises are created equal. The best choices for powerlifters have direct carryover to the competition lifts while building strength, stability, and muscle.

Lower Body Exercises

Bulgarian Split Squat

Best for: Quad strength, hip flexor mobility, balance

  • Rear foot elevated on bench
  • Keep torso upright
  • Drive through front heel
  • High carryover to squat

Walking Lunges

Best for: Dynamic stability, work capacity, quad/glute development

  • Controlled steps forward
  • Knee tracks over toes
  • Good for higher rep ranges
  • Builds conditioning too

Single-Leg RDL

Best for: Hamstring strength, balance, hip hinge pattern

  • Hinge at the hip
  • Back leg extends behind
  • Great for deadlift setup stability
  • Start light, progress slowly

Step-Ups

Best for: Quad strength without back fatigue, starting strength

  • Box height at or above knee
  • No push-off from back leg
  • Control the descent
  • Mimics squat out of the hole

Upper Body Exercises

Single-Arm Dumbbell Press

Best for: Pressing strength, core anti-rotation, shoulder stability

  • Keep hips and shoulders square
  • Core works hard to prevent rotation
  • Great for bench lockout work
  • Can do flat, incline, or overhead

Single-Arm Dumbbell Row

Best for: Back strength, lat development, deadlift support

  • Row to hip, not to chest
  • Full range of motion
  • Control the negative
  • Builds the back for heavy pulls

Single-Arm Farmer's Carry

Best for: Core stability, grip strength, oblique work

  • Stay tall, don't lean
  • Walk controlled steps
  • Great for trunk stability
  • Carryover to bracing under load

Single-Arm Tricep Work

Best for: Lockout strength, elbow health

  • Overhead extensions or pushdowns
  • Identify weak tricep
  • Address bench lockout issues
  • Lower injury risk than barbell

Programming Unilateral Work

The key to effective unilateral programming is integration—not replacement. These exercises supplement your main lifts, they don't replace them.

Unilateral work fits in the third tier—important but never replacing main lifts

Weak Side First Protocol

Always start with your weaker side. This ensures it gets trained when you're freshest and sets the benchmark for your stronger side.

The Match Method

  • Start with your weaker side
  • Perform the set to completion (leave 1-2 RIR)
  • Match the reps exactly with your stronger side—don't do more
  • Over time, the weaker side catches up

Sets and Reps for Powerlifters

Phase Exercise Type Sets x Reps Notes
Hypertrophy Lower body unilateral 3-4 x 8-12 Build muscle on weaker side
Hypertrophy Upper body unilateral 3-4 x 10-15 Higher reps for smaller muscles
Strength Lower body unilateral 3-4 x 5-8 Heavier loads, quality reps
Strength Upper body unilateral 3-4 x 6-10 Moderate loads, stability focus
Peaking All unilateral 2-3 x 6-8 Maintenance only, reduce volume

Don't Go to Failure

Unilateral exercises require more balance and coordination. Going to complete failure increases injury risk. Keep 1-2 reps in reserve, especially on exercises like Bulgarian split squats where balance is a factor.

Sample Weekly Integration

Here's how unilateral work might fit into a typical 4-day powerlifting split:

Day 1: Squat Focus

  • Competition Squat
  • Bulgarian Split Squat: 3x8 each leg
  • Single-Leg RDL: 3x10 each leg (light)

Day 2: Bench Focus

  • Competition Bench
  • Single-Arm Dumbbell Press: 3x10 each arm
  • Single-Arm Row: 3x12 each arm

Day 3: Deadlift Focus

  • Competition Deadlift
  • Step-Ups: 3x8 each leg
  • Single-Arm Farmer's Carry: 3x30m each arm

Day 4: Volume/Accessory

  • Lighter main lift variations
  • Walking Lunges: 3x12 each leg
  • Single-Arm Cable Work: 3x15 each side

Tracking Progress

Monitor your imbalances over time. Reassess every 8-12 weeks using the same tests, and adjust programming based on what you find.

Expect gradual improvement over 8-16 weeks with consistent unilateral training

Key Takeaways

  • Assess first: Know where your imbalances are before programming
  • Start weak side: Always train your weaker side first when fresh
  • Match, don't exceed: Your stronger side only matches what your weaker side did
  • Integrate, don't replace: Unilateral work supplements main lifts
  • Be patient: Expect 8-16 weeks to see significant balance improvements
  • Reassess regularly: Test every 8-12 weeks and adjust accordingly
