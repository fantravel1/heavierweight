Wrist Wraps: Complete Guide

Support your pressing with the right wrist wraps.

8 min read Updated Dec 2024

Wrist wraps are one of the most common accessories in the weight room. They provide support and stability for pressing movements—but not all wraps are created equal, and how you use them matters.

What Wrist Wraps Do

Wrist wraps work by providing external support to the wrist joint, keeping it in a more neutral position under load. They:

  • Limit extension: Prevent excessive wrist bend under heavy pressing
  • Add stability: Create a more solid platform to push from
  • Reduce discomfort: Take stress off the joint during high-volume or heavy work
  • Provide confidence: Knowing your wrists are supported can help you commit to the lift
When to Use Them

Wrist wraps are most useful for bench press, overhead press, and front squats. They're allowed in competition (IPF allows up to 1 meter length). Use them for heavy work; train without them for lighter sets to build wrist strength.

Types of Wrist Wraps

By Stiffness

Flexible/Light

Thin, stretchy material. Provides light support while maintaining full mobility. Good for Olympic lifting, accessory work, or lifters who prefer less restriction. Easy to wrap but offers less support for heavy pressing.

Medium Stiffness

The "all-around" option. Offers meaningful support without feeling like a cast. Most lifters start here. Good for general training and recreational competitors.

Stiff/Heavy

Thick, minimal stretch. Maximum support for heavy pressing. Requires more effort to wrap correctly. Preferred by competitive powerlifters for bench press. May feel uncomfortable for lighter work.

By Length

Length Best For Notes

How to Wrap

Proper wrapping technique is as important as the wraps themselves:

Basic Technique

  1. Position the thumb loop: Most lifters put thumb through the loop to anchor, then remove thumb for the lift
  2. Start below the wrist: Begin wrapping on the lower forearm
  3. Overlap onto the wrist: Cover the wrist joint with each wrap
  4. Cover the palm slightly: Extend just onto the base of the palm for maximum support
  5. Secure with velcro: Make sure it's tight but not cutting off circulation

Tension Tips

  • Tighter isn't always better: You need blood flow and feeling in your hands
  • Wrap tighter for heavier lifts: Adjust tension based on the weight
  • Keep wrist neutral: Wrap in the position you want to maintain
  • Remove between sets if needed: Loosen or remove to restore circulation
Competition Rules

IPF/USAPL allows wrist wraps up to 1 meter (100cm) in length and 8cm in width. The wrap cannot extend more than 10cm above or 2cm below the center of the wrist. Check your federation's rules before competition.

Common Mistakes

  • Wrapping too low: The wrap should cover the wrist joint, not just sit on the forearm
  • Too loose: Provides no meaningful support if it's not snug
  • Too tight for too long: Loosen between sets to maintain circulation
  • Using them for everything: Train without wraps on lighter sets to build natural wrist strength
  • Wrong stiffness: Super stiff wraps for Olympic lifting or super flexible for max bench doesn't make sense

Wrist Wraps vs. Wrist Straps

These are completely different pieces of equipment:

Wrist Wraps Wrist Straps
Support the wrist joint Help grip the bar
Used for pressing Used for pulling
Allowed in competition Not allowed in competition
Wrap around wrist Wrap around bar and wrist

When Not to Use Wrist Wraps

  • Light warmup sets: Build wrist strength with unassisted lifting
  • Pulling movements: Wraps don't help deadlifts or rows (straps might)
  • If they mask pain: Wraps shouldn't be a band-aid for wrist problems—address the underlying issue

Caring for Your Wraps

  • Let them air dry after training
  • Hand wash occasionally with mild soap
  • Don't leave them balled up in a hot gym bag
  • Replace when velcro wears out or elastic is shot
The Bottom Line

Wrist wraps are a simple, effective tool for pressing. Choose stiffness based on your needs—stiffer for heavy powerlifting, more flexible for Olympic lifting or general training. Learn to wrap properly, use them for heavy work, and train without them enough to maintain natural wrist strength.
