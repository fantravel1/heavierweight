Squat Troubleshooting Guide

Identify and fix the most common squat problems.

14 min read Updated Dec 2024

Something's wrong with your squat and you're not sure what. This guide covers the most common squat problems, their causes, and practical fixes you can apply immediately.

How to Use This Guide

Video yourself squatting from the side and front. Watch it and identify your problem below. Try the fixes systematically—usually one or two will address your issue.

Butt Wink (Posterior Pelvic Tilt at Bottom)

Your lower back rounds/tucks under at the bottom of the squat

Common Causes:

  • Going deeper than your hip mobility allows
  • Insufficient ankle dorsiflexion
  • Tight hip flexors
  • Stance too narrow for your hip structure
  • Sitting "between" your legs instead of "into" them

Fixes:

  • Widen your stance and turn toes out more
  • Use squat shoes with elevated heels
  • Only go as deep as you can without tucking
  • Hip flexor stretches and 90/90 mobility
  • Cue "spread the floor" to externally rotate
  • Goblet squat holds at depth to build comfort

Knee Cave (Valgus Collapse)

Knees collapse inward during the ascent

Common Causes:

  • Weak hip abductors (glute medius)
  • Poor motor pattern/awareness
  • Weight too heavy
  • Fatigue
  • Stance too wide for your strength

Fixes:

  • Cue "knees out" or "spread the floor"
  • Band around knees during warm-ups for feedback
  • Hip abduction exercises (banded walks, clamshells)
  • Pause squats to build control at weak point
  • Reduce weight until pattern improves
  • Narrow stance slightly if needed

Excessive Forward Lean

Torso tips forward too much, turning squat into a good morning

Common Causes:

  • Weak quads relative to posterior chain
  • Poor ankle mobility
  • Bar position too low for your build
  • Looking down
  • Losing upper back tightness

Fixes:

  • Front squats and high bar to build upright strength
  • Squat shoes with heel elevation
  • Try a higher bar position
  • Pick a spot to look at (eye level or slightly up)
  • Cue "chest up" and "elbows under bar"
  • Tempo squats with slow descent
  • Leg press and quad-dominant accessory work

Not Hitting Depth

Hip crease doesn't get below top of knee

Common Causes:

  • Hip mobility limitations
  • Ankle mobility limitations
  • Fear of losing balance/control
  • Weight too heavy
  • Not understanding where depth actually is

Fixes:

  • Squat to a box at proper depth
  • Video yourself from the side with horizontal line at hip crease
  • Hip mobility work (90/90, pigeon, frog stretch)
  • Ankle mobility work (wall stretches, weighted stretches)
  • Squat shoes with heel elevation
  • Goblet squat holds at depth
  • Widen stance and/or turn toes out more

Heels Rising

Heels come off the floor during descent or ascent

Common Causes:

  • Limited ankle dorsiflexion
  • Weight shifting forward
  • Stance too narrow
  • Bar position too high

Fixes:

  • Squat shoes are the quickest fix
  • Ankle mobility work daily
  • Cue "whole foot" or "tripod foot"
  • Widen stance slightly
  • Box squats to learn proper weight distribution
  • Try a lower bar position

Leaning to One Side

Shifting weight to one leg, uneven descent or ascent

Common Causes:

  • Strength imbalance between legs
  • Hip mobility difference
  • Previous injury compensation
  • Poor proprioception

Fixes:

  • Unilateral work: Bulgarian split squats, lunges, step-ups
  • Tempo squats with video feedback
  • Pause squats to feel balance
  • Address mobility differences with targeted stretching
  • Light squats with focus on symmetry
  • Consider assessment by a physical therapist

Upper Back Rounding

Upper back collapses, shoulders come forward

Common Causes:

  • Weak upper back muscles
  • Poor setup and bracing
  • Weight too heavy
  • Fatigue
  • Bar position not secure

Fixes:

  • Rows, pull-ups, face pulls for upper back strength
  • Better setup: squeeze shoulder blades, pull bar into back
  • Cue "proud chest" or "show your logo"
  • Front squats to build upper back strength
  • Reduce weight if form breaks down
  • Video to catch it early before it becomes a habit

Squat Morning (Hips Rise Faster Than Shoulders)

Out of the hole, hips shoot up but bar/chest doesn't

Common Causes:

  • Weak quads
  • Over-reliance on hip extension
  • Poor bracing out of the hole
  • Weight too heavy

Fixes:

  • Quad-dominant accessory work (leg press, front squat, leg extensions)
  • Tempo squats with slow, controlled ascent
  • Pause squats at parallel
  • Cue "drive your back into the bar"
  • High bar or front squats as main movement for a training block
  • Reduce weight and work on keeping chest up

Can't Control the Descent

Dropping too fast, losing tightness, "dive-bombing"

Common Causes:

  • Lack of eccentric control
  • Poor bracing
  • Trying to use stretch reflex too aggressively
  • Weak muscles throughout range of motion

Fixes:

  • Tempo squats: 3-4 second descent
  • Pause squats at various depths
  • Better bracing before descent
  • Think "control" not "drop and bounce"
  • Reduce weight if needed to maintain control

Bar Slides Down Back (Low Bar)

Bar creeps down during the set

Common Causes:

  • Shoulders not retracted enough
  • Upper back rounding
  • Shirt too slippery
  • Bar not locked in position properly

Fixes:

  • Create a better shelf: squeeze shoulder blades together
  • Wear a shirt with texture (cotton, not slick polyester)
  • Apply chalk to your back/shirt
  • Work on upper back tightness
  • Try a slightly higher bar position

General Troubleshooting Approach

  1. Video yourself: You can't fix what you can't see
  2. Identify the primary issue: Usually one thing causes several symptoms
  3. Reduce weight: Fix technique before chasing numbers
  4. Address mobility if needed: Some issues are physical limitations
  5. Build specific strength: Add accessories for weak areas
  6. Practice with feedback: Cues, tempo work, pauses
  7. Be patient: Movement patterns take time to change
When to Get Help

If you're experiencing pain (not just discomfort from hard work), if an issue persists despite addressing it, or if you're stuck, consider working with a coach or physical therapist. An outside eye often catches things you can't see yourself.

The Bottom Line

Most squat problems have solutions. Video yourself, identify the issue, apply the appropriate fixes, and give yourself time to adapt. Perfect technique is an ongoing process—even elite lifters constantly refine their movement.
