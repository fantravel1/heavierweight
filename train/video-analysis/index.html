Video Analysis Guide

Learn to film, review, and improve your lifts using video as your most honest coach.

Video doesn't lie. What feels right often looks wrong, and what feels wrong sometimes looks perfect. Regular video review is the fastest way to identify technique issues and track improvement over time.

Optimal Camera Angles

Different angles reveal different information. For complete analysis, you need at least two angles per lift.

Set phone at hip height, perpendicular to your movement. Side view is most important.

Recommended Angles by Lift

Side view is primary for all three lifts—start there if you can only film one angle

What to Look For

Squat Analysis Checklist

Side View

  • Bar stays over midfoot throughout
  • Hip crease below knee (depth)
  • Chest and hips rise together
  • No excessive forward lean
  • Knees track forward, not excessive

Back View

  • Knees track over toes
  • No knee cave (valgus)
  • Bar stays level, no tilt
  • Hips don't shift left/right
  • Equal drive from both legs

Bench Analysis Checklist

Side View

  • Bar path shows J-curve back
  • Touch point at lower chest
  • Lockout over shoulders
  • Arch maintained throughout
  • Feet stay planted

Head-On View

  • Elbows at ~45-75° at bottom
  • Bar touches evenly
  • Both sides press equally
  • Wrists stacked over elbows
  • No excessive elbow flare

Deadlift Analysis Checklist

Side View

  • Bar path straight vertical
  • Bar stays close to body
  • Hips and shoulders rise together
  • No excessive back rounding
  • Lockout with hips, not hyperextend

Front View (Sumo)

  • Knees track over toes
  • No knee cave at break
  • Torso stays upright
  • Shoulders stay over bar
  • Even pressure both legs

Analysis Tools and Apps

Even basic phone slow-mo is valuable—fancy apps are optional

Building a Video Library

Save videos of your best sets at various weights. When something feels off, compare current video to your best technique. This is more useful than comparing to elite lifters with different proportions. Your best you is the benchmark.

Key Takeaways

  • Side view is the most important angle for all three lifts
  • Film at hip height, perpendicular to your movement direction
  • Use slow-motion to catch details invisible at full speed
  • Compare yourself to your best lifts, not just to others
  • Look for the same issues repeatedly—patterns reveal weaknesses
  • Save good reps to build a reference library of your best technique
